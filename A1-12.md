A2-11 contains one useful diagnosis, but its claimed WKB closure is not a proof. # 1. Evaluation of A2-11, A3-11, A4-11

## 1.1 A2-11

**Verdict: not a valid completion. Useful diagnosis, invalid closure.**

A2-11 correctly identifies why the constant-frequency Bessel/Sturm majorant is structurally too crude in the Laguerre transition strip. In the Laguerre limit, replacing the true potential by a fixed lower-frequency Bessel model forces a drop
\[
\widetilde\lambda \le \lambda-\frac{u_1}{4},
\]
and hence inflates the boundary amplitude by roughly
\[
\left(\frac{\lambda}{\widetilde\lambda}\right)^{\alpha/2}.
\]
A2-11 correctly observes that when \(\alpha\sim n\), this can become exponentially harmful and can defeat the \(O(\alpha^{-1/3})\) slack from Landau-type Bessel bounds.

That is the valuable part. It justifies abandoning constant-frequency Sturm comparison as the final tool.

The fatal gaps are these.

First, A2-11 repeats the algebraic error that \(\Phi_R\) is "exactly a parabola in \(\beta\)." It is not. The right-overflow polynomial is linear in \(\beta\):
\[
\Phi_R
=
\beta(-\alpha^2+2n\alpha+3n^2+2n)
+
(-\alpha^3+3n^2\alpha+2n\alpha+2n^3+2n^2).
\]
A4-11 explicitly flags this same error, saying the \(\beta^2\) coefficient does not exist.

Second, the "compact geometric box" claim is still false in the needed global sense. Right overflow gives only
\[
\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}\sim3n,
\]
so the remaining strip is not compact uniformly in \(n\). Also, \(\beta\to\infty\) remains an actual boundary problem; it is exactly the Laguerre limit. Previous audits already gave the example \(n=2,\alpha=1,\beta\to\infty\), where overflow persists and the energy criterion fails.

Third, the proposed WKB invariant does not prove the required inequality. A2-11 transforms to
\[
v_{xx}
+
\left(
E-\frac{x^2}{4}-\frac{\alpha^2-1/4}{x^2}
\right)v=0,
\qquad
E=2n+\alpha+1,
\]
but then defines
\[
Q(x)=E-\frac{x^2}{4},
\]
dropping the centrifugal term
\[
-\frac{\alpha^2-1/4}{x^2}.
\]
That dropped term is not harmless in the transition strip, especially when \(\alpha\sim n\). The claimed invariant
\[
E_{\rm WKB}(x)=\sqrt{Q(x)}\,v^2+\frac{v_x^2}{\sqrt{Q(x)}}
\]
is therefore not an invariant for the actual equation.

Fourth, even for the simplified equation, A2-11 only obtains scaling:
\[
v_{\max}\propto E^{-1/4}.
\]
Scaling is not a sharp inequality. To prove KKT, one needs an explicit upper bound with constants:
\[
|v_{\max}|
\le
\left(\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4},
\]
not an asymptotic proportionality. A2-11 gives no error term, no boundary normalization, no treatment of turning points, and no finite verification for small \(n\). Yet it declares "100% complete."

**Adopt from A2-11:** the constant-frequency failure diagnosis and the idea that a variable-frequency Liouville-Green/Prufer method is the right class of tool.

**Reject from A2-11:** the claimed WKB proof, the compactness claim, and the assertion that no gaps remain.

## 1.2 A3-11

**Verdict: the most reliable 11th-round response. It does not complete the proof, but its audit is sound.**

A3-11 correctly states the known reduction. In the right-overflow regime with \(\beta\to\infty\),
\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
\to
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\]
where
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\sqrt{\frac{\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}}\,
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\]
and
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}^4\to\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}.
\]
Thus the limiting endpoint obstruction is
\[
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4}.
\tag{LAG}
\]
A3-11 explicitly identifies this as the sharp Laguerre inequality.

It also correctly records the two Laguerre regions that are already proved. For
\[
0\le\alpha\le\frac12,
\]
one obtains \((\mathrm{LAG})\) by the small-endpoint theorem. For large \(\alpha\), A3-11 uses the Laguerre identities
\[
\int_0^\infty \ell^2\,du=1,\qquad
\int_0^\infty\frac{\ell^2}{u}\,du=\frac1\alpha,\qquad
\int_0^\infty u(\ell')^2\,du=\frac{2n+1}{4},
\]
and Cauchy-Schwarz to get
\[
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|^4
\le
\frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}.
\]
This proves \((\mathrm{LAG})\) when
\[
\alpha\ge
\alpha_E:=
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]
So the remaining strip is exactly
\[
\boxed{
\frac12<\alpha<\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
}
\tag{S}
\]

A3-11's main overstatement is that the "whole KKT conjecture is reduced to proving (L)." Strictly, \((\mathrm{LAG})\) is a necessary limiting boundary inequality, and it is the dominant obstruction. But to complete the Jacobi theorem for every finite \(\beta\), one still needs either a direct finite-\(\beta\) endpoint proof or a uniform Jacobi-to-Laguerre lift with explicit slack. A3-11 acknowledges elsewhere that finite-\(\beta\) approximation and numerical verification require additional uniformity, so this is not a fatal flaw in its audit.

**Adopt from A3-11:** the exact Laguerre strip, the energy proof, and the honest status.

**Reject or refine:** do not say "Laguerre alone automatically proves all finite \(\beta\)" unless the finite-\(\beta\) bridge is supplied with constants.

## 1.3 A4-11

**Verdict: best next-move proposal, not a completed proof.**

A4-11 correctly rejects the finite-\(n\) fallacy. It notes that interval arithmetic for fixed \(n\) is legitimate, but the remaining strip persists for infinitely many \(n\):
\[
\frac12<\alpha<
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3},
\]
whose width grows linearly with \(n\). Therefore one cannot discharge the proof by finite computation unless a uniform large-\(n\) analytic estimate first reduces the problem to finitely many \(n\).

A4-11 also correctly states what an actual WKB/Hilb proof would need. It writes the Laguerre approximation schematically as
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left(\frac{u}{\zeta(u)}\right)^{1/4}
\frac{J_\alpha(2\sqrt{N\zeta(u)})}{\sqrt{N}}
+
R_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\]
and then explicitly identifies the missing step:
\[
|R_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
C N^{-5/6}\alpha^{-1/3}
\]
with an actual constant \(C\).

That is exactly the right standard: a WKB proof requires a principal term plus a uniform remainder small enough to fit under the KKT target.

But A4-11 does not execute this. It says the proof is "substantially complete, pending explicit error-constant derivation," then admits one must compute \(C\), determine \(N_0\), and verify all \(n<N_0\) rigorously. That is not a completed proof.

There are two additional weaknesses in A4-11's proposed asymptotic closure.

First, it says that in the transition strip \(\alpha=O(n)\). The strip also includes fixed \(\alpha>1/2\) as \(n\to\infty\). In that hard-edge substrip, Landau's bound gives only \(O(1)\), not \(O(n^{-1/3})\). This may still be enough, but it requires a different hard-edge Bessel constant argument, not the large-\(\alpha\) scaling stated.

Second, the finite-\(\beta\) bridge
\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
=
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)+O(\beta^{-1})
\]
does not by itself prove the finite-\(\beta\) endpoint estimate. One needs a strict margin
\[
T-\sup|\ell|>\delta
\]
or a uniform one-sided comparison. Near boundary regimes, such a margin is not established in A4-11.

**Adopt from A4-11:** the insistence on explicit WKB remainder constants, a large-\(n\)/finite-\(n\) split, and a separate finite-\(\beta\) bridge.

**Reject from A4-11:** the phrase "substantially complete" unless the constants and verification are actually supplied.

Second, I sharpen the remaining target. In the Laguerre strip, it is enough to prove the stronger universal estimate
\[
\boxed{
\sup_{u\ge0}|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|^4\le\frac12,
\qquad n\ge1,\quad \alpha\ge\frac12.
}
\tag{U}
\]
Indeed, on the unresolved strip
\[
\frac12<\alpha<\alpha_E
=

one has
\[
\left(\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}\right)
>

|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|^4

<

which is exactly \((\mathrm{LAG})\) in the strip.

This is now the cleanest final target.

# 3. My proof with details

I can give a rigorous proof of the KKT estimate on the validated regions and a precise reduction of the remaining region. I cannot honestly fill the last Laguerre inequality from A2-11's WKB argument, because the required constants and comparison estimates are missing.

## 3.1 Setup

Let
\[
s=\alpha+\beta,\qquad d=\beta-\alpha,
\]
and define
\[
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}^2
=
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+s+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}.
\]
Set
\[
g(x)
=
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x).
\]
The target is
\[
|g(x)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta},
\]
where
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

The uploaded review states the same refined KKT bound for integer parameters and explains that the group-theoretic derivation forces \(\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb N_0\), while the conjectural real-parameter extension asks for all \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\).

## 3.2 Jacobi self-adjoint equation

Let
\[
A(x)=1-x^2,\qquad
\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac{s}{2}.
\]
Then \(g\) satisfies
\[
(Ag')'+\frac{F(x)}{A(x)}g=0,
\]
where
\[
F(x)
=
\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2.
\]

On \(F>0\), define
\[
S(x)=g(x)^2+\frac{A(x)^2g'(x)^2}{F(x)}.
\]
A direct computation gives
\[
S'(x)
=
-\frac{A(x)^2F'(x)}{F(x)^2}g'(x)^2.
\]
Since \(F\) is a concave quadratic, \(F'\) changes sign once. Hence Sonin monotonicity pushes the largest local extrema toward the two outer endpoint lobes. This is a validated module of the prior proof architecture.

The roots of \(F\) are
\[
x_\pm
=
\frac{ds\pm4\sqrt{\kappa(\kappa+\alpha\beta)}}{s^2+4\kappa}.
\]

Thus the global problem reduces to the central region plus possible right and left overflow lobes.

## 3.3 Central contour region

Let
\[
\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n}.
\]
For the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull saddle contour, the radius is
\[
r=
\sqrt{\frac{1-x^2}{1+s/n}}.
\]
For real noninteger parameters, the contour must avoid the branch points \(\pm1\), so one needs
\[
r<1-x,\qquad r<1+x.
\]
These are equivalent to
\[
|x|<\sigma.
\]
Therefore the branch-safe contour theorem gives
\[
|g(x)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\qquad (|x|<\sigma).
\]
So if
\[
[x_-,x_+]\subseteq[-\sigma,\sigma],
\]
then the KKT bound follows immediately by Sonin localization plus the central contour theorem. This central lemma is already identified as a genuine validated region.

## 3.4 Weighted-energy region

For \(\alpha,\beta>0\), the weighted identities are
\[
\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{g(x)^2}{1-x}\,dx=\frac1\alpha,
\qquad
\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{g(x)^2}{1+x}\,dx=\frac1\beta,
\]
and
\[
\int_{-1}^{1}g(x)^2\,dx
=
\frac{2}{2n+s+1}.
\]
Integration by parts gives
\[
E:=
\int_{-1}^{1}(1-x^2)g'(x)^2\,dx
=
n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)}.
\]
Also,
\[
J:=
\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{g(x)^2}{1-x^2}\,dx
=
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta}.
\]
The two-sided Cauchy-Schwarz argument yields
\[
|g(x)|^4\le JE.
\]
Hence the KKT estimate follows whenever
\[
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta}
\left(
n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)}
\right)
\le
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}.
\]
In particular, this includes the large balanced region such as \(\alpha,\beta\ge2n\).

## 3.5 Small endpoint exponents

On the right endpoint, put
\[
x=\cos\theta,
\qquad
v(\theta)=\sqrt{\sin\theta}\,g(\cos\theta).
\]
Then
\[
v''+Q(\theta)v=0,
\]
where
\[
Q(\theta)
=
N^2
+
\frac{1/4-\alpha^2}{4\sin^2(\theta/2)}
+
\frac{1/4-\beta^2}{4\cos^2(\theta/2)},
\qquad
N=n+\frac{s+1}{2}.
\]
The validated endpoint Bessel comparison proves the right endpoint lobe for
\[
0\le\alpha\le\frac12.
\]
By symmetry, the left endpoint lobe is controlled for
\[
0\le\beta\le\frac12.
\]

## 3.6 Exact overflow confinement

Right overflow means
\[
x_+>\sigma.
\]
Since
\[
F(1)=-\alpha^2\le0,
\]
right overflow implies
\[
F(\sigma)>0.
\]
A direct calculation gives
\[
F(\sigma)=
\frac{\Phi_R(n,\alpha,\beta)}{s+2n},
\]
where
\[
\Phi_R
=
\beta(-\alpha^2+2n\alpha+3n^2+2n)
+
(-\alpha^3+3n^2\alpha+2n\alpha+2n^3+2n^2).
\]
Therefore
\[
x_+>\sigma
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\Phi_R>0.
\]
If
\[
\alpha\ge n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n},
\]
then the coefficient of \(\beta\) is nonpositive, and the remaining constant term is also nonpositive. Hence
\[
x_+>\sigma
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
\]
Similarly,
\[
x_-<-\sigma
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\beta<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
\]

This is a confinement theorem, not a closure theorem. It does not remove the unbounded \(n\)-family, and it does not remove \(\beta\to\infty\).

## 3.7 Laguerre endpoint limit

In the right-overflow large-asymmetry limit,
\[
\beta\to\infty,
\qquad
x=1-\frac{2u}{\beta},
\]
one obtains
\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
\to
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\]
where
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]
The target tends to
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}^4
\to
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}.
\]
Therefore every full proof must imply
\[
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/4}.
\tag{LAG}
\]

A3-11 correctly identifies this as the sharp limiting obstruction.

## 3.8 Proven part of the Laguerre inequality

Let
\[
f(u)=\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]
The identities are
\[
\int_0^\infty f(u)^2\,du=1,
\]
\[
\int_0^\infty \frac{f(u)^2}{u}\,du=\frac1\alpha,
\]
and
\[
\int_0^\infty u f'(u)^2\,du=\frac{2n+1}{4}.
\]
Cauchy-Schwarz gives
\[
|f(u)|^4
\le
\frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}.
\]
Thus \((\mathrm{LAG})\) follows whenever
\[
\frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}
\le
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}.
\]
Solving,
\[
\alpha\ge
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]

Together with the small-endpoint theorem, \((\mathrm{LAG})\) is proved for
\[
0\le\alpha\le\frac12
\]
and for
\[
\alpha\ge
\alpha_E
=
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]

The unresolved Laguerre strip is
\[
\boxed{
\frac12<\alpha<
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
}
\]

# 5. Next move

The most efficient next target is the universal Laguerre bound
\[
\boxed{
\sup_{u\ge0}
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|^4
\le
\frac12,
\qquad n\ge1,\quad \alpha\ge\frac12.
}
\tag{U}
\]
If \((\mathrm{U})\) is proved, then the entire remaining Laguerre strip is closed immediately because
\[
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
>
\frac12
\]
throughout the strip.

There are two realistic ways to prove \((\mathrm{U})\):

1. **Analytic route:** prove a hard-edge/turning-point Laguerre maximum theorem using a quantified Liouville-Green/Prufer argument that keeps the centrifugal term
   \[
   -\frac{\alpha^2-1/4}{x^2}
   \]
   inside the potential and supplies explicit constants.

2. **Hybrid route:** prove \((\mathrm{U})\) for large \(n\) using a uniform Hilb asymptotic with a certified remainder, then verify the finite \(n<N_0\) cases by interval arithmetic. A4-11 describes this framework but does not yet provide the constant \(C\), threshold \(N_0\), or interval certificates.

That is the next move I would take.
