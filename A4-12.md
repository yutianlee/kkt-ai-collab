# KKT Conjecture: Round 11 Evaluation, Proof Attempt, and Audit

This round is valuable mainly as an audit of the remaining Laguerre endpoint obstruction. It corrects several false proof attempts, explores possible analytic routes, and ends with the same honest conclusion: the proof is not complete until a uniform Laguerre estimate, with explicit constants, is supplied.

## 1. Evaluation of A1-11

### 1.1 Valuable corrections

A1-11 gives the strongest correction of the round. In particular, it correctly identifies that Szego's Theorem 8.22.4 gives a **multiplicative** Hilb-type error,
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\operatorname{Principal}(u)
\left(
1+\frac{E(u)}{N}
\right),
\]
not an additive error of the form
\[
O\left(N^{-5/6}\alpha^{-1/3}\right).
\]
Thus the error inflates the principal Bessel term by a relative factor; it cannot simply be treated as an independent small additive correction.

A1-11 also correctly rejects the proposed concavity argument. The map
\[
\alpha\longmapsto \log |L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|^2
\]
is not generally concave, since the second derivative involves a signed measure coming from the oscillation and sign changes of the Laguerre polynomial.

Finally, A1-11 identifies the precise remaining obstruction: the Laguerre endpoint inequality in the strip
\[
\frac12<\alpha<
\alpha_E(n):=
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]
The required Laguerre bound is
\[
\boxed{
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/4}
}
\tag{LAG}
\]
for all \(u\ge0\), where
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]

## 2. Attempted Laguerre Closure

Set
\[
y(u)=\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\qquad
\lambda=n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2}.
\]
The transformed Laguerre equation has the form
\[
y''(u)+Q_L(u)y(u)=0,
\]
with
\[
Q_L(u)
=
\frac{\lambda}{u}
-\frac14
-\frac{\alpha^2-\frac14}{4u^2}.
\]

The goal is to prove \((\mathrm{LAG})\) throughout the strip
\[
\frac12<\alpha<\alpha_E(n).
\]

### 2.1 Krasikov/Sonin Bound Away From Turning Points

A Krasikov-type estimate suggests that at a local extremum \(u_*\),
\[
|y(u_*)|^2
\le
\frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{Q_L(u_*)}},
\]
provided \(Q_L(u_*)>0\) and the normalization constant is handled correctly.

The maximum of \(Q_L\) occurs at
\[
u_{\max}
=
\frac{\alpha^2-\frac14}{2\lambda},
\]
and
\[
Q_L(u_{\max})
=
\frac{\lambda^2}{\alpha^2-\frac14}
-\frac14.
\]
For \(\alpha\sim n\) and \(\lambda\sim 3n/2\), this gives
\[
Q_L(u_{\max})\sim2,
\qquad
|y(u_*)|^2
\lesssim
\frac{1}{\pi\sqrt2}
\approx0.225.
\]
This is far below the target scale
\[
T_L^2
=
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/2}
\sim
\frac{1}{\sqrt2}.
\]

However, this does not prove the global estimate. Near the outer turning point \(u_+\), one has \(Q_L(u_+)=0\), so the bound
\[
\frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{Q_L(u)}}
\]
diverges. The turning-point lobe remains the hard case.

### 2.2 First Extremum

Near \(u=0\), the function vanishes for \(\alpha>0\). The first zero is controlled by the Bessel approximation,
\[
z_1
\approx
\frac{j_{\alpha,1}^2}{4\lambda},
\qquad
j_{\alpha,1}
\approx
\alpha+1.856\,\alpha^{1/3}.
\]
The first extremum \(u_1\) lies before \(z_1\), so heuristically
\[
u_1\lesssim
\frac{\alpha^2}{4\lambda}.
\]
Then
\[
Q_L(u_1)
\sim
\frac{\lambda}{u_1}
\sim
\frac{4\lambda^2}{\alpha^2}.
\]
For \(\alpha\sim n\), this is roughly \(9/4\), giving
\[
|y(u_1)|^2
\lesssim
\frac{2}{3\pi}
\approx0.212,
\qquad
|y(u_1)|^4
\lesssim0.045.
\]
This is much smaller than the target
\[
T_L^4
=
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\sim
\frac12.
\]

This gives strong evidence at the first lobe, but it still does not control the last lobe near \(u_+\).

### 2.3 Last Extremum and Airy Scaling

Near the turning point \(u_+\approx4\lambda\), the wave transitions from oscillatory to exponentially decaying. The Airy approximation has the schematic form
\[
y(u)
\approx
C_{\mathrm{Ai}}\,
\operatorname{Ai}\!\left((u_+-u)\kappa^{1/3}\right),
\qquad
\kappa=\frac{|Q_L'(u_+)|}{4}.
\]
Since
\[
\max_x|\operatorname{Ai}(x)|=\operatorname{Ai}(0)\approx0.355,
\]
this route is plausible. But it requires an explicit normalization constant and a certified error term. The rough appeal to Szego's turning-point estimates does not supply those constants, and several attempted normalizations lead to contradictory growth estimates. Therefore this Airy route is not yet a proof.

### 2.4 Failed \(L^2\) and Sobolev Bounds

One possible route is the one-dimensional Sobolev estimate
\[
\|y\|_\infty^2
\le
\|y\|_2\|y'\|_2.
\]
Using the Laguerre identity
\[
\int_0^\infty \frac{y(u)^2}{u}\,du
=
\frac1\alpha,
\]
one gets only
\[
\|y'\|_2^2
\le
\frac{\lambda}{\alpha}
=
\frac{n}{\alpha}
+\frac12
+\frac{1}{2\alpha}.
\]
Thus
\[
\|y\|_\infty^4
\le
\frac{n}{\alpha}
+\frac12
+\frac{1}{2\alpha}.
\]
But for \(\alpha=n\), this is approximately \(3/2\), whereas the target is approximately \(1/2\). The Sobolev bound is therefore much too crude.

Similarly, the weighted estimate
\[
y(u)^4
\le
4\lambda^2
\int_0^\infty \frac{y(t)^2}{t^2}\,dt
\]
combined with
\[
\int_0^\infty \frac{y(t)^2}{t^2}\,dt
=
\frac{1}{\alpha(\alpha-1)}
\qquad(\alpha>1)
\]
gives
\[
y(u)^4
\le
\frac{4\lambda^2}{\alpha(\alpha-1)}.
\]
For \(\alpha\sim n\) and \(\lambda\sim3n/2\), this is roughly \(9\), again far above the target scale.

### 2.5 Turan and Christoffel-Darboux Routes

Classical Turan-type inequalities give
\[
\left[L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right]^2
-
L_{n-1}^{(\alpha)}(u)L_{n+1}^{(\alpha)}(u)
\ge0.
\]
The Christoffel-Darboux identity gives pointwise information about sums such as
\[
\sum_{k=0}^n
\frac{k!}{\Gamma(k+\alpha+1)}
\left[L_k^{(\alpha)}(u)\right]^2,
\]
but these identities do not immediately imply the sharp single-polynomial bound \((\mathrm{LAG})\). They may be useful ingredients, but they do not close the strip as written.

### 2.6 Hilb-Type Dynamic-Frequency Route

The most promising route remains the Hilb/Liouville-Green approximation with the true dynamic frequency. In schematic form,
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left(\frac{\zeta(u)}{u}\right)^{1/4}
\left(\frac{u}{\zeta'(u)}\right)^{1/2}
J_\alpha\!\left(2\sqrt{N\zeta(u)}\right)
\left(
1+\frac{E(u)}{N}
\right),
\]
where
\[
N=n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2}.
\]
The Bessel maximum satisfies the Landau-type scale
\[
\max_x |J_\alpha(x)|
\sim
c\,\alpha^{-1/3},
\qquad
c\approx0.6749.
\]
At the expected Bessel maximum, the geometric prefactor is heuristically close to \(1\), so the principal term should satisfy
\[
|y(u)|^4
\lesssim
0.207\,\alpha^{-4/3}
\left(
1+\frac{C_E}{N}
\right)^4.
\]
This strongly suggests that \((\mathrm{LAG})\) is true for all sufficiently large \(n\). But without a rigorous bound for \(C_E\) and an explicit threshold \(N_0\), the argument is still incomplete.

## 3. Status

The established regions remain:
\[
\alpha\le\frac12
\]
by the small-exponent Hadamard-Bessel argument, and
\[
\alpha\ge
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}
\]
by the Laguerre energy method.

The unproved strip is exactly
\[
\boxed{
\frac12<\alpha<
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
}
\]

This document does not complete the KKT proof. It gives strong evidence and identifies the right analytic route, but the decisive technical lemma is still missing: a uniform Hilb/Liouville-Green estimate with explicit constants strong enough to imply \((\mathrm{LAG})\) for all \(n\ge N_0\), followed by certified interval arithmetic for the finite range \(1\le n<N_0\).

## 4. Next Move

To complete the proof, one should prove a theorem of the following form.

There exist explicit constants \(C_E\) and \(N_0\) such that for every \(n\ge N_0\), every
\[
\frac12<\alpha<
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3},
\]
and every \(u\ge0\),
\[
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]
Then the remaining finite set
\[
1\le n<N_0,
\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}
\]
can be checked by rigorous interval arithmetic.

Until those constants and finite certificates are supplied, the proof remains incomplete at the Laguerre strip.
