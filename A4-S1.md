# KKT Conjecture for Jacobi Polynomials: Comprehensive Evaluation and Proof Strategy

This note synthesizes the current state of the KKT conjecture after the round-12 reports. The main conclusion is conservative: several parts of the proof are certified, but the final Laguerre strip still needs explicit constants and finite verification.

# 1. Evaluation of the Reports

## 1.1 A1-12

**Verdict: the most rigorous audit.**

The strongest point of A1-12 is its correction of the Hilb-type error term. Szego's Theorem 8.22.4 gives a multiplicative error,
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\operatorname{Principal}(u)
\left(
1+\frac{E(u)}{N}
\right),
\]
not an additive estimate such as
\[
O\left(N^{-5/6}\alpha^{-1/3}\right).
\]
This matters because the error inflates the Bessel principal term by a relative factor; it cannot be discarded as a smaller independent correction.

A1-12 also systematically rejects several plausible but insufficient approaches:

- Krasikov/Sonin estimates, because the bound degenerates at the turning point;
- Airy estimates, because the normalization constant is not yet computed;
- Sobolev bounds, because they give only \(\|y\|_\infty^4\lesssim 3/2\) against a target near \(1/2\);
- Turan and Christoffel-Darboux inequalities, because they do not directly give the sharp single-polynomial bound.

It precisely identifies the remaining Laguerre strip:
\[
\frac12<\alpha<
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]

It is also careful about the finite-\(\beta\) bridge: one needs a strict margin
\[
T-\sup |\ell|>\delta
\]
before a finite-\(\beta\) perturbation argument can be made rigorous.

The weakness is that A1-12 does not introduce a new analytic tool beyond the WKB/Hilb and finite-certification program.

## 1.2 A4-12

**Verdict: the best roadmap, but not an executed proof.**

A4-12 correctly rejects the finite-\(n\) fallacy: the remaining strip grows linearly with \(n\), so one cannot reduce directly to a finite computation without a uniform large-\(n\) estimate.

It also correctly insists on:

- explicit WKB or Liouville-Green remainder constants;
- a large-\(n\) / finite-\(n\) split;
- a separate finite-\(\beta\) bridge from Jacobi to Laguerre.

The weakness is that it does not execute those steps. Calling the proof "substantially complete" would be too strong: the architecture is clear, but the decisive constants are still missing.

# 2. Certified Modules

The following components are currently reliable.

| Module | Region or consequence |
| --- | --- |
| Sonin-Polya localization | Reduces the global maximum to endpoint lobes |
| Central contour theorem | Covers \([x_-,x_+]\subseteq[-\sigma,\sigma]\) |
| Weighted energy | Covers the region where \(J E\le T^4\) |
| Small endpoint exponent | Covers \(\alpha\le\frac12\) on the right and \(\beta\le\frac12\) on the left |
| Overflow confinement | Right overflow implies \(\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}\), with the left analogue |

Here
\[
J=
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta},
\qquad
E=
n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)},
\]
and the energy criterion is
\[
J E
\le
T^4.
\]

The remaining right endpoint obstruction is the Laguerre inequality
\[
\boxed{
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/4}
}
\tag{LAG}
\]
for all \(u\ge0\) in the strip
\[
\boxed{
\frac12<\alpha<
\alpha_E(n):=
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
}
\tag{S}
\]
The left endpoint has the symmetric condition with \(\beta\).

# 3. Laguerre Reduction

Let
\[
y(u)=\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\qquad
\lambda=n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2}.
\]
The normalized Laguerre function satisfies
\[
y''(u)+Q_L(u)y(u)=0,
\]
where
\[
Q_L(u)
=
\frac{\lambda}{u}
-\frac14
-\frac{\alpha^2-\frac14}{4u^2}.
\]

The point where \(Q_L\) is maximal is
\[
u_{\max}
=
\frac{\alpha^2-\frac14}{2\lambda},
\]
and
\[
Q_L(u_{\max})
=
\frac{\lambda^2}{\alpha^2-\frac14}
-\frac14.
\]
In the strip \((\mathrm{S})\), this quantity is uniformly positive and is large enough to make interior estimates plausible. The hard part is still the final lobe near the outer turning point \(u_+\), where \(Q_L(u_+)=0\).

# 4. Interior Estimate Program

A refined Krasikov/Sonin estimate should have the form
\[
|y(u_*)|^2
\le
\frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{Q_L(u_*)}}
\left(
1+\frac{C_1}{\lambda}
\right)
\tag{K}
\]
at a local extremum \(u_*\) with \(Q_L(u_*)>0\), for an explicit constant \(C_1\).

The proof sketch is:

1. Use Sonin-Polya monotonicity for the Laguerre equation.
2. Combine it with the \(L^2\) normalization
   \[
   \int_0^\infty y(u)^2\,du=1.
   \]
3. Compare each half-wave between consecutive zeros with a constant-frequency sine wave.

For a half-wave near \(u_*\), the expected lower mass estimate is
\[
\int_{z_k}^{z_{k+1}} y(u)^2\,du
\gtrsim
\frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{Q_L(u_*)}}
|y(u_*)|^2.
\]
Summing over half-waves gives the desired pointwise control away from the turning point.

This covers much of the oscillatory region, but it does not settle the last lobe because \(Q_L\) tends to zero there.

# 5. Turning-Point Airy Program

The turning point should be handled with an explicit Liouville-Green/Airy transformation. Define \(\xi=\xi(u)\) by
\[
\frac{2}{3}\xi^{3/2}
=
\int_{u_+}^{u}
\sqrt{-Q_L(t)}\,dt,
\qquad u\ge u_+,
\]
and
\[
\frac{2}{3}(-\xi)^{3/2}
=
\int_u^{u_+}
\sqrt{Q_L(t)}\,dt,
\qquad u\le u_+.
\]
Let
\[
\phi(u)=|Q_L(u)|^{-1/4}|\xi(u)|^{1/4},
\qquad
W(\xi)=\phi(u)^{-1}y(u).
\]
Then \(W\) should satisfy an Airy-normal equation of the schematic form
\[
\frac{d^2W}{d\xi^2}
=
\left(
\xi+\frac{\psi(\xi)}{\lambda^2}
\right)W,
\]
where \(\psi\) is explicitly computable from \(Q_L\).

Olver's theorem then gives
\[
W(\xi)
=
a\,\operatorname{Ai}(\xi)
+b\,\operatorname{Bi}(\xi)
+\varepsilon(\xi),
\]
with an error estimate of the form
\[
|\varepsilon(\xi)|
\le
\frac{C_0}{\lambda}
\max(|a|,|b|)\,M(\xi).
\]
Since the Laguerre function decays for \(u>u_+\), the growing \(\operatorname{Bi}\) component should vanish:
\[
b=0.
\]
The constant \(a\) must be determined from the \(L^2\) normalization. This is the first genuinely hard missing calculation.

# 6. Prufer Growth Control

Another route is to write
\[
y(u)=r(u)\sin\theta(u),
\qquad
\frac{y'(u)}{\sqrt{Q_L(u)}}=r(u)\cos\theta(u)
\]
inside the oscillatory region. Then
\[
\frac{d}{du}\log r(u)
=
-
\frac{Q_L'(u)}{4Q_L(u)}
\sin(2\theta(u)).
\]
Integrating gives the crude but rigorous estimate
\[
\frac{r(u)}{r(u_0)}
\le
\left(
\frac{\max(Q_L(u),Q_L(u_0))}
{\min(Q_L(u),Q_L(u_0))}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

This is useful away from the turning point. For example, if \(u_1\) is the first extremum and \(u_k\) is an interior extremum with \(Q_L(u_k)\ge1\), then
\[
|y(u_k)|
\le
|y(u_1)|
\left(
\frac{Q_L(u_1)}{Q_L(u_k)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]
Using the heuristic estimates
\[
u_1\sim\frac{\alpha^2}{4\lambda},
\qquad
Q_L(u_1)\sim\frac{4\lambda^2}{\alpha^2},
\qquad
|y(u_1)|^2\lesssim\frac{2}{3\pi},
\]
one gets strong interior control.

The same crude Prufer estimate is still too weak at the final lobe. A sharpened, averaged Prufer argument is needed.

# 7. Hilb Prefactor and Gamma Ratios

The Hilb-type approach isolates a prefactor
\[
K_{\mathrm{Hilb}}
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
N^{-\alpha/2},
\qquad
N=n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2}.
\]

For \(\alpha\ge1\), Kershaw's inequality gives
\[
\frac{\Gamma(x+s)}{\Gamma(x)}
\le
\left(
x+\frac{s-1}{2}
\right)^s,
\qquad x>0,\quad s\ge1.
\]
Taking \(x=n+1\) and \(s=\alpha\),
\[
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}
\le
\left(
n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2}
\right)^\alpha
=
N^\alpha.
\]
Thus
\[
K_{\mathrm{Hilb}}\le1.
\]

For \(1/2\le\alpha<1\), Wendel-Gautschi type inequalities similarly give
\[
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}
\le
(n+1)^\alpha
\le
N^\alpha,
\]
and hence again
\[
K_{\mathrm{Hilb}}\le1.
\]

This is an important cancellation. The remaining problem is not the Gamma-ratio prefactor, but rather the geometric factor and the explicit Hilb error term.

# 8. Bessel and Geometric Factors

The Bessel factor satisfies Landau-type bounds. For \(\alpha\ge1\),
\[
\sup_{x\ge0}|J_\alpha(x)|
\le
c_L\alpha^{-1/3},
\qquad
c_L\approx0.6749.
\]
For \(\alpha=1/2\),
\[
J_{1/2}(x)
=
\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi x}}\sin x,
\]
so
\[
\sup_{x\ge0}|J_{1/2}(x)|
=
\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}
\approx0.7979.
\]

The Hilb geometric factor has the schematic form
\[
G(u)
=
\left(\frac{\zeta(u)}{u}\right)^{1/4}
\left(\frac{u}{\zeta'(u)}\right)^{1/2}.
\]
Near the origin,
\[
\zeta(u)
=
u
\left(
1-\frac{u}{8N}
+O\left(\frac{u^2}{N^2}\right)
\right),
\]
so \(G(u)\) is close to \(1\) there.

A conservative provisional bound would be
\[
G(u)\le2,
\]
which gives
\[
|\operatorname{Principal}(u)|
\le
2c_L\alpha^{-1/3}.
\]
For \(\alpha\ge8\), this is below \(0.675\), while the target in the strip satisfies
\[
T_L
=
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/4}
\ge
2^{-1/4}
\approx0.841.
\]

This indicates substantial room. But the bound \(G(u)\le2\), and the associated Hilb remainder, must be proved explicitly before this becomes a proof.

# 9. Known Maximum Bounds

Several known families of estimates may be relevant:

- Krasikov-type inequalities for Laguerre polynomial amplitudes;
- Lorch-type inequalities for Laguerre forms;
- sharp uniform Laguerre function bounds in the recent literature.

The exact theorem needed is:
\[
\sup_{u\ge0}
\left|
\left(
\frac{n!}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
\right|
\le
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/4}
\]
for every \(n\ge1\) and \(\alpha\ge1/2\).

If such a theorem is available with the exact constants above, then the Laguerre strip closes immediately. The current draft does not prove or cite such a theorem in a verifiable way, so it remains a target rather than a completed step.

# 10. Failed Crude Alternatives

The ordinary Sobolev estimate
\[
\|y\|_\infty^2
\le
\|y\|_2\|y'\|_2
\]
is too weak. For fixed \(\alpha\) and large \(n\), it leads to growth of order \(n/\alpha\), whereas the KKT target is \(O(1)\).

Christoffel-Darboux estimates also appear too crude. The kernel
\[
K_n(x,y)
=
\sum_{k=0}^n
\frac{k!}{\Gamma(k+\alpha+1)}
L_k^{(\alpha)}(x)L_k^{(\alpha)}(y)
\]
gives
\[
\frac{n!}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\left[L_n^{(\alpha)}(x)\right]^2
\le
K_n(x,x),
\]
and hence
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)^2
\le
e^{-u}u^\alpha K_n(u,u).
\]
But standard Christoffel-function estimates still grow near the oscillatory edges and do not recover the required sharp pointwise constant.

# 11. Feasible Completion Program

The proof can be completed if the following lemmas are established.

## Lemma A: Interior Krasikov Bound

There is an explicit constant \(C_A\) such that every interior local extremum satisfies
\[
|y(u_*)|^4
\le
C_A n^{-4/3}
\]
away from the turning point.

This should follow from refined Sonin-Polya theory, Prufer variables, and \(L^2\) normalization.

## Lemma B: Turning-Point Lobe Bound

There is an explicit constant \(C_{\mathrm{tp}}\le10\) such that the maximum in the last oscillatory lobe satisfies
\[
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}|_{\max,\mathrm{last}}^4
\le
C_{\mathrm{tp}}n^{-4/3}.
\]

The expected proof combines:

- the bound \(\mathcal S(u_{\max})\le C/n\) from \(L^2\) normalization;
- averaged Prufer growth;
- the turning-point scaling \(Q_L(u_{\mathrm{last}})\gtrsim c n^{-2/3}\);
- an explicit Olver error estimate.

This is the hardest step.

## Lemma C: Finite Verification

For
\[
1\le n<N_0,
\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le\alpha_E(n),
\]
verify \((\mathrm{LAG})\) by rigorous interval arithmetic. For each fixed \(n\), the function is a degree-\(n\) polynomial times \(u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}\), so the verification is finite once \(N_0\) is explicit.

## Lemma D: Jacobi-to-Laguerre Lift

For \(\beta\ge\beta_0\), prove a uniform estimate
\[
\left|
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
\right|
\le
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
+\frac{C'}{\beta},
\]
with \(C'\) explicit on the relevant parameter range.

A strict Laguerre margin then transfers the endpoint bound to finite but large \(\beta\).

## Lemma E: Compact Finite-\(\beta\) Region

For \(0\le\beta<\beta_0\) and
\[
\frac12<\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n},
\]
cover the remaining compact region by a combination of analytic estimates and interval arithmetic.

# 12. Conditional Assembly

If Lemmas A-E are proved with explicit constants, then the final KKT estimate follows:
\[
\boxed{
|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}
}
\]
for all
\[
\alpha,\beta\ge0,
\qquad
n\ge1,
\qquad
x\in[-1,1].
\]

# 13. Honest Status

The strategy is complete in architecture, but not complete as a proof. The missing work is explicit and technical:

| Step | Difficulty | Main tool |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lemma A | Medium | Prufer analysis plus \(L^2\) normalization |
| Lemma B | Hard | Olver Liouville-Green theory or sharp averaged Prufer estimates |
| Lemma C | Easy computationally | Arb or interval arithmetic |
| Lemma D | Medium | Jacobi-Laguerre asymptotics |
| Lemma E | Medium | Analytic estimates plus finite certification |

The single hardest task is Lemma B: making the averaged Prufer growth through the turning-point lobe rigorous with explicit constants. Olver's Liouville-Green theory is the most natural replacement for the current heuristic.

Useful references for the next step include:

- F. W. J. Olver, *Asymptotics and Special Functions*, Chapter 11;
- I. Krasikov, inequalities for Laguerre polynomial amplitudes;
- D. K. Dimitrov and G. P. Nikolov, sharp Laguerre zero bounds;
- G. Nemes, explicit Hilb-type Laguerre expansions.

Until those constants and finite certificates are supplied, the KKT conjecture remains open at the final Laguerre strip.
