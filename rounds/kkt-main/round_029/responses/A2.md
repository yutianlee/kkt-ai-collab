## Detailed Derivations

### 1. Rational Normal Form and Residual Bound

We start with the exact endpoint equation $(p_B H')' + q_B H = 0$, where $p_B(u) = u(1-u/B)$ and $K_B(u) = p_B q_B = -\frac{\alpha^2}{4} + \Lambda_B u - \Delta_B u^2$.
Applying the Liouville transformation $Y_u = p_B^{1/2} H$ yields $Y_u'' + Q_u(u) Y_u = 0$, with $Q_u(u) = \frac{K_B(u) + 1/4}{p_B(u)^2}$.

We transform to the rational coordinate $z = \frac{Bu}{B-u}$, which implies $u = \frac{Bz}{B+z}$ and $\frac{du}{dz} = \frac{B^2}{(B+z)^2}$.
Let $Y(z) = z^{1/2} H(u(z))$. Since $z$ is a Mobius transformation of $u$, its Schwarzian derivative is identically zero. The transformed potential is simply $Q_z(z) = Q_u(u(z)) \left( \frac{du}{dz} \right)^2$.
Substituting $K_B(u)$ and $p_B(u)$ gives:
$$ Q_z(z) = \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} + \frac{\Lambda_B B}{z(B+z)} - \frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2} $$
Using partial fractions $\frac{B}{z(B+z)} = \frac{1}{z} - \frac{1}{B+z}$, we obtain the exact rational normal form:
$$ Y'' + \left( \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} + \Delta Q(z) \right) Y = 0 $$
where the residual is $\Delta Q(z) = -\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z} - \frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2}$.

Since $z \ge 0$, $\Delta Q(z)$ is strictly negative, and its magnitude is maximized at $z=0$:
$$ M_Q = \sup_{z \ge 0} |\Delta Q(z)| = \frac{\Lambda_B}{B} + \Delta_B $$
Using $\Lambda_B = c_B + \frac{\alpha r_B}{2}$ and $\Delta_B = \frac{c_B}{B} + \frac{r_B^2}{4}$, where $c_B \approx n$ and $B \approx n$, we find $M_Q \approx \frac{2n}{n} + \frac{\alpha}{2n} + \frac{1}{4} = 2.25 + \frac{\alpha}{2n}$. For $\alpha \le n$, $M_Q \le 2.75$. This confirms the cap residual bound at the heuristic level, but the proof draft must replace the approximation by a monotone rational inequality. A safe way to do this is to write
$$
M_Q=\frac{c_B}{B}+\frac{\alpha r_B}{2B}+\frac{c_B}{B}+\frac{r_B^2}{4}
=\frac{2c_B}{B}+\frac{\alpha r_B}{2B}+\frac{r_B^2}{4}.
$$
Since $0\le r_B\le1$, the last term is at most $1/4$. The two terms involving $c_B/B$ and $\alpha r_B/(2B)$ must then be bounded from the exact residual-strip assumptions. This is not difficult, but it is one of the places where the proof must avoid replacing $B$ by $n$ too early. For example, $c_B/B=(n+1/2-(n+1)/(2B))/B$ is below $(n+1/2)/B$, and $\alpha r_B/(2B)\le \alpha/(2B)$. The remaining task is to combine $\alpha<\alpha_E(n)$ with $B=n+\alpha+\beta+1$ to give a printed uniform constant. The constant $2.75$ is plausible and likely conservative, but in a formal proof it should be introduced as a lemma with an exact one-line verification, not as an asymptotic estimate.

### 2. Pre-Peak Volterra Bound

Let $W_1(z) = \sqrt{z} J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})$ be the Bessel reference solution, satisfying $W_1'' + \left( \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} \right) W_1 = 0$.
Define the relative amplitude $h(z) = Y(z) / W_1(z)$. Substituting $Y = h W_1$ into the normal form yields the exact identity:
$$ (W_1^2 h')' = |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h $$
Integrating twice from $0$ to $z$, and using $h'(0)=0$, we get the Volterra equation:
$$ h(z) = h(0) + \int_0^z \frac{1}{W_1(x)^2} \int_0^x |\Delta Q(s)| W_1(s)^2 h(s) \, ds \, dx $$
Let $T = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}$. Then $dz = \frac{T}{2\Lambda_B} dT$ and $W_1(z)^2 = \frac{T^2}{4\Lambda_B} J_\alpha(T)^2$.
For $T \le j'_{\alpha,1}$ (the first Bessel peak), $J_\alpha(T)$ is strictly increasing. Thus, in the inner integral, $J_\alpha(S) \le J_\alpha(X)$ for $S \le X$.
Bounding $|\Delta Q| \le M_Q$ and pulling $\|h\|$ out, the inner integral is bounded by:
$$ \int_0^X \frac{S^2}{4\Lambda_B} J_\alpha(S)^2 \frac{S}{2\Lambda_B} dS \le \frac{J_\alpha(X)^2}{8\Lambda_B^2} \int_0^X S^3 dS = \frac{J_\alpha(X)^2 X^4}{32\Lambda_B^2} $$
The outer integral becomes:
$$ \int_0^T \frac{4\Lambda_B}{X^2 J_\alpha(X)^2} \left( \frac{M_Q \|h\| J_\alpha(X)^2 X^4}{32\Lambda_B^2} \right) \frac{X}{2\Lambda_B} dX = \frac{M_Q \|h\|}{16\Lambda_B^2} \int_0^T X^3 dX = \frac{M_Q T^4}{64\Lambda_B^2} \|h\| $$
By Gronwall's inequality, we obtain the pre-peak Volterra bound:
$$ h(z) \le M_{n,\alpha,B} \exp\left( M_Q \frac{T^4}{64\Lambda_B^2} \right) \qquad \text{for } T \le j'_{\alpha,1} $$

### 2a. Audit of the Volterra Kernel

The Volterra estimate is the cleanest part of the rational-Bessel route, but it has three boundary conditions that must be made explicit in the proof.

First, the normalization at $z=0$ must be read through the Frobenius expansion, not imposed by hand. Near $z=0$, the Bessel reference satisfies
$$
W_1(z)=\sqrt{z}J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})
\sim \frac{\Lambda_B^{\alpha/2}}{\Gamma(\alpha+1)}z^{(\alpha+1)/2}.
$$
The true endpoint solution $Y=z^{1/2}H$ has the same power $z^{(\alpha+1)/2}$ multiplied by the endpoint normalization. Therefore $h(0)$ is finite and equals the gamma-ratio factor $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ after the $u$-to-$z$ conversion is accounted for. This endpoint matching is not optional: if a factor of $B/(B+z)$ is lost, the Volterra estimate would carry the wrong amplitude.

Second, the sign in $(W_1^2 h')'=|\Delta Q|W_1^2h$ depends on $\Delta Q\le0$ and on the convention for the normal-form equation. With $Y''+(P_0+\Delta Q)Y=0$ and $W_1''+P_0W_1=0$, substitution gives
$$
(W_1^2 h')'=-\Delta Q\,W_1^2h.
$$
Thus the right side is positive only because $\Delta Q$ is negative. This sign is the reason the quotient $h$ is increasing and the true critical point is delayed. If a later draft changes the sign convention to $-Y''+VY=0$, the same line must be rewritten; otherwise the comparison direction reverses.

Third, the inequality using monotonicity of $J_\alpha(T)$ is valid only up to $j'_{\alpha,1}$. It does not give a bound on the post-peak interval where $J_\alpha$ decreases and eventually crosses zero. Consequently the pre-peak Gronwall estimate is a local lemma. It can support a small-$\alpha$ proof only if the true first critical point is shown to occur before the first zero or if the argument is replaced by a zero-free amplitude variable.

### 3. Post-Peak Critical Point Balance and Phase Shift

At the true first critical point $u_1$, $H'(u_1) = 0$. Since $Y(z) = z^{1/2} H(u(z))$, we have $Y' = \frac{1}{2} z^{-1/2} H + z^{1/2} H_u \frac{du}{dz}$. At $z_1^*$, $H_u = 0$, so $Y'(z_1^*) = \frac{1}{2z_1^*} Y(z_1^*)$.
Using $Y = h W_1$, we have $Y' = h' W_1 + h W_1'$. Equating the two expressions for $Y'$:
$$ h' W_1 + h W_1' = \frac{1}{2z} h W_1 \implies h' = h \left( \frac{1}{2z} - \frac{W_1'}{W_1} \right) $$
Since $W_1(z) = z^{1/2} J_\alpha(T)$, we have $\frac{W_1'}{W_1} = \frac{1}{2z} + \frac{2\Lambda_B}{T} \frac{J_\alpha'(T)}{J_\alpha(T)}$.
Thus, at the critical point $z_1^*$:
$$ h'(z_1^*) = -h(z_1^*) \frac{2\Lambda_B}{T_1^*} \frac{J_\alpha'(T_1^*)}{J_\alpha(T_1^*)} $$
From the integral identity $(W_1^2 h')' = |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h$, we integrate from $0$ to $z_1^*$:
$$ W_1(z_1^*)^2 h'(z_1*) = \int_0^{z_1^*} |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h \, dz $$
Substituting $h'(z_1^*)$ and $W_1(z_1^*)^2 = \frac{(T_1^*)^2}{4\Lambda_B} J_\alpha(T_1^*)^2$, we get the exact post-peak balance:
$$ -T_1^* J_\alpha(T_1^*) J_\alpha'(T_1^*) = \frac{2}{h(z_1^*)} \int_0^{z_1^*} |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h \, dz $$
Since the RHS is strictly positive, we must have $J_\alpha'(T_1^*) < 0$. This proves that the true critical point $T_1^*$ lies *after* the Bessel reference peak $j'_{\alpha,1}$.

Let $\delta = T_1^* - j'_{\alpha,1}$ be the phase shift. Near the peak, $J_\alpha'(T_1^*) \approx J_\alpha''(j'_{\alpha,1}) \delta$.
From Bessel's equation, $J_\alpha''(j'_{\alpha,1}) = - \left( 1 - \frac{\alpha^2}{(j'_{\alpha,1})^2} \right) J_\alpha(j'_{\alpha,1})$.
Thus, the LHS is approximately $j'_{\alpha,1} J_\alpha(j'_{\alpha,1})^2 \left( 1 - \frac{\alpha^2}{(j'_{\alpha,1})^2} \right) \delta$.
The RHS is bounded by $\frac{M_Q}{4\Lambda_B^2} J_\alpha(j'_{\alpha,1})^2 \frac{(j'_{\alpha,1})^4}{4}$.
Equating and solving for $\delta$:
$$ \delta \lesssim \frac{M_Q (j'_{\alpha,1})^3}{16\Lambda_B^2 \left( 1 - \frac{\alpha^2}{(j'_{\alpha,1})^2} \right)} $$
For large $\alpha$, $j'_{\alpha,1} \approx \alpha + 0.8086 \alpha^{1/3}$, and $1 - \frac{\alpha^2}{(j'_{\alpha,1})^2} \approx 1.617 \alpha^{-2/3}$.
This gives the phase shift estimate:
$$ \delta \lesssim \frac{M_Q \alpha^{11/3}}{25.8 \Lambda_B^2} $$

### 4. Zero-Safety Bootstrap Threshold

For the quotient $h = Y/W_1$ to remain non-singular, the true critical point $T_1^*$ must occur before the first Bessel zero $j_{\alpha,1}$. Thus, we require $\delta < j_{\alpha,1} - j'_{\alpha,1}$.
The Bessel zero gap is $j_{\alpha,1} - j'_{\alpha,1} \approx 1.047 \alpha^{1/3}$.
Enforcing the zero-safety condition:
$$ \frac{M_Q \alpha^{11/3}}{25.8 \Lambda_B^2} < 1.047 \alpha^{1/3} \implies \alpha^{10/3} < \frac{27 \Lambda_B^2}{M_Q} $$
Since $\Lambda_B \approx n$ and $M_Q \approx 2.25$, we have $\alpha^{10/3} \lesssim 12 n^2$, which yields the threshold:
$$ \alpha \lesssim 2.0 n^{3/5} $$
This indicates that the rational-Bessel quotient method is not zero-safe for $\alpha \ge C n^{3/5}$ unless a stronger post-peak continuation lemma is added. The phase-shift estimate overwhelms the zero gap in the bulk transition strip $\alpha = c n$, so the quotient variable is expected to become singular before the relevant true critical point. This is a serious method obstruction, but the exact threshold and constant still require interval-enclosed Bessel zero bounds and a Taylor remainder estimate.

### 4a. What Is Exact and What Is Bootstrap

The following separation is important for the judge and for A1/A3.

Exact identities:

1. The rational normal form and residual $\Delta Q(z)$.
2. The sign $\Delta Q(z)<0$ for $z\ge0$.
3. The relative-amplitude identity $(W_1^2h')'=-\Delta Q W_1^2h$ on any interval where $W_1\neq0$.

## Potential Gaps

- The conversion between the physical cap endpoint $u_\sigma$ and the Bessel variable $T=2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}$ should be checked. ## Counterexample Or Obstruction Search

The primary obstruction is the **Phase Shift Singularity**. If we attempt to use the rational-Bessel quotient method for $\alpha = n/2$, the phase shift estimate scales as $O(n^{5/3})$, while the zero gap $j_{\alpha,1} - j'_{\alpha,1}$ scales as $O(n^{1/3})$. ## Divergent Alternatives And 20% Exploration

**Ermakov-Pinney Non-Dividing Amplitude Equation:**
To bypass the zero-safety obstruction entirely, we can abandon the quotient $h = Y/W_1$ and instead define a strictly positive amplitude $A(z)$ such that $Y(z) = A(z) \sin \theta(z)$, with $\theta'(z) = 1/A(z)^2$.
The normal form $Y'' + P(z) Y = 0$ transforms into the Ermakov-Pinney equation:
$$ A'' + P(z) A = \frac{1}{A^3} $$
where $P(z) = \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} + \Delta Q(z)$.
Let $A_0(z)$ be the amplitude for the Bessel reference equation, satisfying $A_0'' + P_0(z) A_0 = \frac{1}{A_0^3}$, where $P_0(z) = \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2}$.
Since $\Delta Q(z) < 0$, we have $P(z) < P_0(z)$. This implies $A'' - \frac{1}{A^3} = -P A > -P_0 A$, which strongly suggests $A(z) > A_0(z)$ (assuming matching initial conditions).
Because $A(z)$ is strictly positive, it never encounters the Bessel zeros. The KKT target requires bounding $Y(z_1^*) = A(z_1^*) \sin \theta(z_1^*)$. At the critical point, $Y' = \frac{1}{2z} Y$, which provides a boundary condition linking $A, A'$, and $\theta$. This non-dividing amplitude method is highly promising for extending the rational-Bessel track to larger $\alpha$ without hitting the zero-safety wall.
