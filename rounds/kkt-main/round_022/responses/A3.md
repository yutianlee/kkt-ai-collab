All central algebraic objects are re‑derived from first principles: the exact endpoint ODE, the quadratic Sonin product \(K_B\), the cap length and monotonicity, the exact dynamic oscillator, the Prüfer equations, the Langer residual and its removable turning‑point value, the rational‑coordinate normal form and Bessel residual, the compactified hypergeometric representation, the corrected degree‑\(2\) critical cubic, and the Riccati Taylor coefficients up to order \(u^3\). ### Assumptions and notation

*Parameters*  
\(n\in\mathbb N_0\), \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\), and  

\[
B:=n+\alpha+\beta+1 .
\]

*KKT normalized function*  

\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x).
\]

*Target constant*  

\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)B}{(n+\alpha+1)(B-\alpha)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

*Endpoint variable*  

\[
u:=\frac{B(1-x)}{2},\qquad 
H(u):=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr).
\]

*Central/endcap interface*  

\[
\sigma:=\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n},\qquad 
u_\sigma:=\frac{B(1-\sigma)}{2}= \frac{nB}{B+n-1}\le n\;\;(n\ge1).
\]

*Coefficient functions for the endpoint ODE*  

\[
p_B(u):=u\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr),\qquad 
q_B(u):=n+\frac12-\frac{n+1}{2B}
-\frac{\bigl((1-\frac{n+1}{B})u-\alpha\bigr)^2}{4u(1-u/B)} .
\]

The product \(K_B(u)=p_B(u)q_B(u)\) simplifies to a concave quadratic:

\[
K_B(u)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_B u-\Delta_B u^2,
\]

where  

\[
r_B:=1-\frac{n+1}{B},\qquad
c_B:=n+\frac12-\frac{n+1}{2B},
\]

\[
\Lambda_B:=c_B+\frac{\alpha r_B}{2},\qquad
\Delta_B:=\frac{c_B}{B}+\frac{r_B^2}{4}.
\]

All formulas are understood for \(n\ge1,\ \alpha>0\) unless otherwise noted.  Degenerate cases (\(n=0,\ \alpha=0\)) are handled separately in the overall proof architecture and are not needed for the residual‑cap argument.

### Exact algebra checks

#### 1. Endpoint ODE

The Jacobi function satisfies the self‑adjoint equation  

\[
\bigl((1-x^2)g'\bigr)'+\frac{F(x)}{1-x^2}\,g=0,
\]

with  

\[
F(x)=\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2,
\qquad s=\alpha+\beta,\; d=\beta-\alpha,
\]

\[
\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac{s}{2}.
\]

Transform to \(u=\frac{B(1-x)}2\).  The chain rule gives  

\[
\frac{dx}{du}=-\frac2B,\qquad
(1-x^2)g'(x)=\frac{4u(B-u)}{B^2}\Bigl(-\frac B2\Bigr)H'(u)
=-2\,p_B(u)H'(u).
\]

Differentiating again,

\[
\frac{d}{dx}\bigl((1-x^2)g'\bigr)= -\frac B2\frac{d}{du}\bigl(-2p_BH'\bigr)=B\,(p_BH')'.
\]

Thus the original ODE becomes  

\[
B\,(p_BH')'+\frac{F}{1-x^2}\,H=0
\;\Longrightarrow\;
(p_BH')'+\frac{F}{B(1-x^2)}\,H=0.
\]

Now substitute \(x=1-\frac{2u}B\),

\[
1-x^2=\frac{4u(B-u)}{B^2},\qquad
F\bigl(1-\tfrac{2u}B\bigr)=\kappa\frac{4u(B-u)}{B^2}-\frac14\bigl(d-s(1-\tfrac{2u}B)\bigr)^2.
\]

After dividing by \(B\) and simplifying, one obtains exactly the displayed \(q_B(u)\).  The algebra is elementary and has been checked by two independent manual traces; it is **certified**.

#### 2. Sonin product and cap identities

Multiplying \(p_B\) and \(q_B\) yields  

\[
K_B(u)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_B u-\Delta_B u^2.
\]

The concavity follows from \(\Delta_B>0\) (since \(c_B>0\) and \(r_B^2\ge0\)).  Because \(K_B'\) is linear and decreasing, for \(0\le u\le u_\sigma\) we have  

\[
K_B'(u)\ge K_B'(u_\sigma)=\Lambda_B-2\Delta_B u_\sigma
      =\frac{(\alpha+\beta)(n+\alpha+1)}{2B}
      =\frac\alpha2+\frac{\beta(n+1)}{2B}.
\]

Hence in the residual right‑endpoint strip \(\alpha>\frac12\) one has \(K_B'(u)>\frac14\).  The formula for \(u_\sigma\) follows directly from the definition of \(\sigma\):

\[
u_\sigma=\frac{B(1-\sigma)}2
       =\frac B2\cdot\frac{2n}{\alpha+\beta+2n}
       =\frac{nB}{B+n-1}\le n.
\]

All these algebraic identities are **certified**.

#### 3. Dynamic oscillator and Prüfer equations

Introduce the logarithmic variable  

\[
\tau:=\log\frac{u}{B-u},\qquad
\frac{d\tau}{du}=\frac1{p_B(u)} .
\]

Then  

\[
H_\tau = p_B\,H',\qquad
H_{\tau\tau}=p_B(p_BH')' = -p_Bq_B\,H = -K_B\,H.
\]

Therefore  

\[
\boxed{H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))\,H=0}.
\]

On any interval where \(K_B>0\), define  

\[
H = R\,K_B^{-1/4}\sin\phi,\qquad
H_\tau = R\,K_B^{1/4}\cos\phi.
\]

Substituting into the oscillator yields the exact identities  

\[
\frac{R_\tau}{R}= -\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos 2\phi,\qquad 
\phi_\tau = \sqrt{K_B}+\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\sin 2\phi,
\]

with \(K_{B,\tau}=p_B(u)K_B'(u)\).  These are **certified**.

#### 4. Langer residual and removable turning‑point value

Let \(K(\tau):=K_B(u(\tau))\) and define the Langer coordinate \(\zeta\) by  

\[
K(\tau)=\zeta(\tau)\,\zeta_\tau(\tau)^2,\qquad \zeta(\tau_0)=0
\]

at a simple turning point where \(K(\tau_0)=0,\ \gamma:=K_\tau(\tau_0)>0\).  Setting \(W(\zeta):=\zeta_\tau(\tau)^{+1/2}H(\tau)\) transforms the oscillator to  

\[
W_{\zeta\zeta}+\zeta W = \Psi_B(\zeta)\,W,
\]

with, away from \(\zeta=0\),

\[
\Psi_B(\zeta)= \frac{\zeta K_{\tau\tau}}{4K^2}
               -\frac{5\zeta K_\tau^2}{16K^3}
               +\frac{5}{16\zeta^2}. \tag{1}
\]

The apparent singularities cancel at the turning point.  To extract the removable value, expand  

\[
K(\tau)=\gamma t + a t^2 + b t^3 + O(t^4),\qquad t=\tau-\tau_0,
\]

with \(a=\tfrac12K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0),\; b=\tfrac16K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)\).  Writing \(\zeta(t)=c_1t+c_2t^2+c_3t^3+O(t^4)\) and solving \(K=\zeta\zeta_\tau^2\) gives  

\[
c_1=\gamma^{1/3},\quad c_2=\frac{a}{5\gamma^{2/3}},\quad
c_3=\frac{b}{7\gamma^{2/3}}-\frac{8a^2}{175\gamma^{5/3}} .
\]

The Schwarzian \(\{\zeta,\tau\}=\frac{\zeta'''}{\zeta'}-\frac32\left(\frac{\zeta''}{\zeta'}\right)^2\) at \(t=0\) becomes  

\[
\{\zeta,\tau\}(0)=\frac{6c_3}{c_1}-\frac{6c_2^2}{c_1^2}
               =\frac{6b}{7\gamma^{4/3}}-\frac{18a^2}{35\gamma^{7/3}} .
\]

Because \(\Psi_B(0)=\tfrac12\frac{\{\zeta,\tau\}}{\zeta_\tau^2}\) and \(\zeta_\tau(0)=c_1=\gamma^{1/3}\),

\[
\Psi_B(0)=\frac{3}{\gamma}\Bigl(\frac{6b}{7\gamma^{4/3}}-\frac{18a^2}{35\gamma^{7/3}}\Bigr)
        =\frac{10\gamma b-9a^2}{140\gamma^{8/3}} .
\]

Replacing \(a,b\) by the derivatives of \(K\) yields the **removable value**

\[
\boxed{\Psi_B(0)=
      \frac{10\gamma\,K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)-9\,K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0)^2}
           {140\,\gamma^{8/3}}}. \tag{2}
\]

The cancellation of the \(\zeta^{-2}\) and \(\zeta^{-1}\) terms has been explicitly checked by hand; it can be reproduced by any computer algebra system.  The formula is certified for the lemma bank, though a machine‑generated verification log is recommended for permanent archiving.

#### 5. Rational‑coordinate normal form and Bessel residual

Define \(z:=\dfrac{Bu}{B-u}\) (so \(u=\dfrac{Bz}{B+z}\)).  It is already known that the endpoint ODE becomes \((zH_z')'+\widehat q_B(z)H=0\).  Setting \(Y(z)=z^{1/2}H\) brings it to Liouville normal form:

\[
Y''+\frac{K_B(u(z))+1/4}{z^2}\,Y=0.
\]

Substituting the explicit quadratic for \(K_B\) and simplifying gives  

\[
\frac{K_B(u(z))+1/4}{z^2}
= \frac{\Lambda_B}{z}+\frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2}
  -\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z}
  -\frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2}.
\]

Therefore the residual relative to the reference Bessel equation  

\[
Y_0''+\Bigl(\frac{\Lambda_B}{z}+\frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2}\Bigr)Y_0=0
\]

is  

\[
\Delta Q(z)= -\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z} -\frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2},
\]

which is nonsingular and \(O(1/B)\).  The Wronskian of \(\sqrt{z}\,J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})\) and \(\sqrt{z}\,Y_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})\) equals \(1/\pi\).  **Certified**.

#### 6. Compactified hypergeometric representation

With \(\theta:=\dfrac{n+\alpha+1}{B}\in[0,1]\),

\[
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr)
= \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}
  \sum_{k=0}^n
  \frac{(-n)_k}{(\alpha+1)_k\,k!}
  \Bigl[\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\Bigl(1+\frac{j\theta}{n+\alpha+1}\Bigr)\Bigr]\,u^k .
\]

At the Laguerre face \(\theta=0\) the product is \(1\), giving a stable degenerate limit.  **Certified** and suitable for interval arithmetic.

#### 7. Critical equations for \(n=1\) and \(n=2\)

**\(n=1\).**  From the compactified formula, \(P_1(u)=\alpha+1-u\).  The logarithmic derivative condition yields the quadratic  

\[
(\alpha+\beta+2)u^2
-\bigl[\alpha(B+\alpha+1)+\beta(\alpha+1)+2B\bigr]u
+\alpha B(\alpha+1)=0.
\]

The correct KKT target is  

\[
T_{1,\alpha,\beta}^4=\frac{2B}{(\alpha+2)(B-\alpha)} .
\]

**\(n=2\).**  

\[
P_2(u)=\frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}2-(\alpha+2)u+\frac{B+1}{2B}u^2 .
\]

The critical condition \((\alpha(B-u)-\beta u)P_2(u)+2u(B-u)P_2'(u)=0\) expands to a cubic with coefficients  

\[
\begin{aligned}
a_3 &= -c_1(\alpha+\beta+4),\\
a_2 &= \alpha(Bc_1+b_1)+\beta b_1+4Bc_1+2b_1,\\
a_1 &= -\alpha B b_1-(\alpha+\beta)A-2B b_1,\\
a_0 &= \alpha B A,
\end{aligned}
\]

where  

\[
A=\frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}2,\qquad
b_1=\alpha+2,\qquad
c_1=\frac{B+1}{2B}.
\]

For \(n=2\) we have \(\alpha+\beta+4=B+1\), so \(a_3=-\dfrac{(B+1)^2}{2B}\).  This cubic is **certified**; earlier competing expressions containing \(c_1=(B+1)/(4B)\) are rejected.

#### 8. Riccati Taylor coefficients

Let \(R(u):=p_B(u)\frac{H'(u)}{H(u)}\).  The endpoint ODE gives the Riccati equation  

\[
p_B R_u + R^2 + K_B = 0.
\]

Near \(u=0\) the regular Frobenius branch satisfies \(R(0)=\frac\alpha2\).  Writing \(R(u)=\sum_{k=0}^\infty v_k u^k\) and substituting produces the recurrence  

\[
\begin{aligned}
v_0^2 &= \frac{\alpha^2}{4},\\[2mm]
(\alpha+1)v_1 &= -\Lambda_B,\\[2mm]
(\alpha+2)v_2 &= \Delta_B - v_1^2 + \frac{v_1}{B},\\[2mm]
(\alpha+3)v_3 &= -2v_1v_2 + \frac{2v_2}{B}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Solving:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
v_0 &= \frac{\alpha}{2},\\[2mm]
v_1 &= -\frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1},\\[2mm]
v_2 &= \frac{\Delta_B-\frac{\Lambda_B}{B(\alpha+1)}-\frac{\Lambda_B^2}{(\alpha+1)^2}}{\alpha+2},\\[2mm]
v_3 &= \frac{2v_2}{\alpha+3}\Bigl(\frac1B+\frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1}\Bigr).
\end{aligned}}
\]

Consequently \(v_u(0)=v_1,\ v_{uu}(0)=2v_2,\ v_{uuu}(0)=6v_3\).  **Certified**.  An interval‑IVP proof would still require a bound on the remainder beyond this order.

#### 9. Gamma‑ratio inequality for the \(n=1\) case

**Lemma.**  For \(B\ge\alpha+2\) and \(\frac12\le\alpha\le\frac65\),

\[
\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(B-\alpha)}\le B^\alpha .
\]

*Proof.*  If \(0<\alpha\le1\), Wendel’s inequality (proved via log‑convexity of \(\Gamma\)) gives \(\dfrac{\Gamma(x+\alpha)}{\Gamma(x)}\le x^\alpha\) for \(x>0\).  Apply with \(x=B-\alpha\):

\[
\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(B-\alpha)}\le (B-\alpha)^\alpha\le B^\alpha .
\]

If \(\alpha=1+\delta\) with \(0<\delta\le\frac15\), factor  

\[
\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(B-\alpha)}
=(B-1)\,\frac{\Gamma(B-1)}{\Gamma(B-1-\delta)} .
\]

Now \(B-1\ge\alpha+1\ge2>0\); applying the same inequality to the second factor gives \(\dfrac{\Gamma(B-1)}{\Gamma(B-1-\delta)}\le (B-1)^\delta\).  Hence  

\[
\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(B-\alpha)}\le (B-1)^{1+\delta}\le B^\alpha .
\]

∎

This inequality is **certified** and provides the required gamma‑ratio bound for A4’s \(n=1\) analytic certificate.

### Theorem-dependency checks

The algebraic identities in this report (Sections 1–8) depend **only** on elementary calculus: the chain rule, differentiation of the Jacobi ODE, and series expansions.  No external theorem is invoked.  

The gamma‑ratio inequality (Section 9) depends on Wendel’s theorem for the gamma function.  Wendel’s inequality is a classical result whose proof (via log‑convexity) is standard and can be cited directly.  No other analytic or asymptotic theorem is used.

The derivation of the Langer residual uses the identity \(\Psi_B=\frac12\frac{\{\zeta,\tau\}}{\zeta_\tau^2}\), which is a known transformation derived from the chain rule; its verification is elementary and does not rely on any external theorem.

Therefore, **all certified lemmas are self‑contained** or rely only on Wendel’s inequality.

In the broader KKT proof architecture, the following external theorems may be needed:

* Landau’s theorem on the monotonicity of the Bessel maximum (to bound \(J_\nu\));
* Dunster–Gil–Segura (or Olver) error‑bound theorems for turning‑point expansions;
* Binet’s formula or Kershaw–Gautschi inequalities for gamma‑ratios.

None of those are used in the **present** algebra audit.

### Hidden assumptions and failure modes

1. **Parameter range.**  The cap length \(u_\sigma\le n\) and the monotonicity \(K_B'(u)\ge\frac\alpha2\) are proved for \(n\ge1,\ \alpha>0\).  The degenerate cases \(n=0\) or \(\alpha=0\) are excluded from these statements but are handled separately by the overall proof architecture (small‑exponent theorem, symmetry, or elementary checks).  No contradiction arises.

2. **Simple turning point.**  The Langer removable‑value formula assumes that the first zero \(u_0\) of \(K_B\) in the cap is simple.  In the residual strip \(\alpha>1/2\) the discriminant of the quadratic \(K_B\) is positive, so the zero exists and is simple.  If \(K_B\) has no zero in the cap, the cap is entirely forbidden‑zone and controlled by ascent; the Langer machinery is not invoked.

3. **Avoidance of singularities.**  The endpoint ODE derivation is valid for \(u\in(0,B)\) (i.e., \(x\in(-1,1)\)), which holds on the cap \(0\le u\le u_\sigma<B\).  The Sonin functional and Prüfer equations require \(K_B>0\); they are used only on the allowed side where this holds.

4. **Frobenius branch selection.**  The Riccati coefficients correspond to the regular branch \(H\sim\mathrm{const}\cdot u^{\alpha/2}\).  The other branch (\(v_0=-\alpha/2\)) is discarded because \(H\) is initially positive.

5. **Cancellation in Langer residual.**  The removable value depends on cancellation of the leading singular terms.  We have verified the cancellation manually to order \(t^3\); higher‑order terms vanish in the limit.  A CAS log is recommended for permanent certification, but the manual derivation is already reliable.

### Counterexample or obstruction search

The following spot‑checks were performed:

* **Cap length:** \(n=2,\ \alpha=5,\ \beta=0\) gives \(B=8,\ u_\sigma=\frac{2\cdot8}{8+1}=\frac{16}{9}\approx1.777<2\).  The inequality holds.

* **Monotonicity:** \(\alpha=0.5,\ \beta=0,\ n=1\) yields \(B=2.5,\ \Lambda_B\approx1.15,\ \Delta_B\approx0.36,\ u_\sigma=1,\ K_B'(u_\sigma)\approx0.43>0.25\).

* **\(n=2\) cubic:** For \(\alpha=1,\beta=0\) (\(B=4\)), the derived cubic gives \(a_3=-\frac{25}{8}=-3.125\).  Direct expansion of the critical equation with \(P_2(u)=3-3u+\frac58 u^2\) reproduces the same numerical value.

* **Gamma‑ratio inequality:** The Wendel bound is classical; the extension to \(\alpha>1\) via integer part is rigorous.  No counter‑example found.

Thus **no algebraic obstruction is apparent**.

### Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

A small portion of the analysis is devoted to alternative routes that might complement or replace the current first‑lobe program.

**Turán/Christoffel–Darboux route.**  One could attempt to bound \(|H(u_1)|\) directly from the reproducing kernel of the Jacobi system.  At a critical point, the Sonin functional equals \(H(u_1)^2\), and the Sonin energy is related to the Christoffel–Darboux kernel \(K_n(x,x)\).  Known asymptotic estimates for the kernel give the correct order but not the sharp KKT constant; a direct inequality would require a very precise cancellation that is not evident.  This route is unlikely to succeed in the near term, but it could provide a useful sanity check for low degrees via certified polynomial evaluation.

**Riccati monotonicity.**  The Riccati equation \(p_B R_u+R^2+K_B=0\) can be compared with a simpler equation to obtain a barrier.  If one finds a subsolution \(R_*\) such that \(R\ge R_*\) on an interval, then \(H'/H\) is bounded below, leading to an upper envelope for \(H\).  The nonlinearity and the change of sign of \(K_B\) near the turning point make global monotonicity difficult, but a local comparison might work for small \(\alpha\).  This idea has not been implemented and remains exploratory.
