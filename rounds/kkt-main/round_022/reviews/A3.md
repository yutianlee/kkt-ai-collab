## Claims that need proof

Several central claims are either expressed only as $O(\cdot)$ without constants, rely on uninstantiated external theorems, or have not yet been bounded numerically.

**A1 – Bounds for $E_0,G_0,E_{\mathrm{prop}}$.** The full Langer ratio is now expressed entirely through explicit integrals, but no inequality for any of them has been supplied. Until an upper bound for $E_0+E_{\mathrm{prop}}$ and a bound for $G_0$ are produced (even on a single non‑trivial box), the Langer track remains a framework, not a proof.

**A1 – Asymptotic $\Psi_B(\zeta)=5/(16\zeta^2)+O(|\zeta|^{-7/2})$ for $\zeta\to-\infty$.** The decay is stated but not derived; the order of the remainder and its coefficient are needed for the finiteness of $E_0,G_0$. A symbolic expansion of $\Psi_B$ in powers of $\zeta$ is a small but mandatory step for certifying the Volterra integrals.

**A1 – Safety of a fixed cut $\zeta_c=1$.** The numerical spot‑check shows $\zeta_1-1\approx0.007$. A certified proof cannot rely on $\zeta_c=1$ without a rigorous lower bound for $\zeta_1$, or without switching to an adaptive cut like $\zeta_c=0.9$. This is a concrete definitional gap.

**A2 – Explicit finite constants for the rational‑Bessel variation.** The derivation gives

$$
\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}\le\frac{\pi\Delta_{\max}}{8\Lambda_B^2}\int_0^{x_1}x^3|J_\alpha Y_\alpha|\,dx.
$$

The pre‑transition part is bounded by $\frac{2\alpha^3}{3\pi}$ using Nicholson’s inequality, which is rigorous. The transition‑zone contribution is estimated as $O(\alpha^{8/3})$ but lacks a numerical constant. To convert the scaling into a theorem, the exact supremum (or interval enclosure) of the transition integral must be supplied. The statement $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}=O(\alpha^3/n^2)$ is therefore a derived scaling, not a proved inequality with numbers.

**A2 – Sign‑definiteness and kernel simplification.** The argument that the Volterra kernel reduces to $|W_1W_2|$ depends on the sign of $W_2(z_1)$ and the interlacing of zeros for the actual solution $H$ (not just the reference $J_\alpha$). While the reasoning is plausible, a perturbation lemma showing that the first critical point of $H$ precedes the first zero of $Y_\alpha$—or an equivalent condition—has not been supplied. This gap is not fatal but must be closed before the variation integral can be used as a strict bound.

**A2 – Overlap threshold with an actual constant $C$.** Setting $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}\le\alpha/(4n)$ to derive $\alpha\le C\sqrt{n}$ is a heuristic. Even if the scaling is correct, the constant $C_{\mathrm{Bess}}$ that multiplies $\alpha^3/n^2$ is unknown, so no concrete $C$ can be stated. Moreover, the Bessel maximum and gamma‑ratio envelope would also consume margin; the overlap condition must account for all multiplicative factors, not just the variation.

**A4 – Executed $n=2$ interval certificate.** A precise plan is laid out, but no interval computation has been performed. The bound for $n=2$ is therefore still open. The same holds for the Riccati IVP: the Taylor data are provided, but no validated integration has been run.

**A1/A2 – DGS/Olver weighted error bounds.** Both tracks allude to Dunster–Gil–Segura or Olver theorems, but neither agent instantiates the precise weight functions, hypotheses, or constants for the KKT residual $\Psi_B$. The current allowed‑side bound (A1) uses the crude $\mathfrak m^2$ weight, which is knowingly too weak on the forbidden side. The transition to DGS weights remains an open task.

**General – Uniform gamma‑ratio envelope for $M_{n,\alpha,B}$.** The Wendel argument covers only the limited range needed for $n=1$. For the analytic tracks, a rigorous one‑sided bound on $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ (e.g. $M_{n,\alpha,B}\le 1+ C/(n+1)$) across the residual strip is still missing. The leading entropy negativity proved by A3 is only asymptotic and does not provide finite‑$n$ control.

These unproved claims are the precise edges of the current knowledge. They must be the primary targets of Round 23.

## Possible errors or hidden assumptions

**A2 – Unqualified extension of Nicholson’s inequality.** The bound $|J_\alpha(x)Y_\alpha(x)|\le(\pi\sqrt{\alpha^2-x^2})^{-1}$ is valid only for $x<\alpha$. Pushing the integral to $x=\alpha$ is a harmless over‑estimate, but the subsequent transition‑zone estimate uses an Airy scaling without tracking the error from the Nicholson envelope at $x=\alpha-O(\alpha^{1/3})$. The final expression $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}\le\frac{\Delta_{\max}}{12\Lambda_B^2}\alpha^3+O(\alpha^{8/3}/n^2)$ mixes rigorous and heuristic parts; the boundary between them is not clearly marked. A reader might mistake the $O$ term as a proved bound.

**A2 – Overlap threshold interpretation.** Equating $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}$ directly to the KKT slack $\alpha/(4n)$ assumes that all other factors (Bessel maximum, gamma normalization, exponential amplification) combine to a number near $1$. This is not justified. The slack available for the variation is not $\alpha/(4n)$ but rather

$$
\ln\bigl(T_{n,\alpha,\beta}/(\text{Bessel max}\times\text{gamma norm})\bigr),
$$

which may be much smaller or even negative if the gamma normalization exceeds $1$. A safe overlap threshold must incorporate all these constants.

**A1 – The cut $\zeta_c$ is assumed to lie in $(0,j_{\operatorname{Ai},1})$ and to be strictly less than $\zeta_1$.** The numerical spot‑check shows $\zeta_1-1\approx0.007$; a slightly different box could push $\zeta_1$ below $1$. If $\zeta_1\le\zeta_c$ anywhere, the allowed‑side propagation interval $[\zeta_c,\zeta_1]$ would be empty and the bound invalid. The proof must either prove a uniform positive lower bound for $\zeta_1-\zeta_c$ or replace the fixed cut by an adaptive one.

**A1 – The “removable value” at $\zeta=0$ is not used; the actual propagation avoids the turning point via the cut.** This is not an error, but the integrals $E_0,E_{\mathrm{prop}}$ may receive large contributions from near the turning point if the cut is too small. Without an explicit bound for $\Psi_B$ near $\zeta=0$ (which can be obtained from the removable value plus a Taylor remainder), the cut integral may be underestimated or overestimated.

**A4 – The trigamma bound in the convexity proof is given as a numeric approximation $-\psi'(2.5)\ge -0.4905$, etc., without a formal inequality.** While the proof is human‑readable and convincing, a formally certified lemma would require a rigorous interval enclosure for $\psi'$ (e.g. via the series representation). This is a minor gap that can be easily filled.

**General – The $K_B+1/4$ sign is repeatedly flagged as settled, but none of the Round 22 outputs contains a fresh derivation of the Liouville normal form sign.** They rely on earlier certified algebra, which is acceptable.

**A2 – The claim that $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}$ is $O(\alpha^3/n^2)$ is presented as a “derived‑under‑assumptions” result, but at times the text slips into treating it as a firm scaling.** In a rigorous audit, every $O$ statement must be accompanied by an explicit constant or at least a verifiable computable bound. The same applies to A1’s $O(|\zeta|^{-7/2})$.

**A4 – The assumption $(1-u/B)^\beta\le 1$ is used; it is true because $u/B\le1/(\alpha+2)\le1/2.5=0.4$, so the factor is indeed $\le1$.** No error.

**A2 – The kernel sign argument relies on $-c_1W_1(s)-W_2(s)$ being positive.** A2 gives a geometric justification using $f(x)=c_1J_\alpha(x)+Y_\alpha(x)$, with $f(x_1)=0$ and $f(x)<0$ on $(0,x_1)$. This is correct if $c_1=-W_2(z_1)/W_1(z_1)$ and $x_1$ is the first peak of the **actual solution** $H$. However, the argument uses properties of the **reference** Bessel functions $J_\alpha,Y_\alpha$, not the perturbed $H$. The peak $x_1$ is taken as the first peak of $J_\alpha$, which is a good approximation, but the sign of $W_2(z_1)$ for the actual $H$ may differ if the perturbation shifts the peak or introduces a zero of $W_2$ earlier. A rigorous perturbation bound is needed to guarantee that the sign structure is preserved. This is a genuine hidden assumption that could affect the strictness of the kernel reduction.

Most of these items are minor or easily corrected; none appear to invalidate the overall strategy. The most dangerous hidden assumption is the reliance on unproven sign‑regularity for the actual solution in A2’s Volterra kernel.

## Suggested synthesis

The three outputs are naturally complementary. The synthesis should preserve the regime split that has now become unavoidable:

* **Track C (low‑degree analytic/interval):** Partially closed. $n=1$ is proved; $n=2$ has a corrected cubic and a concrete interval plan.  
* **Track B (small‑$\alpha$ rational‑Bessel/Riccati):** Formulated with an exact residual and a Volterra variation expressed in terms of Bessel cross‑products. The missing constants are identified.  
* **Track A (bulk Langer/Airy):** Reduced to bounding a handful of explicit integrals ($E_0,G_0,E_{\mathrm{prop}}$) plus the initial connection constant $\mathfrak C_-$ and a safe cut.

The next stage of synthesis should **not** attempt to merge these into a single monolithic proof. Instead, the Round 23 judge should direct each agent to produce the missing numbers.

A combined picture is emerging:

- For very small $\alpha$ (say $\alpha\le C\sqrt{n}$ after $C$ is measured), the rational‑Bessel track likely provides a clean, low‑residual certificate. The residual $\Delta Q$ is uniformly small, and the Volterra integral can be bounded rigorously once the transition‑zone constants are computed.
- For bulk $\alpha$ (say $\alpha\ge C\sqrt{n}$), the Langer track must be used. A1’s new recessive connection formula turns the problem into a finite number of explicit integrals. The first priority is to bound these integrals on a representative hard box to determine whether the Langer ratio can actually be forced below $1$.
- For low $n$, the $n=1$ result is proved; $n=2$ is algebraically ready and awaits execution. The Riccati IVP, with the now‑certified Taylor data, may be the simplest way to handle $n=2$ and perhaps $n=3$ without heavy polynomial algebra.

The conditional theorems (A1’s full Langer ratio, A2’s rational‑Bessel variation bound) should be entered into the lemma bank as “conditional with open constants”. A4’s $n=1$ proof should be entered as “certified”. The uninstantiated DGS/Olver weights remain open.

## Research strategy adjustments

The strategy from Round 21 is still sound, but Round 22 allows a sharper allocation.

1. **Make Track A measurable, not schematic.** A1’s conditional ratio is now a collection of named integrals. The task of the next round is to bound those integrals on a non‑trivial parameter box, preferably the judge’s hard box $(n=100,\;45\le\alpha\le50,\;0.05\le\theta\le0.10)$, and to determine whether a uniform bound exists. The proof architecture is sufficiently detailed; the next step is execution.

2. **Develop Track B into a quantitative competitor.** A2’s Volterra integral is a concrete definite integral over known functions. The missing constants can be obtained by rigorous interval quadrature (or even a careful analytic bound). A4 (or A2) should compute

$$
C_{\mathrm{Bess}} = \sup_{\alpha\in[0.5,50]} \alpha^{-3}\int_0^{x_1}x^3|J_\alpha(x)Y_\alpha(x)|\,dx
$$

and the analogous constant for the transition region. Once $C_{\mathrm{Bess}}$ is known, the variation bound becomes explicit: $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}\le C_{\mathrm{Bess}}\frac{\Delta_{\max}}{12}\frac{\alpha^3}{\Lambda_B^2}$. Then the overlap threshold can be chosen by comparing this number with the KKT slack.

3. **Prioritize the gamma‑ratio envelope.** The uniform bound on $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ is now the single most important missing piece that affects both analytic tracks. A3 (or A2/A4) must apply Binet’s formula with explicit remainders to prove $M_{n,\alpha,B}\le 1+ C/(n+1)$ (or a similar inequality) for the residual strip. Without this, the analytic tracks cannot translate variation bounds into amplitude bounds.

4. **Execute the $n=2$ certificate.** Whether by Riccati IVP or by interval arithmetic on the cubic, a certified proof for $n=2$ must be produced. The Riccati route, with the already‑derived Taylor data, may be simpler and more extensible to small $n$.

5. **Do not declare routes “impossible” without rigorous constants.** The obstruction analyses (Laguerre‑face stagnation, Airy‑handoff instability) are valuable warnings, but they do not rule out modified versions of the same routes. The judge should avoid language like “the Langer route is falsified” until a concrete counterexample box with rigorously computed constants shows $\mathcal R_{\mathrm{Lang}}^{\mathrm{full}}>1$.

6. **Exploratory allocation (20%).** The Riccati IVP deserves a serious test: can validated integration of $R(u)$ from a small step $\varepsilon$ to the first zero $u_1$ be performed with outward rounding for $n=2,3$? If successful, the Riccati method could become the primary computational engine for low degrees, bypassing polynomial root isolation. The semi‑discrete $\beta\in\mathbb N_0$ case remains a secondary target; no theorem exploits integrality, so it should not distract from the main tracks.

7. **Falsification criteria.** The Langer track is falsified on a parameter box if an interval computation yields $\mathcal R_{\mathrm{Lang}}^{\mathrm{full}}>1$. The rational‑Bessel track is falsified on a box if $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}}$ exceeds the available slack after including the Bessel maximum and gamma envelope. Every agent should aim to produce either a positive bound or a clear falsification box.
