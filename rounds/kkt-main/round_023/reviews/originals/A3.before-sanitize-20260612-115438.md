## 1. Most valuable input from others

The three Stage‑A outputs contain pieces that, once corrected and combined, can form a viable route toward a first‑lobe amplitude theorem.

**A2 – Rational‑coordinate Volterra framework**  
A2 systematically transforms the endpoint ODE  

\[
(p_BH')'+q_BH=0,\qquad p_B(u)=u(1-u/B),
\]

into the rational variable  

\[
z=\frac{Bu}{B-u},\qquad u=\frac{Bz}{B+z},
\]

and obtains the normal form  

\[
Y''+\Bigl(\frac{\Lambda_B}{z}+\frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2}+\Delta Q(z)\Bigr)Y=0,\qquad
Y=z^{1/2}H,
\]

with a residual  

\[
\Delta Q(z)=-\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z}-\frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2}
\]

that is uniformly bounded by $2.25$ and has no hard‑edge singularity.  
The construction of the Volterra integral equation and the **kernel‑sign audit** (using the Wronskian monotonicity $\dfrac{d}{dt}(Y_\alpha/J_\alpha)=\dfrac{2}{\pi t J_\alpha^2}>0$ to avoid absolute‑value inflation) are the first concrete attempts to control the perturbation amplitude without crude inflation.  Even though the variation bound is not yet fully rigorous, the algebraic steps are largely correct and provide a clear structure.

**A4 – Corrected Bessel‑maximum certificate**  
A4 reduces the problem to $\nu=1/2$ (via an assumed monotonicity theorem) and performs an outward‑rounded interval computation of the first positive maximum of $J_{1/2}$.  The result  

\[
\sup_{t>0} J_{1/2}(t)\in[0.679417,\,0.679418]
\]

is rigorous for $\nu=1/2$; the missing piece is a precise citation for the monotonicity of the Bessel maximum in the order.

**A1 – Coarse gamma‑ratio lemma**  
The inequality  

\[
\frac{\Gamma(x+\alpha)}{\Gamma(x)}<(x+\alpha)^\alpha\qquad(x>0,\;\alpha>0),
\]

proved via $\psi(y)<\log y$, is a clean elementary bound that can help control the normalisation factor $M_{n,\alpha,B}$.

**A4 – Structural observation**  
The idea that a negative discriminant for the quadratic whose roots are the critical points of the $n=1$ cap function would trivialise the endpoint bound (no stationary point inside the cap) is an important insight, even though the quadratic itself must be corrected.

These four contributions are the building blocks; the remaining gaps (§3 and §4) must be filled before any theorem‑level claim can be certified.

## 2. Claims that look correct

After algebraic verification, the following claims appear to be correct and could be admitted into the lemma bank once missing citations are supplied.

* **A2 – Rational‑coordinate ODE and invariant identity**  
  The transformation  

  \[
  z=\frac{Bu}{B-u},\qquad u=\frac{Bz}{B+z},\qquad
  \frac{du}{dz}=\frac{B^2}{(B+z)^2},
  \]

  together with $p_B(u)= \dfrac{B^2z}{(B+z)^2}$, gives $p_B\frac{dz}{du}=z$, so  

  \[
  (p_BH_u')_u' = \frac{(B+z)^2}{B^2}(zH_z')_z'.
  \]

  Hence the endpoint ODE becomes  

  \[
  (zH_z')_z' + \frac{B^2}{(B+z)^2}\,q_B\,H=0.
  \]

  The identity $z\,\widehat{q}_B(z)=K_B(u(z))$ (where $K_B=p_Bq_B$) is exact, and A2’s decomposition of $K_B/z^2$ into a Bessel‑core plus $\Delta Q(z)$ is algebraically correct.

* **A2 – Uniform residual bound**  
  $\Delta Q(z)$ is strictly negative.  Using the previously certified bounds $\Lambda_B/B<1$ and $\Delta_B\le 1.25$, one obtains  

  \[
  |\Delta Q(z)|\le \frac{\Lambda_B}{B}+\Delta_B\le 2.25\qquad(z\ge0).
  \]

  This bound remains finite in the Laguerre limit $\theta\to0$.

* **A2 – Volterra kernel structure**  
  With $W_1(z)=\sqrt{z}\,J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})$, $W_2(z)=\sqrt{z}\,Y_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})$, the Wronskian is $W(W_1,W_2)=1/\pi$, and the variation‑of‑parameters kernel is correctly written.

* **A4 – Scalar Bessel maximum for $\nu=1/2$**  
  Solving $\tan t=2t$ and evaluating $J_{1/2}(t)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}\sin t$ with outward‑rounded interval arithmetic yields $J_{1/2}(t_0)<0.6795$.  The computation is elementary and reproducible; if Landau’s monotonicity (or an equivalent reference) is accepted, the bound $B_*<0.680$ holds for all $\nu\ge 1/2$.

* **A4 – Volterra singularity cancellation**  
  The product $Y_\alpha(s)H(s)$ remains bounded near $s=0$ because the Neumann singularity is balanced by the Frobenius growth $H(s)\sim s^\alpha$.  This is a classical observation and correct.

* **A1 – Lemma 1 (gamma‑ratio coarse bound)**  
  The proof via $\psi(y)<\log y$ is rigorous.

* **General – Hypergeometric representation**  
  The identity  

  \[
  P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr)
  =\frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}\,{}_2F_1\Bigl(-n,B;\alpha+1;\frac{u}{B}\Bigr)
  \]

  (with $B=n+\alpha+\beta+1$) is standard and correct; it is the basis for exact critical‑point equations at low degrees.

These fragments can be assembled into a proof if the missing estimates and corrections detailed below are supplied.

## 3. Claims that need proof

Several central claims are either unproved, rely on hidden assumptions, or contain algebraic mistakes that invalidate them in their present form.

### 3.1 A1 – The entire $n=1$ residual endpoint theorem (invalid as written)

A1 writes  

\[
H_1(u)^4 \le
\frac{2\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+2)}{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(\beta+1)}\,
\frac{1}{B^\alpha}\,
u^\alpha\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr)^{\!\beta}
(\alpha+1-u)^4 .
\]

The actual KKT‑normalised function for $n=1$, evaluated at $x=1-2u/B$, is  

\[
g_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}\!\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr)
=
\sqrt{\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(\alpha+2)\Gamma(\beta+2)}}\,
\Bigl(\frac{u}{B}\Bigr)^{\!\alpha/2}
\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr)^{\!\beta/2}
(\alpha+1-u) .
\]

Hence the exact fourth power is  

\[
H_1(u)^4 =
\Bigl(\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(\alpha+2)\Gamma(\beta+2)}\Bigr)^{\!2}
\Bigl(\frac{u}{B}\Bigr)^{\!2\alpha}
\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr)^{\!2\beta}
(\alpha+1-u)^4 .
\]

A1’s inequality uses different Gamma factors, a single $\Gamma(B)$ (not squared), and exponents $\alpha,\beta$ instead of $2\alpha,2\beta$.  No derivation is given that would relate the true expression to the claimed envelope; the step is completely unsupported.  Consequently, the whole $n=1$ “theorem” must be considered unproved and likely false in its current form.  A1’s confidence $0.97$ is unjustified.

**Action required:** A1 must restart from the correct $g_1$ normalisation and either deliver a valid bound or admit that the $n=1$ case remains open.

### 3.2 A2 – The explicit variation bound $\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{Bess}} \le \frac{3}{16}\frac{\alpha^3}{\Lambda_B^2}$ and the threshold $\alpha\le 1.04\,n^{2/3}$

A2 splits the Volterra variation integral  

\[
\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{Bess}} \le \frac{\pi Q_{\max}}{8\Lambda_B^2}
\int_0^{t_1} t^3|J_\alpha(t)Y_\alpha(t)|\,dt
\]

into a pre‑transition piece $[0,\alpha]$ bounded by Watson’s product inequality, and a transition tail $[\alpha,t_1]$ whose contribution is **only estimated as $\mathcal{O}(\alpha^{8/3})$** using Airy‑function asymptotics.  No concrete constant for the tail is stated, and no named turning‑point theorem with explicit error bounds (e.g., Olver 1974, Chapter 11; Dunster 1990) is invoked.  Until the integral $\int_\alpha^{t_1} t^3|J_\alpha Y_\alpha|\,dt$ is rigorously bounded with finite numbers, the variation estimate remains a conjecture.  The provisional threshold $\alpha\le 1.04\,n^{2/3}$ is therefore **not a certified design parameter** for the proof strategy.

**Action required:** A2 must either bound the tail explicitly or construct a different bound that does not rely on untamed asymptotics.

### 3.3 A2 – Kernel positivity under the true perturbed critical point

The derivation shows that the relative Volterra kernel $\mathcal{W}(z,s)$ is positive when the integration endpoint $z_1$ is the first stationary point of the **reference** Bessel function.  The Gronwall estimate is then evaluated at that point.  However, the amplitude that must be controlled is $|H(u_1)|$ where $u_1$ is the first critical point of the **perturbed** solution $H$.  The two points may differ because of the residual $\Delta Q(z)$.  A2 provides no bound on this shift, nor an argument that the amplitude at the reference point dominates that at the true perturbed point.  The lemma is incomplete.

**Action required:** A2 must prove a lemma that either bounds the phase shift or connects the amplitude at the reference point to the amplitude at the perturbed critical point (e.g., via Sonin monotonicity).

### 3.4 A4 – $n=1$ critical‑point quadratic (contains an algebraic mistake)

A4 writes  

\[
P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}(1-2v) = \alpha+1 - (B+1)v \qquad (v=u/B).
\]

The correct Jacobi polynomial at $x=1-2u/B$ is  

\[
P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}\!\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr) = \alpha+1 - u = \alpha+1 - Bv .
\]

Thus the coefficient of $v$ is $B$, not $B+1$.  The entire quadratic, its discriminant, the $\beta=0$ factorisation, and all numerical examples in §4 are incorrect.  The claim that a negative discriminant trivialises the cap may still be true after correction, but it must be recomputed from scratch.

**Action required:** A4 must re‑derive the quadratic with the correct coefficient; A3 should audit the corrected algebra.

### 3.5 A4 – $n=2$ cubic coefficients (unverified, likely parameter error)

A4 uses the expansion  

\[
P_2^{(\alpha,\beta)}(1-2v) = \frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}{2}
{}_2F_1(-2, B+1; \alpha+1; v).
\]

The standard formula is  

\[
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x) = \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}
{}_2F_1\!\Bigl(-n,\, n+\alpha+\beta+1; \,\alpha+1; \,\frac{1-x}{2}\Bigr).
\]

For $n=2$ and $x=1-2v$, the second hypergeometric parameter is $B=n+\alpha+\beta+1$, **not** $B+1$.  Hence the polynomial coefficients $E,F,G$ reported by A4 are suspect.  The cubic $c_3 v^3 + c_2 v^2 + c_1 v + c_0 = 0$ must be re‑derived with the correct ${}_2F_1$ and independently verified before it can be used for interval root isolation.

**Action required:** A4 and A3 must separately derive the correct cubic and cross‑check.

### 3.6 Gamma‑ratio bound with explicit $C_\Gamma$

A4 sketches a Robbins‑bracket argument but **does not compute a concrete constant** $C_\Gamma$ such that  

\[
M_{n,\alpha,B} \le 1 + \frac{C_\Gamma}{n+1}.
\]

For any analytic first‑lobe estimate that compares the perturbed solution to the Bessel reference, an explicit numeric bound on $M$ is indispensable.  Without it, the slack available for the Volterra variation (or any other perturbation) cannot be quantified.

**Action required:** A1 must compute $C_\Gamma$ using Robbins’ bounds and a compact parameter box, delivering a fully explicit inequality.

### 3.7 Landau’s Bessel‑maximum monotonicity

A4 claims that $\max_{t>0} |J_\nu(t)|$ is strictly decreasing for $\nu\ge 1/2$, attributing this to Landau.  No precise theorem statement or verifiable source is provided.  The interval computation therefore certifies $B_*<0.680$ **only** for $\nu=1/2$; the extension to all $\nu\ge 1/2$ is an unverified assumption.

**Action required:** A1/A2 must supply the exact reference and verify the hypotheses, or produce a self‑contained elementary bound that is sufficient for all $\nu\ge 1/2$.

### 3.8 Watson’s product bound

The inequality  

\[
|J_\alpha(x)Y_\alpha(x)| \le \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{\alpha^2-x^2}}\qquad(0\le x<\alpha)
\]

is stated without a precise reference.  While such bounds are known, the exact range of validity (all $\alpha\ge 1/2$?) must be verified from a primary source (e.g., Watson’s *Treatise*, §13.5).  Until a theorem statement with page or equation number is supplied, the bound cannot be used inside a certified lemma.

**Action required:** A1/A2 must retrieve the exact theorem and confirm that its hypotheses cover the needed parameter regime.

## 4. Possible errors or hidden assumptions

Beyond the claims that need proof, the following systemic issues affect the integrity of the current round.

* **A1 – Fatal normalisation mismatch**  
  Already detailed in §3.1.  This is not a minor algebraic slip; it completely severs the connection between the claimed inequality and the KKT‑normalised function.  The $n=1$ theorem must be withdrawn until a corrected derivation is supplied.

* **A4 – Structural $n=1$ error**  
  The $B+1$ mis‑identification, if uncorrected, would lead to an entirely wrong discriminant and root location, undermining the interval‑arithmetic plan for $n=1$.

* **A2 – Hidden assumption on the perturbed critical point**  
  The Gronwall estimate is evaluated at the reference Bessel stationary point, but the final bound is needed at the true perturbed critical point.  Without a bound on the phase shift, the argument is incomplete (cf. §3.3).

* **A2 – Transition‑tail asymptotics**  
  The use of Airy‑function scaling without explicit constants is a recognised obstruction in Liouville–Green theory.  The $\mathcal{O}(\alpha^{8/3})$ contribution could, depending on the constants, be large enough to consume the available slack, making the whole threshold illusory.

* **A4 – Landau monotonicity unsubstantiated**  
  The reduction to $\nu=1/2$ may be true, but an unverified citation is a gap.  If the monotonicity fails for some $\nu$, the actual global maximum might be slightly larger; a fallback bound that is provable without heavy literature might be safer.

* **Across agents – Reliance on uncertified global modules**  
  The endpoint‑cap first‑lobe reduction assumes that earlier stages (central‑contour control, weighted‑energy clearance, small‑$\alpha$ theorem for $\alpha\le 1/2$, left‑right symmetry) are rigorous.  These are working hypotheses; a final proof must eventually certify those modules.  This is not an error but a dependency that must be tracked.

* **A1’s implicit use of the gamma‑ratio lemma for $M_{n,\alpha,B}$**  
  A1 attempts to bound $H_1^4$ by a scalar envelope that appears to replace the exact Gamma‑ratio by a coarse bound; however, the exponents in the weight function $(u/B)^{2\alpha}$ versus $u^\alpha$ cannot be reconciled by any simple inequality of the form $\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(\alpha+2)\Gamma(\beta+2)} \le \text{something}$.  Unless there is a hidden step that squares the whole expression differently, the inequality is mathematically impossible as written.

## 5. Suggested synthesis

A realistic proof strategy that avoids the pitfalls identified above is:

1. **Correct the low‑degree algebra**  
   - A4 re‑derives $P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}(1-2u/B)=\alpha+1-u$ and then the quadratic for the critical points from the logarithmic derivative; this quadratic should be expressed in terms of $B$ and $\alpha$ and audited by A3.  
   - A3 independently derives the $n=2$ cubic from the correct hypergeometric expansion (with parameter $B$, not $B+1$).  The coefficients must be expressed in a machine‑checkable form.  
   - For $n=1$, where a critical point exists inside the cap, the KKT bound should be proved by a direct maximisation of the exact $g_1^4$ (or $g_1$), possibly aided by the coarse gamma‑ratio lemma for the Gamma factor and elementary calculus for the polynomial factor – **not** by the oversimplified envelope A1 gave.

2. **Rigorise the rational‑coordinate Volterra lemma**  
   - A2 must supply **explicit constants** for the transition‑tail integral, using a **named turning‑point theorem** (Olver 1974, Ch. 11; Dunster 1990) that gives uniform error bounds for the Airy approximation of $J_\alpha$ near $t=\alpha$.  
   - A2 must **bound the shift** between the reference stationary point and the true perturbed critical point.  One viable route: track the phase $\phi$ via Prüfer variables and use the monotonicity of the Sonin product to show that the amplitude cannot increase between the reference point and the true first lobe.  
   - A1 must provide the exact Watson product bound reference and verify its hypotheses.  
   - A1 must compute a concrete constant $C_\Gamma$ for $M_{n,\alpha,B}$.

3. **Ground the Bessel maximum**  
   - A1/A2 must locate a precise theorem for the monotonicity of $\max |J_\nu|$; if unavailable, prove a slightly weaker but still sufficient bound (e.g., $|J_\nu(t)|<0.6796$ for all $\nu\ge 1/2$) by elementary means such as an integral representation and a known uniform inequality.

4. **Merge low‑degree and asymptotic**  
   - If the analytic track yields an explicit $N_0$ and a uniform bound for all $n\ge N_0$, the remaining finite $n$ can be handled by corrected interval arithmetic (A4) or by case‑by‑case algebraic maximisation.  
   - The compactification $\theta=(n+\alpha+1)/B$ is useful; for fixed $n$, the domain becomes a rectangle $(\alpha,\theta)\in[1/2,\alpha_E(n)]\times[0,1]$, and the boundary $\theta=0$ (Laguerre limit) must be treated by analytic limiting formulas to avoid $1^\infty$ instability.

This synthesis avoids the two biggest dangers of the current round: unverified normalisations and heuristic asymptotics.
