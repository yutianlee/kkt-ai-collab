### Theorem: Degree-one residual endpoint certificate

Assume

$$
n=1,\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le\frac65,\qquad
\beta\ge0.

Set

$$
B=\alpha+\beta+2,
\qquad
u=\frac{B(1-x)}2,
\qquad
H_1(u)=g_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right).
$$

Then on the residual right endpoint cap

$$
0\le u\le u_\sigma=1
$$

one has

$$
H_1(u)^4<T_{1,\alpha,\beta}^4.
$$

Here

$$
T_{1,\alpha,\beta}^4
=
\frac{2B}{(\alpha+2)(B-\alpha)}
=
\frac{2(\alpha+\beta+2)}{(\alpha+2)(\beta+2)}.
$$

### Step 1: exact degree-one formula

For $n=1$,

$$
P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
=
\frac{\alpha-\beta+(\alpha+\beta+2)x}{2}.
$$

With

$$
x=1-\frac{2u}{B},
\qquad
B=\alpha+\beta+2,
$$

this becomes

$$
P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right)
=
\alpha+1-u.
$$

Therefore

$$
H_1(u)^2
=
\frac{\Gamma(B)}
{\Gamma(\alpha+2)\Gamma(\beta+2)}
\left(\frac{u}{B}\right)^\alpha
\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)^\beta
(\alpha+1-u)^2.
$$

Let

$$
x=\beta+2,
\qquad
B=x+\alpha.
$$

Then

$$
\frac{\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(\beta+2)}
=
\frac{\Gamma(x+\alpha)}{\Gamma(x)}.
$$

Using the standard inequality

$$
\psi(y)<\log y,\qquad y>0,
$$

where $\psi=\Gamma'/\Gamma$, we get

$$
\log\frac{\Gamma(x+\alpha)}{\Gamma(x)}
=
\int_0^\alpha \psi(x+t)\,dt
<
\int_0^\alpha \log(x+t)\,dt
\le
\alpha\log(x+\alpha).
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{\Gamma(x+\alpha)}{\Gamma(x)}
<
(x+\alpha)^\alpha
=
B^\alpha.
$$

Thus

$$
H_1(u)^2
<
\frac{
u^\alpha
\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)^\beta
(\alpha+1-u)^2
}{
\Gamma(\alpha+2)
}
\le
\frac{u^\alpha(\alpha+1-u)^2}{\Gamma(\alpha+2)}.
$$

The last inequality uses $\beta\ge0$ and $0\le u\le1<B$, so $0<1-u/B\le1$.

### Step 2: maximize the one-variable envelope

For fixed $\alpha$, define

$$
f_\alpha(u)=u^\alpha(\alpha+1-u)^2,
\qquad
0\le u\le1.
$$

On $(0,1)$,

$$
\frac{d}{du}\log f_\alpha(u)
=
\frac{\alpha}{u}-\frac{2}{\alpha+1-u}.
$$

The critical point is

$$
u_*=\frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{\alpha+2}.
$$

For $1/2\le\alpha\le6/5$,

$$
u_*\le1
\iff
\alpha(\alpha+1)\le\alpha+2
\iff
\alpha^2\le2,
$$

which holds. Therefore

$$
\max_{0\le u\le1} f_\alpha(u)
=
f_\alpha(u_*).
$$

Since

$$
\alpha+1-u_*
=
\frac{2(\alpha+1)}{\alpha+2},
$$

we get

$$
f_\alpha(u_*)
=
\left(\frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{\alpha+2}\right)^\alpha
\left(\frac{2(\alpha+1)}{\alpha+2}\right)^2
=
\frac{
4\alpha^\alpha(\alpha+1)^{\alpha+2}
}{
(\alpha+2)^{\alpha+2}
}.
$$

Thus

$$
H_1(u)^2
<
F(\alpha),
$$

where

$$
F(\alpha)
=
\frac{
4\alpha^\alpha(\alpha+1)^{\alpha+2}
}{
(\alpha+2)^{\alpha+2}\Gamma(\alpha+2)
}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
H_1(u)^4<E(\alpha):=F(\alpha)^2.
$$

This proves the scalar reduction.

### Step 3: prove $E(\alpha)<0.39$ on $[1/2,6/5]$

Let

$$
L(\alpha)=\log F(\alpha).
$$

Then

$$
L(\alpha)
=
\log 4+\alpha\log\alpha+(\alpha+2)\log(\alpha+1)
-(\alpha+2)\log(\alpha+2)-\log\Gamma(\alpha+2).
$$

Differentiate twice:

$$
L''(\alpha)
=
\frac1\alpha+\frac1{\alpha+1}-\frac1{\alpha+2}
-\frac1{(\alpha+1)^2}
-\psi'(\alpha+2).
$$

Use the trigamma series

$$
\psi'(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac1{(x+k)^2}.
$$

For $x>1$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac1{(x+k)^2}
<
\int_{x-1}^{\infty}\frac{dt}{t^2}
=
\frac1{x-1}.
$$

Taking $x=\alpha+2$ gives

$$
\psi'(\alpha+2)<\frac1{\alpha+1}.
$$

Therefore

$$
L''(\alpha)
>
\frac1\alpha-\frac1{\alpha+2}-\frac1{(\alpha+1)^2}
=
\frac{2}{\alpha(\alpha+2)}-\frac1{(\alpha+1)^2}.
$$

The last expression is positive because

$$
\frac{2}{\alpha(\alpha+2)}>\frac1{(\alpha+1)^2}
\iff
2(\alpha+1)^2>\alpha(\alpha+2)
\iff
\alpha^2+2\alpha+2>0.
$$

Hence

$$
L''(\alpha)>0
$$

on $[1/2,6/5]$. So $L$ is convex. A convex function on a compact interval attains its maximum at an endpoint. Therefore

$$
F(\alpha)
\le
\max\left\{F\left(\frac12\right),F\left(\frac65\right)\right\}.
$$

E(\alpha)=F(\alpha)^2<0.39

$$

The proof gives $H_1^4<0.39$, so there is no boundary problem.

### $\alpha=1/2$ boundary

At $\alpha=1/2$,

No equality case is expected in the residual $n=1$ proof. The gamma-ratio step is strict because $\psi(y)<\log y$, and the final comparison has large strict margin.

## Obstruction lessons for higher degree

The $n=1$ success does not materially weaken the arbitrary-$n$ gap. It works because:
- the polynomial has one affine factor;
- the cap is exactly $0\le u\le1$;
- the gamma-ratio bound cancels $B^\alpha$ cleanly;
- the remaining $u$ maximization is one-dimensional and explicit.

For $n=2$, the polynomial is quadratic and the first-critical equation is cubic. The same scalar collapse is not expected without root isolation.

## Alternative 1: corrected-cubic interval root isolation for $n=2$

This should be the primary next finite-degree route.
