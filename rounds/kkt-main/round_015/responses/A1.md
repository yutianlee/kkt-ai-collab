Detailed reasoning:

Formal theorem statement for the lemma bank:

Theorem L15.1: Right endpoint cap and first-lobe reduction.

Hypotheses.

Let

$$
n\ge1,\qquad \alpha>\frac12,\qquad \beta\ge0,
$$

and set

$$
B=n+\alpha+\beta+1,\qquad
H(u)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right).
$$

Assume the global imported modules have already reduced the problem to the right endpoint residual cap. Explicitly, we import:

- central branch-safe contour control at and inside the interface $x=\sigma$;
- small right-endpoint exponent control for $0\le\alpha\le1/2$;
- weighted-energy clearance outside the residual strip;
- left-right symmetry under $(\alpha,\beta,x)\mapsto(\beta,\alpha,-x)$.

Let

$$
\sigma=\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n}.
$$

The cap endpoint corresponding to $x=\sigma$ is

$$
u_\sigma=\frac{B(1-\sigma)}2
=
\frac{nB}{B+n-1}.
$$

Then

$$
u_\sigma\le n.
$$

Proof:

Since $B=n+\alpha+\beta+1$, one has

$$
\alpha+\beta+2n=B+n-1.
$$

Therefore

$$
u_\sigma
=
\frac{B}{2}
\left(
1-\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n}
\right)
=
\frac{nB}{\alpha+\beta+2n}
=
\frac{nB}{B+n-1}.
$$

For $n\ge1$,

$$
B+n-1\ge B,
$$

so $u_\sigma\le n$. Equality in this last step occurs only at $n=1$ at the purely algebraic level; in the residual $\alpha>1/2$ range the cap is nondegenerate and $B>n+1$.

Endpoint ODE.

The standard weighted Jacobi expression satisfies

$$
(A g')'+\frac{F(x)}{A}g=0,
\qquad
A(x)=1-x^2,
$$

where

$$
F(x)=\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2,
$$

with

$$
s=\alpha+\beta,\qquad d=\beta-\alpha,\qquad
\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac{s}{2}.
$$

Substitute

$$
x=1-\frac{2u}{B}.
$$

Then

$$
1-x^2=\frac{4u(B-u)}{B^2},
$$

and

$$
d-sx=2\left(\frac{su}{B}-\alpha\right).
$$

Since

$$
\frac{du}{dx}=-\frac{B}{2},
$$

one obtains

$$
(u(B-u)H')'
+
\left(
\kappa-
\frac{(su-\alpha B)^2}{4u(B-u)}
\right)H=0.
$$

Dividing by $B$ gives

$$
(p_BH')'+q_BH=0,
$$

where

$$
p_B(u)=u\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right),
$$

and

$$
q_B(u)
=
\frac{\kappa}{B}
-
\frac{\left(\frac{s}{B}u-\alpha\right)^2}
{4u\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)}.
$$

Using

$$
\frac{s}{B}=1-\frac{n+1}{B}
$$

and

$$
\frac{\kappa}{B}
=
n+\frac12-\frac{n+1}{2B},
$$

this becomes the displayed formula for $q_B$.

Product structure.

Define

$$
K_B(u)=p_B(u)q_B(u).
$$

Then

$$
K_B(u)
=
c_Bu\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)
-
\frac14(r_Bu-\alpha)^2,
$$

where

$$
r_B=\frac{s}{B},
\qquad
c_B=\frac{\kappa}{B}=n+\frac12-\frac{n+1}{2B}.
$$

Expanding gives

$$
K_B(u)
=
-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}
+
\left(c_B+\frac{\alpha r_B}{2}\right)u
-
\left(\frac{c_B}{B}+\frac{r_B^2}{4}\right)u^2.
$$

Hence

$$
\Lambda_B=c_B+\frac{\alpha r_B}{2},
\qquad
\Delta_B=\frac{c_B}{B}+\frac{r_B^2}{4}.
$$

Thus

$$
K_B(u)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_Bu-\Delta_Bu^2,
$$

a concave quadratic.

Derivative lower bound.

Because

$$
K_B'(u)=\Lambda_B-2\Delta_Bu
$$

and $\Delta_B>0$, $K_B'$ is decreasing in $u$. Therefore, for $0\le u\le u_\sigma$,

$$
K_B'(u)\ge K_B'(u_\sigma).
$$

The exact simplification is

$$
K_B'(u_\sigma)
=
\frac{(\alpha+\beta)(n+\alpha+1)}{2B}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
K_B'(u_\sigma)
=
\frac{\alpha}{2}
+
\frac{\beta(n+1)}{2B}.
$$

This follows from

$$
(\alpha+\beta)(n+\alpha+1)
=
\alpha B+\beta(n+1).
$$

Hence in the residual right endpoint range,

$$
K_B'(u)\ge\frac{\alpha}{2}>\frac14.
$$

The weaker bound $K_B'(u)\ge1/4$ is therefore valid only after the residual hypothesis $\alpha\ge1/2$ is imposed. It must not be recorded as a global statement for all $\alpha,\beta\ge0$.

Forbidden-zone ascent.

Assume $K_B$ has a first zero $u_0\in(0,u_\sigma]$. Since $p_B(u)>0$ on $0<u<B$, the sign of $q_B$ equals the sign of $K_B$. Since

$$
K_B(0)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}<0,
$$

one has

$$
q_B(u)<0
\qquad
(0<u<u_0).
$$

The regular Frobenius branch at $u=0$ satisfies

$$
H(u)\sim A_{n,\alpha,B}u^{\alpha/2},
\qquad
A_{n,\alpha,B}>0.
$$

The exact leading coefficient is

$$
A_{n,\alpha,B}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}
}
B^{-\alpha/2}
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+1)}.
$$

Let

$$
W(u)=p_B(u)H'(u).
$$

The ODE gives

$$
W'(u)=-q_B(u)H(u).
$$

Near $u=0$, $H(u)>0$ and $W(u)>0$. If $H$ or $W$ had a first zero in $(0,u_0)$, then before that first zero one would have $H>0$ and $q_B<0$, hence

$$
W'(u)>0,
$$

so $W$ remains positive and $H'=W/p_B$ remains positive. This prevents both a first zero of $W$ and a first zero of $H$. Therefore

$$
H(u)>0,\qquad H'(u)>0
\qquad
(0<u<u_0).
$$

Thus there is no local maximum before the first turning point.

If $K_B$ has no zero in the cap and $K_B(u_\sigma)\le0$, the same argument applies on the whole cap: $H$ is positive and increasing up to $u_\sigma$, so the cap maximum is at the central boundary and is controlled by the imported central estimate.

Sonin ordering in the allowed region.

Where $q_B>0$, define

$$
S_B(u)
=
H(u)^2+\frac{p_B(u)H'(u)^2}{q_B(u)}.
$$

Using

$$
(p_BH')'=-q_BH
$$

and $K_B=p_Bq_B$, direct differentiation gives

$$
S_B'(u)
=
-\frac{K_B'(u)}{q_B(u)^2}H'(u)^2.
$$

Since $K_B'(u)\ge0$ on the cap,

$$
S_B'(u)\le0.
$$

At a critical point of $H$, one has $H'=0$ and hence

$$
S_B=H^2.
$$

Therefore local extrema in the allowed part of the cap are nonincreasing in amplitude as $u$ increases from the endpoint toward $u_\sigma$.

First-lobe reduction.

Let $u_1$ be the first critical point of $H$ in $(u_0,u_\sigma)$, if such a point exists. Then the maximum of $|H|$ in the residual cap can only occur at $u_1$ or at the central boundary $u_\sigma$. The central boundary is already controlled by the imported central module. Hence the residual cap is controlled once one proves

$$
|H(u_1)|
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)B}{(n+\alpha+1)(B-\alpha)}
\right)^{1/4}.
$$

If no such $u_1$ exists, then after the forbidden-zone ascent there is no interior local maximum before the central boundary. The cap is controlled by the endpoint behavior and the central estimate.

This proves the reduction theorem, conditional only on the imported global modules and the first-lobe amplitude theorem.

Boundary cases:

1. $n=0$.

The endpoint-cap theorem is stated only for $n\ge1$. For $n=0$ the polynomial is $P_0^{(\alpha,\beta)}=1$, so the problem becomes the elementary beta-density maximum inequality

$$
\sqrt{
\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)}
{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(\beta+1)}
}
t^{\alpha/2}(1-t)^{\beta/2}
\le
\left(
\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{(\alpha+1)(\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4},
\qquad 0\le t\le1.
$$

This should be kept as a separate base lemma. It is not part of the endpoint first-lobe reduction.

2. $\alpha=0$.

The right endpoint residual theorem assumes $\alpha>1/2$. The case $\alpha=0$ is covered by the imported small-exponent/right-endpoint module. It must not be forced through the Frobenius ascent proof, because $H(0)$ need not vanish.

3. $\alpha=1/2$.

The boundary $\alpha=1/2$ belongs to the small-endpoint theorem. The residual theorem should be stated with $\alpha>1/2$, or with $\alpha\ge1/2$ only if the imported small-exponent theorem is explicitly included to handle equality.

4. $\beta=0$.

This is not a degeneracy for the right endpoint theorem. It is included. Here

$$
B-\alpha=n+1,
$$

so

$$
T_{n,\alpha,0}=1.
$$

The first-lobe target becomes $|H(u_1)|\le1$. This case is important for semi-discrete applications and should be tested separately.

5. No zero of $K_B$ in the cap.

If $K_B(u)<0$ throughout $0<u\le u_\sigma$, then $q_B<0$ throughout the cap, and the forbidden-zone ascent argument shows $H$ is positive and increasing. The cap maximum is at $u_\sigma$, already controlled.

6. Turning point at $u_\sigma$.

If $K_B(u_\sigma)=0$, then the allowed region begins only at the boundary. The cap is controlled by forbidden-zone ascent and the central-boundary estimate.

7. No critical point $u_1$.

If $K_B$ becomes positive but $H'$ does not vanish before $u_\sigma$, then there is no first interior lobe. The maximum is again at $u_\sigma$ and is controlled.

Conditional dynamic amplitude theorem:

The exact dynamic transformation is

$$
\tau=\int^u\frac{ds}{p_B(s)}
=
\log\frac{u}{B-u}.
$$

Then

$$
u(\tau)=\frac{Be^\tau}{1+e^\tau},
$$

and

$$
\frac{du}{d\tau}=p_B(u).
$$

Since

$$
H_\tau=p_BH',
$$

the endpoint equation becomes

$$
H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0.
$$

This is exact.

If $u_0$ is the first zero of $K_B$, then with

$$
D_B=\sqrt{\Lambda_B^2-\alpha^2\Delta_B},
$$

one has

$$
u_0=\frac{\Lambda_B-D_B}{2\Delta_B},
$$

and

$$
K_B'(u_0)=D_B.
$$

The dynamic turning slope is

$$
\kappa_0
=
\left.\frac{dK_B}{d\tau}\right|_{\tau=\tau_0}
=
p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)
=
u_0\left(1-\frac{u_0}{B}\right)D_B,
$$

where

$$
\tau_0=\log\frac{u_0}{B-u_0}.
$$

Near the turning point,

$$
K_B(u(\tau))
=
\kappa_0(\tau-\tau_0)+O((\tau-\tau_0)^2).
$$

The natural Airy scale is

$$
\lambda_A=\kappa_0^{1/3}.
$$

Writing

$$
\zeta=-\lambda_A(\tau-\tau_0),
$$

the linearized equation becomes

$$
\frac{d^2H}{d\zeta^2}-\zeta H=0.
$$

Thus the regular endpoint branch should match the Airy $Ai$ branch through the turning layer, not a static Bessel model.

Now define the gamma-normalized endpoint coefficient

$$
F_{n,\alpha,B}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}
}
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+1)}.
$$

This is the coefficient in the dynamic endpoint asymptotic

$$
H(\tau)\sim F_{n,\alpha,B}e^{\alpha\tau/2}
\qquad
(\tau\to-\infty).
$$

Equivalently, if $Y$ is the normalized regular solution of

$$
Y_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))Y=0
$$

with

$$
Y(\tau)\sim e^{\alpha\tau/2}
\qquad
(\tau\to-\infty),
$$

then

$$
H=F_{n,\alpha,B}Y.
$$

This coefficient is related to the previous Bessel matching normalization by

$$
M_{n,\alpha,B}
=
F_{n,\alpha,B}\Gamma(\alpha+1)(B\Lambda_B)^{-\alpha/2}.
$$

Let $\tau_h\in(\tau_0,\tau_1)$ be a handoff point in the allowed region, for example

$$
\tau_h=\tau_0+a\kappa_0^{-1/3}
$$

with fixed $a>0$, provided this lies before $\tau_1$.

On $K_B>0$, define modified Prufer variables for $Y$ by

$$
Y=R K_B^{-1/4}\sin\phi,
\qquad
Y_\tau=R K_B^{1/4}\cos\phi.
$$

Then

$$
R^2=K_B^{1/2}Y^2+K_B^{-1/2}Y_\tau^2.
$$

The exact Prufer equations are

$$
\frac{R_\tau}{R}
=
-\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos2\phi,
$$

and

$$
\phi_\tau
=
\sqrt{K_B}
+
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\sin2\phi.
$$

Let $u_1$ be the first critical point of $H$, equivalently of $Y$, after $u_0$, and let $\tau_1=\tau(u_1)$. At $\tau_1$,

$$
Y_\tau(\tau_1)=0,
$$

so

$$
|Y(\tau_1)|=R(\tau_1)K_B(u_1)^{-1/4}.
$$

Therefore

$$
|H(u_1)|
=
F_{n,\alpha,B}R(\tau_1)K_B(u_1)^{-1/4}.
$$

The exact drift identity is

$$
R(\tau_1)
=
R(\tau_h)
\exp\left(
-\frac14
\int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1}
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{K_B}\cos2\phi\,d\tau
\right).
$$

Here

$$
K_{B,\tau}=p_B(u)K_B'(u).
$$

Thus the following conditional theorem is enough.

Conditional theorem L15.2: Dynamic first-lobe amplitude certificate.

Assume the residual right-endpoint hypotheses and assume $u_0,u_1$ exist inside the cap. Let $\tau_h\in(\tau_0,\tau_1)$ and suppose we have explicit quantities $\mathcal A_h$, $E_h$, and $k_1$ such that

$$
R(\tau_h)\le\mathcal A_h,
$$

$$
\left|
\int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1}
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{K_B}\cos2\phi\,d\tau
\right|
\le E_h,
$$

and

$$
K_B(u_1)\ge k_1>0.
$$

Then the KKT first-lobe target follows if

$$
F_{n,\alpha,B}\mathcal A_h\exp(E_h/4)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}k_1^{1/4}.
$$

Equivalently, without replacing $K_B(u_1)$ by a lower bound, it is enough to prove

$$
F_{n,\alpha,B}\mathcal A_h\exp(E_h/4)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}K_B(u_1)^{1/4}.
$$

This theorem is exact. It does not solve the amplitude problem, because $\mathcal A_h$, $E_h$, and $k_1$ still require explicit bounds.

A usable Airy initialization theorem should prove an inequality of the schematic form

$$
\mathcal A_h
\le
(1+\varepsilon_A)
C_{\mathrm{Ai}}(n,\alpha,B)
\kappa_0^{1/6}
\left(
a^{1/2}Ai(-a)^2+a^{-1/2}Ai'(-a)^2
\right)^{1/2},
$$

where $C_{\mathrm{Ai}}$ is the connection coefficient from the regular Frobenius branch to the Airy branch and $\varepsilon_A$ is an explicit Langer/Olver error bound. This line is not yet proved; it is the precise form of the next analytic task.

What remains unproved:

The unproved part is narrow:

1. an Airy/Langer initialization bound for $R(\tau_h)$ from the regular endpoint branch, with explicit constants uniform in

$$
n\ge1,\qquad \frac12<\alpha<\alpha_E(n),\qquad \beta\ge0;
$$

2. a regularized Prufer drift bound

$$
\left|
\int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1}
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{K_B}\cos2\phi\,d\tau
\right|
\le E_h
$$

that does not lose too much near $K_B=0$;

3. a lower bound for $K_B(u_1)$, or an Airy/phase theorem that directly estimates $R(\tau_1)K_B(u_1)^{-1/4}$;

Dependencies:

Certified or imported dependencies:

1. KKT definitions and integer-parameter estimates. KKT derive $|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|\le1$ for integer $\alpha,\beta$ from SU(2) unitarity and record the stronger Lemma 4.3 estimate (4.9). They state Conjecture 6.1 for real $\alpha,\beta\ge0$.

2. KKT link to the discrete Laguerre dispersive estimate. The kernel is expressed through $g_n^{(\alpha,m-n)}$, and (4.8) implies the sharp $\ell^1\to\ell^\infty$ decay for integer $\alpha$; Conjecture 6.1 would extend it to all $\alpha\ge0$.

3. Haagerup--Schlichtkrull's real-parameter Bernstein inequality. It gives a uniform real-parameter Jacobi estimate but not the sharp KKT sup-norm constant needed for closure.

4. Landau's Bessel monotonicity result. Landau states that $\sup_x |J_\nu(x)|$ is decreasing in $\nu$ and gives related sharp uniform Bessel bounds. This supports the candidate bound $\sup_{\nu\ge1/2,t\ge0}|J_\nu(t)|<0.680$, but any Bessel-based closure must still cite or prove the precise half-order maximum. For $\nu=1/2$, the first maximum solves $\tan t=2t$ and has value approximately $0.6791921047$, not $2/\pi$.

5. Imported local workflow state: Round 14 already selected the dynamic oscillator route and identified the remaining amplitude gap; Round 15 explicitly asks A1 to formulate the theorem package and conditional amplitude statement.

Potential gaps:

1. Central-boundary dependency.

The endpoint cap theorem assumes the central module controls $u=u_\sigma$, i.e. $x=\sigma$. If the central theorem is only stated on an open interval $|x|<\sigma$, the final proof needs a continuity closure or an explicit endpoint statement.

2. Airy layer versus endpoint singularity.

For fixed $\alpha>1/2$ and $n\to\infty$, the first turning point satisfies heuristically $u_0=O(1/n)$, so the Airy layer interacts with the endpoint hard-edge scaling. A pure interior Airy theorem may fail there; the correct theorem may need a Bessel-to-Airy uniform transition or a Langer transform adapted to the regular singular endpoint.

3. Transition strip $\alpha=O(n)$.

When $\alpha=cn$, $0<c<1$, the first lobe can lie at $u=O(n)$. Static-frequency Bessel comparison becomes lossy there. A dynamic theorem must track $K_B$ rather than replacing it by a constant.

4. Gamma normalization.

The factor

$$
M_{n,\alpha,B}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B)}
{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}
}
(B\Lambda_B)^{-\alpha/2}
$$

can exceed $1$ in parts of the residual region. A theorem of the form $M\le1+C/(n+1)$ is plausible but unproved and may not be sharp enough without regime splitting.

5. Prufer singularity at $K_B=0$.

The formula

$$
\frac{R_\tau}{R}
=
-\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos2\phi
$$

is singular at the turning point. Starting the drift at $\tau_h>\tau_0$ avoids the pole, but then the Airy initialization must control the full derivative energy at $\tau_h$.

6. Need for $K_B(u_1)$ control.

The target at a critical point is

$$
|H(u_1)|=F_{n,\alpha,B}R(\tau_1)K_B(u_1)^{-1/4}.
$$

A bound on $R(\tau_1)$ alone is insufficient unless $K_B(u_1)$ is also controlled from below or the Airy/phase argument estimates the product directly.

$$

with $\delta>0$ and no compensating gain in $K_B(u_1)$ or normalization would make the Airy/Prufer route too weak.

3. If the Airy layer around $u_0$ overlaps the endpoint region in a way that forces a connection coefficient larger than the KKT slack for fixed $\alpha>1/2$, then the proposed Airy initialization must be replaced by a hard-edge Bessel/Langer connection.

4. If the worst finite-$B$ case is not near $\beta=0$ or $\beta=\infty$ but at an interior value of $\theta=(n+\alpha+1)/B$, then monotonic endpoint or Laguerre-face arguments will be insufficient and a genuinely finite-$B$ theorem is necessary.

Concrete edge cases to test:

- $n=0$: beta-density base inequality.
- $n=1$: possible small finite-dimensional extremal behavior.
- $\alpha=1/2$: boundary between small-exponent theorem and residual theorem.
- $\alpha=\alpha_E(n)$: energy boundary.
- $\beta=0$: semi-discrete and target $T=1$.
- $\beta\to\infty$: Laguerre cap face.
- $K_B(u_\sigma)=0$: turning point exactly at the central interface.
- no $u_1$: monotone cap.

Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration:

1. Semi-discrete specialization $\beta\in\mathbb N_0$.

The main application only needs $\alpha\ge0$ and $\beta\in\mathbb N_0$. In the right endpoint equation, $\beta$ enters continuously through $B$ and $r_B$, so the ODE itself does not immediately simplify when $\beta$ is an integer. However, integer $\beta$ may still help through raising/lowering relations or finite product-formula remnants.

The most promising semi-discrete subroute is to test monotonicity in the compactified parameter

$$
\theta=\frac{n+\alpha+1}{B}.
$$

For fixed $n,\alpha$, integer $\beta$ samples a discrete set of $\theta$ values. If the first-lobe ratio is monotone in $\theta$, or if the worst case is always $\beta=0$ or $\beta=\infty$, then the semi-discrete proof could reduce to two faces. This is plausible but open.

A second semi-discrete possibility is a $\beta$-raising recurrence. Since $\beta$ is integer, one might compare $g_n^{(\alpha,\beta+1)}$ with a finite combination of neighboring Jacobi expressions at parameter $\beta$. This would need positivity or a contraction property in the normalized weighted basis. Without positivity, the route is speculative.

2. Langer/Szego route instead of Prufer drift.

The Prufer route gives exact identities but requires handling an oscillatory integral with a singular coefficient near $K_B=0$. A Langer transform may absorb the turning point more naturally. The correct Langer variable should be defined by

$$
\frac23 \zeta^{3/2}
=
\int_{\tau_0}^{\tau}\sqrt{K_B(u(s))}\,ds
$$

on the allowed side, with the corresponding analytic continuation into the forbidden side. The transformed equation has an Airy core plus a Schwarzian residual. This could produce a direct uniform bound on $H$ through the first lobe.

The obstruction is that the Schwarzian residual must be computed and bounded explicitly, not hidden inside an $O(1/n)$ term. The current dynamic oscillator is simple enough that this calculation is now feasible and should be assigned to A2/A3.

3. Product formula and hypergroup route.

The product-formula route remains attractive for the semi-discrete target because $\beta\in\mathbb N_0$ may correspond to finite-dimensional angular data. But no positive product formula currently gives the exact normalized two-sided KKT bound for all real $\alpha\ge0$ with integer $\beta$. This route should stay secondary until someone states the exact positivity theorem and normalization.

4. Christoffel-function route.

A sharp weighted Christoffel estimate could imply a pointwise bound, but standard Christoffel bounds usually lose constants and do not isolate the first lobe. It is not competitive with the endpoint-cap route unless a sharp reproducing-kernel identity emerges.
