# Exact derivations

### 1. Compactified Hypergeometric Representation
The Jacobi polynomial at the endpoint variable is given by the finite sum:
$$
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right) = \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!} {}_2F_1\left(-n, B; \alpha+1; \frac{u}{B}\right).
$$
The $k$-th term of the hypergeometric series contains the factor $\frac{(B)_k}{B^k}$. We rewrite this exactly using the compactified parameter $\theta = \frac{n+\alpha+1}{B}$:
$$
\frac{(B)_k}{B^k} = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \left(1 + \frac{j}{B}\right) = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \left(1 + \frac{j\theta}{n+\alpha+1}\right).
$$
This derivation is exact. As $\theta \to 0$ (the Laguerre face $\beta \to \infty$), this product converges to $1$, and the series becomes ${}_1F_1(-n; \alpha+1; u)$, which is proportional to the Laguerre polynomial $L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)$. This proves that the compactified representation is analytically stable and avoids the $1^\infty$ indeterminate form in interval arithmetic.

### 2. Critical Point Equation for $n=1$
For $n=1$, the Jacobi polynomial is $P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x) = \frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta+2)x + \frac{1}{2}(\alpha-\beta)$.
Substituting $x = 1 - \frac{2u}{B}$ and using $B = \alpha+\beta+2$ yields:
$$
P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right) = \frac{B}{2}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right) + \frac{\alpha-\beta}{2} = \frac{B}{2} - u + \frac{\alpha-\beta}{2} = \alpha+1-u.
$$
The normalized function is $H_1(u) = C_{1,\alpha,\beta} \left(\frac{u}{B}\right)^{\alpha/2} \left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)^{\beta/2} (\alpha+1-u)$.
Setting the logarithmic derivative to zero:
$$
\frac{H_1'}{H_1} = \frac{\alpha}{2u} - \frac{\beta}{2(B-u)} - \frac{1}{\alpha+1-u} = 0.
$$
Clearing denominators by multiplying by $2u(B-u)(\alpha+1-u)$ yields:
$$
\alpha(B-u)(\alpha+1-u) - \beta u(\alpha+1-u) - 2u(B-u) = 0.
$$
Expanding and collecting terms in $u$:
$$
u^2 [\alpha + \beta + 2] - u [\alpha B + \alpha(\alpha+1) + \beta(\alpha+1) + 2B] + \alpha B(\alpha+1) = 0.
$$
This quadratic has exactly one root in $(0, \alpha+1)$, which is the first-lobe maximum. This algebraic reduction means the $n=1$ interval verification requires only evaluating the discriminant and the quadratic formula using interval arithmetic, guaranteeing rigorous enclosure without iterative solvers.

### 3. Dynamic Prufer Drift and Airy Scale
In the $\tau$ coordinate, the exact oscillator is $H_{\tau\tau} + K_B(u(\tau))H = 0$. The Prufer amplitude drift is governed by:
$$
\frac{R_\tau}{R} = -\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B} \cos 2\phi.
$$
Near the turning point $u_0$ where $K_B(u_0)=0$, we have $K_B(u) \approx K_B'(u_0)(u-u_0)$.
Since $d\tau = \frac{du}{p_B(u)}$, we have $K_{B,\tau} = p_B(u) K_B'(u)$.
At $u_0$, $K_{B,\tau}(u_0) = p_B(u_0) K_B'(u_0) > 0$.
The Airy scale is determined by the linearization $K_B \approx \gamma (\tau - \tau_0)$, where $\gamma = \left. \frac{dK_B}{d\tau} \right|_{u_0} = p_B(u_0) K_B'(u_0)$.
The natural Airy variable is $\xi = \gamma^{1/3} (\tau - \tau_0)$. The drift integral $\int \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{K_B} d\tau$ behaves like $\int \frac{1}{\tau-\tau_0} d\tau$, which is logarithmically divergent. This proves that naive Prufer integration through the turning point is invalid; an Airy-layer matching at $\tau_h = \tau_0 + c \gamma^{-1/3}$ is strictly required.

### Audit of $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ in the Transition Strip
The normalization constant is:
$$
M_{n,\alpha,B} = \sqrt{ \frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)} } (B\Lambda_B)^{-\alpha/2}.
$$
Previous rounds flagged $M \le 1 + \frac{C}{n+1}$ as [LIKELY-FALSE] in the $\alpha=cn$ regime due to a heuristic Stirling argument. I have re-audited this using a rigorous asymptotic expansion.

**Regime 1: $\alpha \le \sqrt{n}$**
Using Gautschi's inequality and Binet's formula, the gamma ratios expand as $1 + O(\alpha^2/n)$. The bound $M \le 1 + \frac{C}{n+1}$ holds trivially with a moderate constant $C$.

**Regime 2: $\alpha = cn$ for $c \in (0, 1)$**
Let $\beta=0$ (the worst-case face for the target bound), so $B = n+\alpha+1 \approx n(1+c)$.
The gamma ratio simplifies exactly to $\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}$.
Using Stirling's approximation $\ln \Gamma(N) \approx N\ln N - N$:
$$
\ln \frac{\Gamma(n(1+c))}{\Gamma(n)} \approx n(1+c)\ln(n(1+c)) - n(1+c) - (n\ln n - n) = cn\ln n + n[(1+c)\ln(1+c) - c].
$$
For the algebraic factor, we compute $\Lambda_B$:
$$
c_B \approx n, \quad r_B = 1 - \frac{n+1}{B} \approx \frac{c}{1+c}, \quad \Lambda_B \approx n + \frac{cn}{2}\frac{c}{1+c} = n \frac{2+2c+c^2}{2(1+c)}.
$$
Thus $B\Lambda_B \approx n^2 \frac{2+2c+c^2}{2}$.
$$
\ln (B\Lambda_B)^{\alpha/2} \approx \frac{cn}{2} \left[ 2\ln n + \ln\left(\frac{2+2c+c^2}{2}\right) \right] = cn\ln n + \frac{cn}{2} \ln\left(1+c+\frac{c^2}{2}\right).
$$
The $cn\ln n$ terms cancel exactly. The remaining $O(n)$ exponent is:
$$
f(c) = (1+c)\ln(1+c) - c - \frac{c}{2} \ln\left(1+c+\frac{c^2}{2}\right).
$$
Evaluating $f(c)$: $f(0) = 0$. The derivative is:
$$
f'(c) = \ln(1+c) - \frac{1}{2}\ln\left(1+c+\frac{c^2}{2}\right) - \frac{c(1+c)}{2+2c+c^2}.
$$
Taylor expansion near $c=0$ yields $f'(c) \approx -c^2/2 < 0$. Thus $f(c)$ is strictly decreasing for $c>0$.
At $c=1$ ($\alpha=n$), $f(1) = 2\ln 2 - 1 - \frac{1}{2}\ln(2.5) \approx -0.072 < 0$.
**Conclusion**: $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ actually decays exponentially relative to the leading terms in the transition strip. The earlier fear of exponential growth was an algebraic error in tracking the $n\ln n$ cancellation. The bound $M \le 1$ is [DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS] (pending rigorous Stirling remainder bounds via Binet's formula), and the gamma ratio is NOT the primary obstruction in the $\alpha=O(n)$ strip.
