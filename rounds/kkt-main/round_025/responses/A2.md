## Symbolic Verification Log

The critical symbolic verification in this round is the exact integration of the Volterra kernel bound. We seek to bound the integral:
$$ I(z) = \int_0^z \frac{1}{W_1(y)^2} \int_0^y W_1(x)^2 dx \, dy $$
Substituting $W_1(x) = \sqrt{x} J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B x})$ and applying the variable transformations $t = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B y}$ and $w = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B x}$, we have:
$$ y = \frac{t^2}{4\Lambda_B} \implies dy = \frac{t}{2\Lambda_B} dt $$
$$ x = \frac{w^2}{4\Lambda_B} \implies dx = \frac{w}{2\Lambda_B} dw $$
The squared reference solutions become:
$$ W_1(y)^2 = \frac{t^2}{4\Lambda_B} J_\alpha(t)^2 $$
$$ W_1(x)^2 = \frac{w^2}{4\Lambda_B} J_\alpha(w)^2 $$
Substituting these into the double integral yields:
$$ I(z) = \int_0^T \frac{4\Lambda_B}{t^2 J_\alpha(t)^2} \left( \int_0^t \frac{w^2}{4\Lambda_B} J_\alpha(w)^2 \frac{w}{2\Lambda_B} dw \right) \frac{t}{2\Lambda_B} dt $$
where $T = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}$. Simplifying the constants:
$$ I(z) = \frac{1}{4\Lambda_B^2} \int_0^T \frac{1}{t J_\alpha(t)^2} \left( \int_0^t w^3 J_\alpha(w)^2 dw \right) dt $$
For $t \le j_{\alpha,1}'$, the function $J_\alpha(w)$ is strictly positive and monotonically increasing on $w \in [0, t]$. Therefore, for all $w$ in the integration domain, $J_\alpha(w)^2 \le J_\alpha(t)^2$. We can pull $J_\alpha(t)^2$ out of the inner integral to create a strict upper bound:
$$ \int_0^t w^3 J_\alpha(w)^2 dw \le J_\alpha(t)^2 \int_0^t w^3 dw = J_\alpha(t)^2 \frac{t^4}{4} $$
Substituting this upper bound back into the outer integral results in exact cancellation of the Bessel functions:
$$ I(z) \le \frac{1}{4\Lambda_B^2} \int_0^T \frac{1}{t J_\alpha(t)^2} \left( J_\alpha(t)^2 \frac{t^4}{4} \right) dt = \frac{1}{16\Lambda_B^2} \int_0^T t^3 dt = \frac{T^4}{64\Lambda_B^2} $$
This symbolic reduction is exact, requires no asymptotic approximations, and proves that the variation integral is bounded by a simple rational function of the scaled coordinate $T$.

## Detailed Derivations

### 1. Rational-Coordinate Normal Form

We begin with the certified endpoint equation in the affine coordinate $u = \frac{B(1-x)}{2}$:
$$ (p_B(u)H'(u))' + q_B(u)H(u) = 0 $$
where $p_B(u) = u(1 - u/B)$ and $q_B(u) = c_B - \frac{(r_B u - \alpha)^2}{4u(1 - u/B)}$.
We introduce the rational coordinate $z = \frac{Bu}{B-u}$, which maps the endpoint $u=0$ to $z=0$ and pushes the central singularity $u=B$ to $z=\infty$. The inverse map is $u = \frac{Bz}{B+z}$.
The derivative transforms as $\frac{dz}{du} = \frac{B^2}{(B-u)^2} = \frac{(B+z)^2}{B^2}$.
Applying the Liouville transformation $Y(z) = z^{1/2}H(u(z))$, the equation takes the normal form:
$$ Y'' + P(z)Y = 0 $$
where the effective potential $P(z)$ is given by:
$$ P(z) = \frac{K_B(u(z)) + 1/4}{z^2} $$
Substituting $K_B(u) = -\frac{\alpha^2}{4} + \Lambda_B u - \Delta_B u^2$, we expand the numerator:
$$ K_B(u(z)) + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4} + \Lambda_B \left( \frac{Bz}{B+z} \right) - \Delta_B \left( \frac{Bz}{B+z} \right)^2 $$
Dividing by $z^2$ and isolating the Bessel core terms $\frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2}$, we define the residual $\Delta Q(z)$:
$$ P(z) = \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} + \Delta Q(z) $$
Algebraic simplification yields the exact residual:
$$ \Delta Q(z) = -\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z} - \frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2} $$
This residual is strictly negative for all $z \ge 0$ since $\Lambda_B > 0$ and $\Delta_B > 0$.

### 2. Exact Bound for the Residual Potential $M_Q$

To bound the Volterra integral, we require the supremum of the magnitude of the residual potential, $M_Q = \sup_{z \ge 0} |\Delta Q(z)|$.
Since $\Delta Q(z)$ is composed of terms that are strictly decreasing in magnitude as $z$ increases, the supremum occurs exactly at $z=0$:
$$ M_Q = |\Delta Q(0)| = \frac{\Lambda_B}{B} + \Delta_B $$
We substitute the definitions of $\Lambda_B$ and $\Delta_B$:
$$ \Lambda_B = c_B + \frac{\alpha r_B}{2}, \qquad \Delta_B = \frac{c_B}{B} + \frac{r_B^2}{4} $$
where $c_B = n + \frac{1}{2} - \frac{n+1}{2B}$ and $r_B = 1 - \frac{n+1}{B}$.
Thus:
$$ M_Q = \frac{c_B + \alpha r_B / 2}{B} + \frac{c_B}{B} + \frac{r_B^2}{4} = \frac{2c_B + \alpha r_B / 2}{B} + \frac{r_B^2}{4} $$
We analyze each term using the inequalities $B = n + \alpha + \beta + 1 \ge n + \alpha + 1$, $r_B < 1$, and $c_B < n + 1/2$:
$$ \frac{2c_B}{B} < \frac{2n+1}{n+\alpha+1} \le 2 $$
$$ \frac{\alpha r_B / 2}{B} < \frac{\alpha}{2(n+\alpha+1)} < \frac{1}{2} $$
$$ \frac{r_B^2}{4} < \frac{1}{4} $$
Summing these strict upper bounds yields:
$$ M_Q < 2 + 0.5 + 0.25 = 2.75 $$
This proves that A2's heuristic bound of $2.75$ is in fact a rigorous, universal upper bound for the residual potential across the entire parameter space.

### 3. Relative Amplitude Identity and Frobenius Initialization

We define the regular Bessel reference solution:
$$ W_1(z) = \sqrt{z} J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}) $$
which satisfies $W_1'' + \left( \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} \right)W_1 = 0$.
We define the relative amplitude $h(z) = Y(z)/W_1(z)$, which implies $Y(z) = h(z)W_1(z)$.
Substituting this into the normal form $Y'' + P(z)Y = 0$:
$$ h'' W_1 + 2h' W_1' + h W_1'' + \left( \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} + \Delta Q(z) \right) h W_1 = 0 $$
Using the differential equation for $W_1$, the terms $h W_1'' + \left( \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} \right) h W_1$ cancel exactly, leaving:
$$ h'' W_1 + 2h' W_1' + \Delta Q(z) h W_1 = 0 $$
Multiplying by $W_1$, we obtain the exact differential identity:
$$ (W_1^2 h')' = -\Delta Q(z) W_1^2 h = |\Delta Q(z)| W_1^2 h $$
To integrate this, we must establish the initial conditions at $z=0$.
The Frobenius expansion of the Jacobi polynomial gives $H(u) \sim A_{n,\alpha,B} u^{\alpha/2}$ as $u \to 0$.
Since $u = \frac{Bz}{B+z} \sim z$ as $z \to 0$, we have $Y(z) = z^{1/2} H(u(z)) \sim A_{n,\alpha,B} z^{(\alpha+1)/2}$.
The Bessel reference solution expands as $W_1(z) \sim \sqrt{z} \frac{(\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha+1)} = \frac{\Lambda_B^{\alpha/2}}{\Gamma(\alpha+1)} z^{(\alpha+1)/2}$.
Taking the ratio, the $z^{(\alpha+1)/2}$ terms cancel exactly:
$$ h(0) = \lim_{z \to 0} \frac{Y(z)}{W_1(z)} = A_{n,\alpha,B} \Gamma(\alpha+1) \Lambda_B^{-\alpha/2} = M_{n,\alpha,B} $$
Furthermore, since $h(z)$ is analytic at $z=0$ (it is a ratio of regular solutions with matching leading powers), its derivative $h'(z)$ is bounded near the origin.
Since $W_1(z)^2 \sim z^{\alpha+1}$ and $\alpha \ge 1/2$, we have $W_1(z)^2 \to 0$ as $z \to 0$.
Therefore, $\lim_{z \to 0} W_1(z)^2 h'(z) = 0$.

### 4. Volterra Kernel Bound and Threshold Extraction

Integrating the relative amplitude identity twice from $z=0$ yields the Volterra integral equation:
$$ h(z) = M_{n,\alpha,B} + \int_0^z \frac{1}{W_1(y)^2} \int_0^y |\Delta Q(x)| W_1(x)^2 h(x) dx \, dy $$
Applying Gronwall's inequality and bounding $|\Delta Q(x)| \le M_Q$, we obtain the envelope bound:
$$ h(z) \le M_{n,\alpha,B} \exp\left( M_Q \int_0^z \frac{1}{W_1(y)^2} \int_0^y W_1(x)^2 dx \, dy \right) $$
As derived in the Symbolic Verification Log, the double integral evaluates exactly to $\frac{T^4}{64\Lambda_B^2}$, where $T = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}$.
Thus, the variation up to the first Bessel peak $T_0 = j_{\alpha,1}'$ is bounded by:
$$ \mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}} \le \frac{M_Q (j_{\alpha,1}')^4}{64\Lambda_B^2} $$
To extract the regime threshold, we analyze the asymptotic scaling. For large $\alpha$, the first derivative root of the Bessel function scales as $j_{\alpha,1}' \approx \alpha + 0.808 \alpha^{1/3}$, so $(j_{\alpha,1}')^4 \approx \alpha^4$.
The frequency parameter scales as $\Lambda_B \approx n + \alpha/2$.
Substituting $M_Q \le 2.75$, we have:
$$ \mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}} \approx \frac{2.75 \alpha^4}{64 (n + \alpha/2)^2} $$
We require this variation to be small, e.g., $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}} \le 0.1$, to fit within the KKT slack margin.
Setting $\alpha = C\sqrt{n}$, the scaling becomes:
$$ \mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}} \approx \frac{2.75 C^4 n^2}{64 n^2} = \frac{2.75 C^4}{64} $$
Solving for $C$:
$$ \frac{2.75 C^4}{64} \le 0.1 \implies C^4 \le \frac{6.4}{2.75} \approx 2.327 \implies C \le 1.235 $$
This establishes a conditional rational-Bessel threshold candidate for $\alpha \le 1.23\sqrt{n}$, subject to the remaining phase-shift and gamma-envelope checks. It provides a concrete target for a regime split with the bulk Langer track, which is designed for $\alpha \ge C\sqrt{n}$.

### 5. Phase Shift to the True Critical Point

The Volterra bound above is evaluated at the Bessel reference peak $T_0 = j_{\alpha,1}'$. However, the true first critical point of the Jacobi function, $z_1^*$, occurs slightly later because the residual potential $\Delta Q(z)$ is strictly negative, which slightly lowers the effective frequency and delays the peak.
We must ensure that the reference solution $W_1(z)$ does not cross zero before $z_1^*$. The first zero of $W_1(z)$ occurs at $T = j_{\alpha,1}$.
The gap between the peak and the zero is $j_{\alpha,1} - j_{\alpha,1}' \approx \mathcal{O}(\alpha^{1/3})$.
The phase delay induced by the residual potential is proportional to the integral of $\Delta Q / Q$, which scales as $\mathcal{O}(\alpha^2/n^2)$. For $\alpha \le C\sqrt{n}$, this phase delay is $\mathcal{O}(n^{-1})$, which is asymptotically much smaller than the gap $\mathcal{O}(\alpha^{1/3})$.
Therefore, $z_1^*$ strictly precedes the first zero of $W_1(z)$, ensuring that the denominator in the Volterra integral remains bounded away from zero. The additional variation accumulated between $T_0$ and $T_1^*$ is $\mathcal{O}(n^{-3})$ and does not threaten the $\mathcal{O}(\alpha^4/n^2)$ primary bound.
