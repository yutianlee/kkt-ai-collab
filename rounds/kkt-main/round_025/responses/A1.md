4. **$n=2$ risk.** The $n=2$ proof is not obtained by copying $n=1$. The critical equation is cubic, and multiple cap roots are possible. A grid or heuristic cap sign argument cannot replace interval root isolation.

5. **Rational-Bessel risk.** The residual

$$
\Delta Q(z)
=
-\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z}
-\frac{\Delta_BB^2}{(B+z)^2}
$$

is regular but not pointwise tiny at $z=0$ in all regimes. A Volterra argument must use the exact Bessel kernel, not just pointwise smallness.

6. **Airy relative-amplitude risk.** The identity $(a^2h')'=\Psi_Ba^2h$ can be useful only on intervals where the reference Airy factor has no zero and where the sign or absolute variation of $\Psi_B$ is controlled. It does not remove the Frobenius-to-Airy normalization problem by itself.

7. **Krasikov-style higher Sonin risk.** A higher-order envelope might bypass first-lobe Airy constants, but positivity of the quadratic form and derivative sign are nontrivial. The candidate must be tested on $n=1$ and $n=2$ before any large-$n$ optimism.

Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration:

### Alternative 1: Airy relative-amplitude identity

Let

$$
W_{\zeta\zeta}+\zeta W=\Psi_B(\zeta)W
$$

and choose a reference Airy solution

$$
a(\zeta)=\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)
$$

or an appropriate nonzero linear combination on the interval of interest. Set

$$
h(\zeta)=\frac{W(\zeta)}{a(\zeta)}.
$$

Then formally

$$
(a^2h')'=\Psi_Ba^2h.
$$

This is the Airy analogue of A2's rational-Bessel relative-amplitude identity. Its attraction is that it might avoid crude Airy matrix norms. Its obstruction is immediate: $a(\zeta)$ has zeros on the allowed side, and $\Psi_B$ is not known to have a uniform favorable sign. A viable theorem would need:

- a zero-free interval $[\zeta_c,\zeta_1]$ for the chosen reference $a$;
- a signed or absolute bound for $\Psi_B$;
- controlled initial data $h(\zeta_c)$ and $a^2h'(\zeta_c)$;
- a scalar inequality implying $|H(u_1)|\le T$.

A quick falsification test is to compute the sign of $\Psi_B$ and the location of the first zero of $\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)$ relative to $\zeta_1$ on the hardest $n=2$ and Laguerre-face samples. If $a$ vanishes before $\zeta_1$, this specific reference choice fails.

### Alternative 2: Krasikov-style higher-order Sonin envelopes

The ordinary Sonin functional

$$
S=H^2+\frac{p_BH'^2}{q_B}
$$

orders extrema but does not bound the first allowed extremum. A higher envelope might have the form

$$
V(u)=A(u)H(u)^2+B(u)H'(u)^2+C(u)H(u)H'(u),
$$

with a derivative identity

$$
V'(u)\le0
$$

or

$$
V'(u)\ge0
$$

on the cap, and with endpoint normalization strong enough to imply the KKT target.

This is structurally attractive because it may avoid explicit Airy matching. It is also dangerous: positivity of the quadratic form requires

$$
A(u)\ge0,\qquad B(u)\ge0,\qquad 4A(u)B(u)-C(u)^2\ge0,
$$

and derivative sign can fail at turning points. The first test should be symbolic for $n=1$, where the theorem is essentially known, and then for $n=2$ with the corrected compactification. If no low-degree positive envelope exists, the route should be demoted.

### Alternative 3: Christoffel-Darboux/Turan bound at critical points

At a critical point,

$$
\left(\beta(1-x)-\alpha(1+x)\right)P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
+
B(1-x^2)P_{n-1}^{(\alpha+1,\beta+1)}(x)=0.
$$

One could combine this relation with a Christoffel-Darboux or Turan inequality to bound $P_n$ at critical points only. This is narrower than a full sup-norm inequality and might match the first-lobe reduction. The missing lemma would be a positive kernel estimate with the exact KKT normalization and endpoint weights. The quick falsification test is to check whether known Jacobi Turan inequalities have the correct sign for $\alpha,\beta\ge0$ and remain sharp enough at $n=1,2$.

### Alternative 4: semi-discrete monotonicity in $\beta$

The application only needs $\beta\in\mathbb N_0$. If the continuous-$\beta$ proof stalls, one should test whether the first-lobe ratio

$$
\frac{|H(u_1)|}{T_{n,\alpha,\beta}}
$$

is monotone or convex in $\beta$ on the semi-discrete sequence. A discrete convexity result would reduce the semi-discrete proof to $\beta=0$ or a Laguerre boundary. Current evidence does not prove such monotonicity. The test should be numerical first, then symbolic for $n=1,2$.
