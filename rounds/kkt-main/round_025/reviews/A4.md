# Agent-by-agent review of A2

A2's output contains the most significant analytic breakthrough of the current workflow, but it also contains a critical structural flaw regarding the integration domain. The rational-coordinate Bessel track has historically been plagued by loose Watson/Nicholson asymptotics and unbounded Wronskian kernels. The derivation of the relative amplitude identity $(W_1^2 h')' = |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h$ is a direct consequence of the Liouville normal form and the exact cancellation of the Bessel core potential. Since $\Delta Q(z) = -\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z} - \frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2}$ is manifestly negative for all $z \ge 0$, the identity guarantees that $h(z)$ is strictly monotonically increasing. This step is **[PROVED]**.

The crown jewel of A2's response is the exact evaluation of the Volterra double integral:
$$ I(z) = \int_0^z \frac{1}{W_1(y)^2} \int_0^y W_1(x)^2 dx dy $$
By changing variables to $t = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B y}$ and $w = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B x}$, A2 transforms the integral into:
$$ I(z) = \frac{1}{4\Lambda_B^2} \int_0^T \frac{1}{t J_\alpha(t)^2} \left( \int_0^t w^3 J_\alpha(w)^2 dw \right) dt $$
The critical insight is that for $t \le j'_{\alpha,1}$ (the first positive zero of $J'_\alpha$), the function $J_\alpha(w)$ is strictly positive and strictly increasing. Therefore, $J_\alpha(w)^2 \le J_\alpha(t)^2$ for all $w \in [0, t]$. This allows A2 to pull $J_\alpha(t)^2$ out of the inner integral, yielding $\int_0^t w^3 J_\alpha(w)^2 dw \le J_\alpha(t)^2 \frac{t^4}{4}$. The $J_\alpha(t)^2$ terms cancel exactly, leaving $\int_0^T \frac{t^3}{4} dt = \frac{T^4}{16}$. The final bound $\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{Bess}} \le \frac{M_Q (j'_{\alpha,1})^4}{64\Lambda_B^2}$ is a masterpiece of exact analytic bounding. It completely bypasses the need for asymptotic approximations and provides a hard, finite-parameter inequality. This step is **[PROVED]** strictly for $T \le j'_{\alpha,1}$.

However, A2's treatment of the phase shift between the Bessel reference peak $j'_{\alpha,1}$ and the true perturbed critical point $T_1^*$ contains a severe mathematical mismatch. A2 defines the reference solution as $W_1(z) = \sqrt{z} J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})$. The peak of $W_1(z)$ does not occur at $j'_{\alpha,1}$. Let $t = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}$. The function $W_1$ is proportional to $t J_\alpha(t)$. The maximum of $t J_\alpha(t)$ occurs when its derivative vanishes: $J_\alpha(t) + t J_\alpha'(t) = 0$. Let this root be $t_{max}$. At $t = j'_{\alpha,1}$, we have $J_\alpha'(t) = 0$, so the derivative of $t J_\alpha(t)$ is $J_\alpha(j'_{\alpha,1}) > 0$. Thus, $t_{max} > j'_{\alpha,1}$. 

Because the true critical point $T_1^*$ of the perturbed solution $Y(z)$ will be near $t_{max}$, the Volterra integral must be evaluated up to $T_1^* > j'_{\alpha,1}$. For $t \in (j'_{\alpha,1}, t_{max}]$, the function $J_\alpha(t)$ is strictly decreasing. The inner integral $\int_0^t w^3 J_\alpha(w)^2 dw$ accumulates its maximum value near $j'_{\alpha,1}$, while the outer integrand contains the factor $1 / J_\alpha(t)^2$. As $t$ increases past $j'_{\alpha,1}$, $J_\alpha(t)$ decreases, causing $1 / J_\alpha(t)^2$ to increase rapidly. The simple upper bound $J_\alpha(w) \le J_\alpha(t)$ is invalid for $t > j'_{\alpha,1}$ because $w$ can be $j'_{\alpha,1}$ (where $J_\alpha$ is maximal) while $J_\alpha(t)$ is smaller. Thus, the exact cancellation $J_\alpha(t)^2 / J_\alpha(t)^2 = 1$ fails. The integral $\int_{j'_{\alpha,1}}^{t_{max}} \frac{1}{t J_\alpha(t)^2} (\text{constant}) dt$ will be significantly larger than A2's polynomial bound, and it risks divergence as $t$ approaches the first zero $j_{\alpha,1}$.
