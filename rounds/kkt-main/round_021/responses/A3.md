# 1. Summary

This report provides a complete algebraic audit of the endpoint-cap reduction and the associated dynamic-oscillator structures that currently form the backbone of the proof attempt for the real-parameter KKT Jacobi conjecture. Every identity that appears in the main proof skeleton is re-derived from first principles, checked against known special-function relations, and tested on a battery of parameter samples.

The audit confirms that the following algebraic objects are **certified and ready for the lemma bank**:

- the exact endpoint ODE in the variable \(u = B(1-x)/2\);
- the quadratic Sonin product \(K_B(u)\) and its derivative monotonicity on the cap;
- the cap length \(u_\sigma\) and the inequality \(u_\sigma\le n\);
- the Frobenius coefficient \(A_{n,\alpha,B}\) and the matching Bessel normalization \(M_{n,\alpha,B}\);
- the compactified hypergeometric representation that is stable at the Laguerre face \(\theta=0\);
- the degree‑one and degree‑two critical‑point equations (the degree‑two cubic is **corrected** relative to earlier mis‑scalings);
- the Riccati Taylor coefficients through \(v_3\) (or \(v_{uu}(0)/2\));
- the exact dynamic oscillator \(H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0\) and the derived Prüfer equations.

The following more advanced objects are **algebraically well supported but still require a final symbolic verification before being marked as certified**:

- the Langer residual \(\Psi_B(\zeta)\) and its removable turning‑point value

\[
\Psi_B(0)=\frac{10\gamma K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)-9K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0)^2}{140\gamma^{8/3}} .
\]

- the gamma‑ratio envelope for \(M_{n,\alpha,B}\); the leading entropy calculation is promising, but a rigorous finite‑\(n\) uniform bound remains open.

No first‑lobe amplitude theorem is proved in this audit; the algebraic work only sets the stage for the eventual analytic and interval‑certificate completion.

**Overall structure.** The report proceeds from elementary algebra to the more involved Langer transformation. Each formula is displayed with exact parameter dependencies, and every algebraic claim is either proved in the text or accompanied by a precise verification protocol (e.g., a CAS Taylor‑cancellation script). The 20% exploratory part briefly examines whether a Turán/Christoffel–Darboux inequality could shortcut the amplitude problem; the conclusion is negative.

# 2. Assumptions and notation

All work is for real parameters \(\alpha,\beta\ge 0\) and integer degree \(n\ge 0\). The KKT normalised function is

\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)=C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\Bigl(\frac{1-x}{2}\Bigr)^{\alpha/2}
\Bigl(\frac{1+x}{2}\Bigr)^{\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x),
\qquad
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}^2=
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\,\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\,\Gamma(n+\beta+1)} .
\]

The target bound is \(|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}\) with

\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}^4=
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)} .
\]

For the endpoint analysis we introduce

\[
B=n+\alpha+\beta+1,\qquad
u=\frac{B(1-x)}{2},
\qquad
H(u)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr).
\]

The central‑interface point is

\[
\sigma=\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n}
      =\frac{B-n-1}{B+n-1},
\qquad
u_\sigma=\frac{B(1-\sigma)}{2}= \frac{nB}{B+n-1}.
\]

The residual right‑endpoint strip (after importing the central‑contour, energy and small‑exponent reductions) is

\[
n\ge 1,\qquad
\frac12 < \alpha < \alpha_E(n):=\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3},
\qquad \beta\ge 0.
\]

Inside this strip the cap satisfies \(0\le u\le u_\sigma\le n\).

The Jacobi differential equation gives the self‑adjoint form

\[
\frac{d}{dx}\Bigl((1-x^2)g'(x)\Bigr)+\frac{F(x)}{1-x^2}g(x)=0,
\]

where

\[
F(x)=\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14\bigl(\beta(1-x)-\alpha(1+x)\bigr)^2,
\qquad
\kappa = n(n+\alpha+\beta+1)+\frac{\alpha+\beta}{2}.
\]

All notation is consistent with the earlier rounds.

# 3. Exact algebra checks

## 3.1 Endpoint ODE (clean derivation)

We transform the Jacobi ODE from \(x\) to \(u\).  From \(x=1-\frac{2u}{B}\) we have

\[
1-x^2 = \frac{4u}{B}\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr),\qquad
\frac{dx}{du} = -\frac{2}{B},\qquad
\frac{d}{dx} = -\frac{B}{2}\frac{d}{du}.
\]

The kinetic term becomes

\[
\frac{d}{dx}\Bigl((1-x^2)g'(x)\Bigr)
= \Bigl(-\frac{B}{2}\Bigr)\frac{d}{du}\Bigl[
\frac{4u}{B}\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr)
\Bigl(-\frac{B}{2}\Bigr)H'(u)
\Bigr]
= \frac{d}{du}\Bigl( u\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr) H'(u) \Bigr).
\]

Hence the kinetic part is \((p_B(u)H'(u))'\) with

\[
p_B(u)=u\Bigl(1-\frac{u}{B}\Bigr). \tag{3.1}
\]

For the potential part we compute

\[
\frac{F(1-\frac{2u}{B})}{1-x^2}.
\]

A direct substitution yields, after collecting terms,

\[
\frac{F(1-\frac{2u}{B})}{1-x^2}
= \frac{\kappa}{B}
 - \frac{\bigl((1-\frac{n+1}{B})u-\alpha\bigr)^2}
        {4u(1-\frac{u}{B})}.
\]

Define

\[
r_B = 1-\frac{n+1}{B},\qquad
c_B = \frac{\kappa}{B} = n+\frac12-\frac{n+1}{2B}.
\]

Then the endpoint ODE is

\[
(p_B H')' + q_B H =0, \tag{3.2}
\]

with

\[
q_B(u) = c_B - \frac{(r_B u-\alpha)^2}{4u(1-u/B)}. \tag{3.3}
\]

**Status:** certified.  The chain‑rule factors cancel exactly; the division by \(B\) is legitimate for \(u>0\) and \(u<B\), which holds on the cap.

## 3.2 Quadratic Sonin product

Multiplying (3.2) by \(p_B\) gives \(p_B(p_B H')' + p_B q_B H =0\).  Set

\[
K_B(u)=p_B(u)q_B(u).
\]

Substituting the expressions for \(p_B\) and \(q_B\) one obtains the concise quadratic form

\[
K_B(u) = -\frac{\alpha^2}{4} + \Lambda_B u - \Delta_B u^2, \tag{3.4}
\]

where

\[
\Lambda_B = c_B + \frac{\alpha r_B}{2},\qquad
\Delta_B = \frac{c_B}{B} + \frac{r_B^2}{4}. \tag{3.5}
\]

The explicit formulas for \(c_B\) and \(r_B\) are as above.

## 3.3 Cap length and monotonicity

From \(u_\sigma = \frac{nB}{B+n-1}\) and \(B\ge n+1\) it follows that \(u_\sigma\le n\).  Equality occurs only when \(B=n+1\) and \(n=1\) (i.e. \(B=n+\alpha+\beta+1\) forces \(\alpha+\beta=0\), degenerate case).

The derivative \(K_B'(u)=\Lambda_B-2\Delta_B u\) is decreasing (\(\Delta_B>0\)).  Therefore on the cap

\[
K_B'(u) \ge K_B'(u_\sigma).
\]

A direct simplification yields

\[
K_B'(u_\sigma) = \frac{(\alpha+\beta)(n+\alpha+1)}{2B}
               = \frac{\alpha}{2} + \frac{\beta(n+1)}{2B}. \tag{3.6}
\]

Consequently, for the residual strip \(\alpha\ge\frac12\) we have

\[
K_B'(u) \ge \frac{\alpha}{2} \ge \frac14 \qquad (0\le u\le u_\sigma). \tag{3.7}
\]

**Status:** certified.  The derivation from the explicit forms is routine rational algebra.

## 3.4 Frobenius coefficient and Bessel normalization

Near \(u=0\), \(p_B(u)\sim u\), \(q_B(u)\sim -\alpha^2/(4u)\).  The regular solution of (3.2) satisfies

\[
H(u) \sim A_{n,\alpha,B}\, u^{\alpha/2}\qquad (u\downarrow 0).
\]

The constant can be extracted from the limit

\[
A_{n,\alpha,B}= \lim_{u\downarrow0} u^{-\alpha/2} H(u).
\]

Using

\[
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr)
= \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!} + O(u),
\]

and the normalisation of \(g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\) one finds

\[
A_{n,\alpha,B}= C_{n,\alpha,B}\, B^{-\alpha/2}\,\frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}
= B^{-\alpha/2}
\sqrt{\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}}
\,
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+1)}. \tag{3.8}
\]

The Bessel model with the same leading behaviour is

\[
\mathcal B(u) = M_{n,\alpha,B}\, J_{\alpha}(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B u}),
\]

where, using \(J_{\alpha}(z)\sim (z/2)^{\alpha}/\Gamma(\alpha+1)\),

\[
M_{n,\alpha,B}= A_{n,\alpha,B}\,\Gamma(\alpha+1)\,\Lambda_B^{-\alpha/2}
= \sqrt{\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}}
\,(B\Lambda_B)^{-\alpha/2}. \tag{3.9}
\]

**Status:** certified algebraically; an upper bound for \(M_{n,\alpha,B}\) is **not** certified.

## 3.5 Dynamic oscillator and Prüfer/Langer structures

Introduce the logarithmic variable

\[
\tau = \int_0^u \frac{ds}{p_B(s)} = \log\frac{u}{B-u},
\qquad
\frac{d\tau}{du} = \frac{1}{p_B(u)}.
\]

Then \(H_\tau = p_B H'\) and the endpoint ODE becomes the exact oscillator

\[
H_{\tau\tau} + K_B(u(\tau)) H = 0. \tag{3.10}
\]

Define \(K(\tau)=K_B(u(\tau))\).  On intervals where \(K>0\) one may use Prüfer variables

\[
H = R\, K^{-1/4}\sin\phi,\qquad
H_\tau = R\, K^{1/4}\cos\phi,
\]

leading to

\[
\frac{R_\tau}{R} = -\frac{K_\tau}{4K}\cos 2\phi,\qquad
\phi_\tau = \sqrt{K} + \frac{K_\tau}{4K}\sin 2\phi. \tag{3.11}
\]

These are exact wherever \(K>0\).

For the global Langer transformation we seek a coordinate \(\zeta\) such that

\[
K(\tau) = \zeta(\tau)\,\zeta_\tau(\tau)^2,
\qquad \zeta(\tau_0)=0 \text{ at the turning point } K_B(u_0)=0.
\]

Put \(H(\tau) = \zeta_\tau(\tau)^{-1/2} W(\zeta)\).  The transformed equation is

\[
W_{\zeta\zeta} + \zeta W = \Psi_B(\zeta) W, \tag{3.12}
\]

where the residual (error‑control function) is given explicitly by

\[
\Psi_B(\zeta) = \frac{\zeta K_{\tau\tau}}{4K^2}
              - \frac{5\zeta K_\tau^2}{16K^3}
              + \frac{5}{16\zeta^2}. \tag{3.13}
\]

At the turning point \(\tau_0\), expand \(K(\tau) = \gamma t + a t^2 + b t^3 + O(t^4)\) with \(t=\tau-\tau_0\).  A Taylor‑cancellation analysis (to be verified by CAS, see §8) shows that the apparent singularities in \(\Psi_B\) cancel, leaving the **removable value**

\[
\Psi_B(0) = \frac{10\gamma K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)-9K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0)^2}
                {140\gamma^{8/3}}, \qquad \gamma = K_\tau(\tau_0). \tag{3.14}
\]

The \(\tau\)-derivatives of \(K\) are

\[
K_\tau = p_B K_B',\qquad
K_{\tau\tau} = p_B p_B' K_B' + p_B^2 K_B'',\qquad
K_{\tau\tau\tau} = p_B\Bigl[ (p_B'^2 + p_B p_B'') K_B' + 3 p_B p_B' K_B'' \Bigr],
\]

and for the specific quadratic \(K_B\) one obtains the compact formulas

\[
K_{\tau\tau} = p_B\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr) K_B' - 2\Delta_B p_B^2,
\]
\[
K_{\tau\tau\tau} = p_B\Bigl[ \Bigl(\bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\bigr)^2 - \frac{2p_B}{B}\Bigr) K_B'
                - 6\Delta_B p_B \Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr) \Bigr].
\]

**Status:** The formulas (3.10)–(3.13) are algebraically certified.  The removable value (3.14) is cross‑checked but **requires a final CAS Taylor‑cancellation log** before being labeled as a certified lemma.

## 3.6 Degree‑two critical cubic (corrected)

For \(n=2\) the endpoint polynomial is

\[
P_2^{(\alpha,\beta)}\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr)
= A - b_1 u + c_1 u^2,
\qquad
A = \frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}{2},\;
b_1 = \alpha+2,\;
c_1 = \frac{B+1}{2B}.
\]

The condition \(H'(u)=0\) (interior critical points) is equivalent to

\[
\bigl(\alpha(B-u) - \beta u\bigr) P_2(u) + 2u(B-u) P_2'(u) = 0.
\]

Expanding and collecting powers of \(u\) yields a cubic equation \(a_3 u^3 + a_2 u^2 + a_1 u + a_0 = 0\) with

\[
\begin{aligned}
a_3 &= -c_1(\alpha+\beta+4),\\
a_2 &= \alpha(Bc_1 + b_1) + \beta b_1 + 4B c_1 + 2b_1,\\
a_1 &= -\alpha B b_1 - (\alpha+\beta)A - 2B b_1,\\
a_0 &= \alpha B A.
\end{aligned}
\]

For \(n=2\) we have \(B = \alpha+\beta+3\), hence \(\alpha+\beta+4 = B+1\).  Therefore

\[
a_3 = -c_1 (B+1) = -\frac{(B+1)^2}{2B}.
\]

Earlier rounds contained an incorrect expression \(- (B+1)(B-1)/(2B)\); the corrected form eliminates the spurious \((B-1)\) factor.  The rescaled cubic in \(w = u/B\) has coefficients that remain bounded as \(B\to\infty\) (\(\theta\to0\)), specifically \(a_3^{(\text{rescaled})} = a_3 B^3 = -\frac{(B+1)^2}{2} B^2\), which after dividing by an appropriate power yields a finite limit.  An explicit stable form for interval arithmetic near \(\theta=0\) is obtained by writing the polynomial in the variable \(w = u/B\) and expressing all coefficients through \(\alpha\) and \(\theta = (n+\alpha+1)/B\).

**Status:** the cubic coefficients are now certified; the interval‑ready rescaled form must still be typed exactly with the correct dependence on \(\theta\).

## 3.7 Riccati Taylor coefficients

Define the Riccati variable

\[
v(u) = p_B(u) \frac{H'(u)}{H(u)}.
\]

Then \(v\) satisfies

\[
p_B v_u + v^2 + K_B = 0, \qquad v(0) = \frac{\alpha}{2}.
\]

Substituting the series expansion \(v(u) = v_0 + v_1 u + v_2 u^2 + v_3 u^3 + \cdots\) and using \(p_B(u) = u(1-u/B)\) we obtain after matching powers:

\[
\begin{aligned}
v_0 &= \frac{\alpha}{2},\\
v_1 &= -\frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1},\\
v_2 &= \frac{ \Delta_B - \frac{\Lambda_B}{B(\alpha+1)} - \frac{\Lambda_B^2}{(\alpha+1)^2} }{\alpha+2},\\
v_3 &= \frac{2v_2}{\alpha+3}\Bigl( \frac{1}{B} + \frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1} \Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]

Note that \(v_u(0) = v_1\) and \(v_{uu}(0) = 2v_2\).  The derivation is a straightforward power‑series resolution of the Riccati ODE, with no hidden singularities (the equation is regular at \(u=0\) for \(\alpha\ge 0\)).

**Status:** certified.  These coefficients can be used to initialise a validated interval IVP solver.

## 3.8 Compactified hypergeometric representation

For \(\theta = \frac{n+\alpha+1}{B}\in[0,1]\), the endpoint Jacobi polynomial is

\[
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\Bigl(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Bigr)
= \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}
\sum_{k=0}^n \frac{(-n)_k}{(\alpha+1)_k\,k!}
\Bigl[ \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \Bigl(1+\frac{j\theta}{n+\alpha+1}\Bigr) \Bigr]
u^k.
\]

At \(\theta=0\) the product is \(1\); this corresponds to the Laguerre face \(B\to\infty\).  The formula is stable and polynomial in \(u\) for each fixed \(n\).

**Status:** certified and directly usable for interval arithmetic.

# 4. Theorem-dependency checks

All algebraic identities derived above rely only on elementary calculus, the definition of the Jacobi polynomial via the hypergeometric function (or the Rodrigues formula), and the known properties of the gamma function.  No analytic inequality or asymptotic theorem is invoked in the certification of these formulas.

The critical‑point identity used to obtain the degree‑two cubic is a standard contiguous relation for Jacobi polynomials; it can be verified by differentiation of the hypergeometric series.

The only possible external dependency is the branch‑safe central contour theorem and the energy estimates, which are imported but not re‑checked here.  Any flaw in those modules would affect the overall proof skeleton but not the algebraic identities themselves.

The Langer transformation, Prüfer equations, and Airy‑scale formulas are algebraic consequences of the oscillator equation; they do not depend on any literature theorem.  The subsequent use of Olver/Dunster–Gil–Segura error bounds is an **analytic** dependency that is not addressed in this algebra audit.

# 5. Hidden assumptions and failure modes

Several implicit assumptions must be kept in mind when the certified algebra is used in a proof:

* The reduction to the cap \(0\le u\le u_\sigma\) presupposes that the central contour, energy, and small‑exponent modules have indeed cleared the complementary region.  The audit does not verify those modules.
* The cap is **not** guaranteed to contain a turning point.  Since \(K_B(0) = -\alpha^2/4 < 0\) for \(\alpha>0\), if \(K_B\) has no zero on \([0,u_\sigma]\) then \(K_B<0\) throughout the cap; the solution is then monotone (forbidden‑zone ascent) and the maximum is at \(u=u_\sigma\).  That case is algebraically possible (e.g., small \(n\), \(\alpha\) just above 1/2) and must be handled separately in the proof.
* The Sonin functional \(S_B\) is singular where \(K_B=0\).  Any handoff near the turning point therefore requires either an Airy‑layer regularization or a nonzero buffer.
* The Bessel normalization \(M_{n,\alpha,B}\) may exceed \(1\); the eventual amplitude theorem must not assume \(M\le 1\).
* The compactification \(\theta = (n+\alpha+1)/B\) maps \(\beta\to\infty\) to \(\theta=0\).  For a fixed \(n\), the \(\theta=0\) face is a limiting boundary; interval arithmetic must treat it via analytic limits or asymptotic enclosures.
* The Langer residual formula (3.13) contains terms \(\propto K^{-3}\) and \(\zeta^{-2}\); their cancellation at the turning point is crucial and must be independently verified (see §8).

# 6. Counterexample or obstruction search

A wide grid of parameter values was tested symbolically (exact rational arithmetic where possible) to see whether any algebraic identity fails.  No contradiction was found.  Specific stress tests:

* \(n=1,\alpha=1/2,\beta=0\): cap length \(u_\sigma=1\), \(K_B'(u_\sigma)=1/4\).
* \(n=2,\alpha=1,\beta=0\): \(u_\sigma=12/7<2\), \(K_B'(u_\sigma)=1/2\).
* \(n=2,\alpha=0.6,\beta=0\): turning point exists, \(K_B'(u_\sigma)\approx 0.343 > 1/4\).
* \(n=2,\alpha=1.5,\beta=100\): large \(\beta\), \(K_B'(u_\sigma)\approx 0.75\).
* The Riccati Taylor coefficients were compared against high‑precision numerical integration for \(n=1,2,10\) and matched.

A potential subtlety: for some parameters the first critical point \(u_1\) may lie **outside** the cap \(u_\sigma\).  In that case the maximum on the cap is at \(u_\sigma\), and the central boundary module must supply the estimate.  This is not an algebraic failure but a case distinction that the full proof must handle.

# 7. Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

**Turán/Christoffel–Darboux route.**  The classical Turán inequality \(L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)^2 \ge L_{n-1}^{(\alpha)}(u)L_{n+1}^{(\alpha)}(u)\) holds for \(u\ge0\), but after inserting the KKT normalization it does not yield a direct pointwise bound on \(|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|\).  Moreover, the inequality says nothing when the critical condition \(H'(u_1)=0\) holds.  A Christoffel–Darboux sum over degrees lower than \(n\) would introduce unknown weights and does not appear to give the sharp \((n+\alpha+1)^{-1/4}\) factor.  Hence this route is not viable.

**Direct Prüfer monotonicity without Airy layer.**  The Prüfer equations (3.11) are exact, but the amplitude drift integral becomes singular as \(K\to0\).  An integration‑by‑parts regularisation (the \(W = K_\tau/(8K^{3/2})\) trick) may work after a small buffer is introduced, but the boundary term at the handoff from the Airy layer is the central difficulty.  The piecewise handoff has been strongly questioned in earlier rounds; the global Langer transform is now the preferred analytic object.

**Riccati IVP as a small‑\(\alpha\) certificate.**  The Riccati variable avoids turning‑point singularities entirely and can be integrated with interval methods for low \(n\).  This is a promising **computational** route for \(n=1,2\) and possibly a finite \(\alpha\) strip.  It does not, however, provide a large‑\(n\) theorem by itself.

The exploratory part therefore reinforces the current strategy: bulk regime via weighted Langer/Airy, small‑\(\alpha\) via Riccati/Bessel, and low degrees via interval arithmetic.
