## Theorem-Dependency Audit

*   **Stirling's Approximation:** A1 uses the strict lower bound $\Gamma(x) > \sqrt{2\pi} x^{x-1/2} \exp(-x + \frac{1}{12x+1})$ for $x>0$. This is a standard, rigorously established inequality (e.g., Robbins 1955, "A Remark on Stirling's Formula", *The American Mathematical Monthly*, Vol. 62, No. 1, pp. 26-29) and is correctly applied at $x=16/5$.
*   **Digamma and Trigamma Inequalities:** A1 uses $\psi(x) < \log x$ and $\psi'(x) < \frac{1}{x-1}$ for $x>1$. These are standard properties of the polygamma functions, derivable from their integral representations, and are correctly applied.
*   **Landau's Bessel Monotonicity:** Cited correctly by A1 to support the reduction of the global Bessel supremum to the half-order case. The exact hypothesis is that the magnitude of $J_\nu(x)$ at its positive stationary points is strictly decreasing with respect to the order $\nu > 0$.
*   **Dunster-Gil-Segura (DGS):** Cited correctly by A1 as the necessary framework for the bulk Langer/Airy track. The required theorem must provide explicit error bounds for Airy-type expansions of linear second-order equations with a simple turning point. This dependency remains uninstantiated with exact KKT constants.

The theorem audit has two separate levels. The first level is the theorem name and the allowed range of variables. On this level, the $n=1$ certificate is in good shape: the Gamma input is used at $x=16/5$, safely inside the positive range; the logarithmic derivative inequalities are invoked only on intervals where the arguments stay above $1$; and the endpoint comparisons are reduced to finite rational arithmetic. The second level is the conversion of analytic inequalities into a reproducible certificate. Here the judge should insist that every non-rational quantity in the appendix be enclosed by rational upper or lower bounds before it is used in a sign decision. A displayed decimal such as $2.42256$ is useful for orientation, but the archived proof should preserve the exact rational enclosure that makes the final integer comparison independent of floating-point evaluation.

The Bessel and DGS dependencies are not at the same certification level. Landau-type monotonicity can be a valid theorem input only after the order range, the relevant stationary point convention, and the normalization of $J_\nu$ are written in the exact form needed by the rational-Bessel reduction. A proof cannot silently move from a global Bessel maximum statement to a first-critical-point statement for the perturbed Jacobi equation; the missing phase comparison is precisely the obstruction. Similarly, an Olver/DGS Airy theorem is not a proof until the KKT normal form has a named simple turning point, a verified sign convention, an explicit variation integral, and constants that remain finite uniformly over the residual strip. Therefore these references should be treated as theorem needs, not as accepted modules.
