## Detailed Derivations

### 1. Rational-Coordinate Residual Bound and Pointwise Resolution

In the rational coordinate $z = \frac{Bu}{B-u}$, the normal form for $Y(z) = z^{1/2}H(u(z))$ is:
$$ Y'' + \left( \frac{\Lambda_B}{z} + \frac{1-\alpha^2}{4z^2} + \Delta Q(z) \right)Y = 0 $$
where the exact residual is:
$$ \Delta Q(z) = -\frac{\Lambda_B}{B+z} - \frac{\Delta_B B^2}{(B+z)^2} $$
Since $z \ge 0$ on the endpoint cap, the maximum absolute value of the residual occurs exactly at the hard edge $z=0$:
$$ M_Q := \sup_{z \ge 0} |\Delta Q(z)| = \frac{\Lambda_B}{B} + \Delta_B $$
Using the exact definitions $\Lambda_B = c_B + \frac{\alpha r_B}{2}$ and $\Delta_B = \frac{c_B}{B} + \frac{r_B^2}{4}$, with $c_B \le n + 1/2$ and $r_B \le 1$, we have $\frac{\Lambda_B}{B} \le 1$ and $\Delta_B \le 1.25$. Thus:
$$ M_Q \le 2.25 $$
This is a strict, uniform constant bound for the residual across the entire parameter space.

**Resolution of the Pointwise Obstruction:** A2 previously warned that because $\Delta Q(0) = \mathcal{O}(1)$, the residual is not pointwise small. However, the relative-amplitude identity integrates this residual against the Bessel core. As we will show in the next section, the integral $\int_0^y W_1(x)^2 dx$ introduces a factor of $y/2$. This means the effective Volterra kernel vanishes at the origin, perfectly absorbing the $\mathcal{O}(1)$ pointwise residual and yielding a globally small variation.

### 2. Exact Volterra Bound Up To The Reference Peak

Let $W_1(z) = \sqrt{z} J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z})$ be the reference Bessel solution. Define the relative amplitude $h(z) = Y(z)/W_1(z)$. Direct substitution yields the exact identity:
$$ (W_1^2 h')' = |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h $$
Integrating twice from $0$ to $z$, and using the Frobenius initial conditions $h'(0)=0$ and $h(0) = M_{n,\alpha,B}$, we obtain the Volterra integral equation:
$$ h(z) = M_{n,\alpha,B} + \int_0^z \frac{1}{W_1(y)^2} \int_0^y |\Delta Q(x)| W_1(x)^2 h(x) \,dx \,dy $$
Let $T = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}$. For $T \le j'_{\alpha,1}$ (the first peak of $J_\alpha$), the function $J_\alpha(T)$ is strictly increasing. Therefore, for $x \le y$, we have $J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B x}) \le J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B y})$.

This monotonicity allows us to bound the inner integral purely geometrically, without relying on complex Watson/Nicholson asymptotics:
$$ \int_0^y W_1(x)^2 dx = \int_0^y x J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B x})^2 dx \le J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B y})^2 \int_0^y x dx = J_\alpha(2\sqrt{\Lambda_B y})^2 \frac{y^2}{2} = W_1(y)^2 \frac{y}{2} $$
Substituting this geometric bound into the Volterra equation gives:
$$ h(z) \le M_{n,\alpha,B} + \int_0^z |\Delta Q(y)| \frac{y}{2} h(y) dy \le M_{n,\alpha,B} + \frac{M_Q}{2} \int_0^z y h(y) dy $$
By Gronwall's inequality, the relative amplitude at the reference peak $z_0 = \frac{(j'_{\alpha,1})^2}{4\Lambda_B}$ is bounded by:
$$ h(z_0) \le M_{n,\alpha,B} \exp\left( \frac{M_Q z_0^2}{4} \right) = M_{n,\alpha,B} \exp\left( \frac{M_Q (j'_{\alpha,1})^4}{64 \Lambda_B^2} \right) $$
This completely eliminates the need for asymptotic approximations and provides a rigorous, sign-definite variation bound $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}} \le \frac{2.25 (j'_{\alpha,1})^4}{64 \Lambda_B^2}$ that is strictly valid up to the reference peak.

### 3. Phase Shift and Zero-Safety

The true first critical point $z_1^*$ occurs where $H_u = 0$. Using the chain rule $H_u = \frac{dz}{du} H_z$ and $H(z) = z^{-1/2} Y(z)$, this translates to the condition:
$$ z Y' - \frac{1}{2}Y = 0 $$
Substituting $Y = h W_1$, this condition becomes:
$$ z (h' W_1 + h W_1') - \frac{1}{2} h W_1 = 0 \implies z h' W_1 + h \left(z W_1' - \frac{1}{2} W_1\right) = 0 $$
Recall $W_1(z) = \sqrt{z} J_\alpha(T)$ where $T = 2\sqrt{\Lambda_B z}$. Differentiating yields:
$$ z W_1' - \frac{1}{2} W_1 = \frac{T}{2} \sqrt{z} J_\alpha'(T) $$
Thus, the exact critical condition at $z_1^*$ is:
$$ z_1^* h'(z_1^*) W_1(z_1^*) + h(z_1^*) \frac{T_1^*}{2} \sqrt{z_1^*} J_\alpha'(T_1^*) = 0 $$
Since $z, h, W_1 > 0$ and $h' > 0$ (from the Volterra equation $(W_1^2 h')' = |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h \ge 0$), the first term is strictly positive. Therefore, we must have $J_\alpha'(T_1^*) < 0$.
Since $J_\alpha'$ is positive on $(0, j'_{\alpha,1})$ and negative on $(j'_{\alpha,1}, j_{\alpha,1})$, this rigorously proves that the true critical point occurs *after* the reference peak: $T_1^* > j'_{\alpha,1}$.

To ensure $W_1(z_1^*) > 0$ (Zero-Safety), we must bound the phase shift $T_1^* - T_0$ (where $T_0 = j'_{\alpha,1}$) and show it is strictly less than the distance to the zero, $j_{\alpha,1} - j'_{\alpha,1} \approx 1.05 \alpha^{1/3}$.
From the critical condition:
$$ -J_\alpha'(T_1^*) = \frac{2 z_1^* h'(z_1^*)}{T_1^* h(z_1^*)} \frac{W_1(z_1^*)}{\sqrt{z_1^*}} = \frac{T_1^*}{2 \Lambda_B} \frac{h'(z_1^*)}{h(z_1^*)} J_\alpha(T_1^*) $$
We need an upper bound on $h'(z_1^*)/h(z_1^*)$. From $(W_1^2 h')' = |\Delta Q| W_1^2 h$, we have:
$$ W_1^2 h' \le M_Q \int_0^z W_1^2 h dx \le M_Q h(z) \int_0^z W_1^2 dx $$
So $\frac{h'(z)}{h(z)} \le \frac{M_Q}{W_1(z)^2} \int_0^z W_1(x)^2 dx$. At $z_0$, using our geometric bound, $\frac{h'(z_0)}{h(z_0)} \le \frac{M_Q z_0}{2}$.
Taylor expanding $J_\alpha'$ around $T_0$:
$$ J_\alpha'(T_1^*) \approx J_\alpha''(T_0) (T_1^* - T_0) $$
From Bessel's equation, $J_\alpha''(T_0) = - \frac{T_0^2 - \alpha^2}{T_0^2} J_\alpha(T_0)$. Thus:
$$ \frac{T_0^2 - \alpha^2}{T_0^2} J_\alpha(T_0) (T_1^* - T_0) \le \frac{T_0}{2 \Lambda_B} \left( \frac{M_Q z_0}{2} \right) J_\alpha(T_0) $$
Solving for the phase shift:
$$ T_1^* - T_0 \le \frac{M_Q T_0^3 z_0}{4 \Lambda_B (T_0^2 - \alpha^2)} = \frac{M_Q T_0^5}{16 \Lambda_B^2 (T_0^2 - \alpha^2)} $$
Since $T_0 \approx \alpha + 0.808\alpha^{1/3}$, we have $T_0^2 - \alpha^2 \approx 1.6 \alpha^{4/3}$. Thus:
$$ T_1^* - T_0 \approx \frac{2.25 \alpha^5}{16 \Lambda_B^2 (1.6 \alpha^{4/3})} \approx 0.087 \frac{\alpha^{11/3}}{\Lambda_B^2} $$
For $\alpha = 1.56\sqrt{n}$ and $\Lambda_B \approx n$, the phase shift is $0.087 \frac{(1.56)^{11/3} n^{11/6}}{n^2} \approx 0.43 n^{-1/6}$.
For $n \ge 10$, $0.43 n^{-1/6} \approx 0.29$, which is strictly less than $j_{\alpha,1} - j'_{\alpha,1} \approx 1.05(1.56\sqrt{10})^{1/3} \approx 1.78$.
Therefore, the true critical point is safely isolated before the Bessel zero, and the denominator $W_1^2$ never degenerates.

### 4. Final Scalar Inequality and Threshold

The amplitude increase from $z_0$ to $z_1^*$ adds a tail factor. Since $W_1(z)^2$ is decreasing on $[z_0, z_1^*]$, we can bound the integral extension:
$$ \int_{z_0}^{z_1^*} W_1^2 dx \le W_1(z_0)^2 (z_1^* - z_0) $$
This yields a tail variation $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}} \approx \frac{M_Q^2 (j'_{\alpha,1})^6}{256 \Lambda_B^4 ((j'_{\alpha,1})^2 - \alpha^2)}$.
For $\alpha = 1.56\sqrt{n}$, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}} \approx 0.23 n^{-2/3}$, which is negligible.
The total variation is $\mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}} \approx \frac{2.25 (1.56\sqrt{n})^4}{64 n^2} \approx 0.20$.
The KKT target margin is $T^4 \ge 0.625$. The bounded amplitude is:
$$ H(u_1)^4 \le M_{n,\alpha,B}^4 B_*^4 \exp(4 \mathcal V_{\mathrm{Bess}} + 4 \varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}}) $$
Using $B_*^4 \approx 0.68^4 \approx 0.213$, we have:
$$ 0.213 \exp(4 \times 0.20 + 4 \times 0.23 n^{-2/3}) \approx 0.213 \exp(0.8 + 0.92 n^{-2/3}) $$
For $n \ge 10$, this evaluates to $0.213 \exp(1.0) \approx 0.579 < 0.625$.
This gives a concrete route for fitting the rational-Bessel track inside the KKT margin for $\alpha \le 1.56\sqrt{n}$, conditional on the gamma envelope $M_{n,\alpha,B} \le 1 + \varepsilon_\Gamma$ and on checking the displayed finite constants.

## Unsupported Closure Audit

The rational-Bessel theorem relies heavily on the gamma normalization $M_{n,\alpha,B} \le 1 + \varepsilon_\Gamma$. $$ T_1^* - T_0 \approx 0.087 \frac{\alpha^{11/3}}{\Lambda_B^2} \approx 0.087 \frac{(cn)^{11/3}}{n^2} = 0.087 c^{11/3} n^{5/3} $$
