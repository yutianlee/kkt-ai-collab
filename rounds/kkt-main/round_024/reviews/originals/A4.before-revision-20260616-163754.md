# Agent-by-agent review of A1

A1 provides a comprehensive synthesis of the lemma bank, a formal statement of the $n=1$ residual theorem, corrected $n=2$ formulas, and an allowed-side Airy propagation lemma. The $n=1$ proof is a genuine artifact, correctly leveraging the scalar envelope $E(\alpha)$ and standard gamma inequalities. The $n=2$ sign bracket proving the existence of a critical point in the cap is a valuable topological guarantee, though it does not isolate the *first* critical point after the turning point $u_0$, which is required for the Sonin reduction.

However, A1's allowed-side Airy envelope theorem contains a severe mathematical error in the matrix norm bound. A1 defines $\mathfrak{m}(\zeta)^2 = \operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)^2 + \operatorname{Bi}(-\zeta)^2$ and claims that the variation of parameters yields a growth bound controlled by $\pi \mathfrak{m}(\zeta)^2$. Specifically, A1 asserts that $\|A^{-1} (0,1)^T\| \|A\| \le \pi \mathfrak{m}^2$. 

Let us compute the Euclidean norms exactly. The inverse matrix applied to the vector is $A^{-1} (0,1)^T = \pi (-\operatorname{Bi}(-\zeta), \operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta))^T$, which has Euclidean norm exactly $\pi \mathfrak{m}(\zeta)$. The matrix $A$ has Frobenius norm $\|A\|_F = \sqrt{\operatorname{Ai}^2 + \operatorname{Bi}^2 + \operatorname{Ai}'^2 + \operatorname{Bi}'^2} = \sqrt{\mathfrak{m}^2 + \mathfrak{m}'^2}$. 

For large $\zeta > 0$, the asymptotic behavior of the Airy functions gives $\mathfrak{m}^2 \sim \pi^{-1} \zeta^{-1/2}$ while $\mathfrak{m}'^2 \sim \pi^{-1} \zeta^{1/2}$. Therefore, $\|A\|_F \sim \pi^{-1/2} \zeta^{1/4}$, and the product of the norms scales as $\pi \mathfrak{m} \sqrt{\mathfrak{m}^2 + \mathfrak{m}'^2} \sim \pi (\pi^{-1/2} \zeta^{-1/4}) (\pi^{-1/2} \zeta^{1/4}) = 1$. A1's bound of $\pi \mathfrak{m}^2 \sim \zeta^{-1/2}$ is off by a factor of $\zeta^{1/2}$. This missing derivative term is exactly why Olver and Dunster--Gil--Segura introduce specialized weight functions $\Omega(\zeta)$ to balance the $\zeta^{1/4}$ and $\zeta^{-1/4}$ behaviors. A1's crude Euclidean norm bound is [LIKELY-FALSE] for large $\zeta$ and cannot be used to close the bulk Langer track.

A2 then bounds the Volterra variation by majorizing the inner integral $\int_0^t \tau^3 J_\alpha(\tau)^2 d\tau \le J_\alpha(t)^2 \frac{t^4}{4}$. This step requires $J_\alpha(\tau) \le J_\alpha(t)$ for all $\tau \in [0, t]$, which is rigorously true only for $t \le j_{\alpha,1}'$ (the first peak of $J_\alpha$). However, A2 integrates up to $t_1^*$, the first critical point of the perturbed solution $Y(z)$. Because the potential for $Y$ is more negative than for $W_1$ (since $\Delta Q < 0$), Sturm comparison dictates that $Y$ oscillates slower, meaning $t_1^* > j_{\alpha,1}'$. 

For $t > j_{\alpha,1}'$, the function $J_\alpha(t)$ is decreasing, so the bound $J_\alpha(\tau) \le J_\alpha(t)$ fails for $\tau$ near $j_{\alpha,1}'$. The inner integral approaches a constant $C_\alpha \sim \alpha^4$, while the outer integrand behaves like $\frac{C_\alpha}{t J_\alpha(t)^2}$. As $t$ approaches the first zero $j_{\alpha,1}$, $J_\alpha(t) \to 0$, and the integral diverges. 

A2 claims the phase shift $\delta = t_1^* - j_{\alpha,1}'$ is $\mathcal{O}(\alpha^4/n^2)$, keeping $t_1^*$ safely away from $j_{\alpha,1}$. While this is physically plausible, it is currently [HEURISTIC]. A2 must rigorously bound $t_1^*$ away from $j_{\alpha,1}$ to prevent the singularity in the Volterra kernel. Until this phase-shift bound is formalized, the $\mathcal{O}(\alpha^4/n^2)$ scaling remains [DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS].
