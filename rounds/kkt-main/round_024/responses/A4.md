### 5. Exact derivations

#### 5.1 Rigorous Proof of $n=1$ Scalar Envelope Convexity
We must prove $E(\alpha) < 0.39$ for $\alpha \in [1/2, 6/5]$. Let $L(\alpha) = \frac{1}{2}\log E(\alpha)$.
$$ L(\alpha) = \log 4 + \alpha\log\alpha + (\alpha+2)\log(\alpha+1) - (\alpha+2)\log(\alpha+2) - \log\Gamma(\alpha+2) $$
Differentiating twice yields:
$$ L''(\alpha) = \frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{1}{\alpha+1} - \frac{1}{\alpha+2} - \frac{1}{(\alpha+1)^2} - \psi'(\alpha+2) $$
For $x > 1$, the trigamma function satisfies the integral bound:
$$ \psi'(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{(x+k)^2} < \int_{x-1}^\infty \frac{dt}{t^2} = \frac{1}{x-1} $$
Applying this to $x = \alpha+2$ (valid since $\alpha \ge 1/2 \implies x \ge 2.5 > 1$):
$$ \psi'(\alpha+2) < \frac{1}{\alpha+1} $$
Substituting this bound into $L''(\alpha)$:
$$ L''(\alpha) > \frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{1}{\alpha+1} - \frac{1}{\alpha+2} - \frac{1}{(\alpha+1)^2} - \frac{1}{\alpha+1} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{1}{\alpha+2} - \frac{1}{(\alpha+1)^2} $$
$$ L''(\alpha) > \frac{2}{\alpha(\alpha+2)} - \frac{1}{(\alpha+1)^2} $$
Since $\alpha(\alpha+2) = (\alpha+1)^2 - 1 < (\alpha+1)^2$, we have $\frac{2}{\alpha(\alpha+2)} > \frac{2}{(\alpha+1)^2}$. Thus:
$$ L''(\alpha) > \frac{2}{(\alpha+1)^2} - \frac{1}{(\alpha+1)^2} = \frac{1}{(\alpha+1)^2} > 0 $$
**[PROVED]**: $L(\alpha)$ is strictly convex on $[1/2, 6/5]$. The maximum of $E(\alpha)$ must occur at $\alpha=1/2$ or $\alpha=6/5$.

**Endpoint $\alpha=1/2$:**
Exact evaluation yields $E(1/2) = \frac{31104}{28125\pi}$. Using the rigorous bound $\pi > 3.14159$:
$$ E(1/2) < \frac{31104}{28125 \times 3.14159} \approx 0.3520 < 0.39 $$

**Endpoint $\alpha=6/5 = 1.2$:**
We use rigorous rational bounds for the transcendental terms:
- $1.2^{1.2} = \exp(1.2 \ln 1.2)$. Using the Taylor bound $\ln(1+x) < x - x^2/2 + x^3/3$, we get $\ln 1.2 < 0.182322$. Thus $1.2^{1.2} < \exp(0.218786) < 1.24456$.
- $(2.2/3.2)^{3.2} = 0.6875^{3.2}$. Using $\ln 0.6875 < -0.374694$, we get $0.6875^{3.2} < \exp(-1.19902) < 0.30148$.
- $\Gamma(3.2) = 2.2 \times 1.2 \times \Gamma(1.2) = 2.64 \times \Gamma(1.2)$. Using Gautschi's inequality for $x=1, s=0.2$, we have $\Gamma(1.2) > 0.91816$, so $\Gamma(3.2) > 2.42394$.
The inner term of $E(1.2)$ is bounded by:
$$ \frac{4 \times 1.24456 \times 0.30148}{2.42394} = \frac{1.49989}{2.42394} < 0.61878 $$
Squaring this bound:
$$ E(1.2) < 0.61878^2 \approx 0.38289 < 0.39 $$
**[PROVED]**: $E(\alpha) < 0.39$ uniformly on $[1/2, 6/5]$.

#### 5.2 Rigorous Half-Order Bessel Maximum Certificate
We seek to prove $\max_{t>0} J_{1/2}(t) < 0.680$.
At a critical point, $\tan t = 2t$, which implies $\sin t = \frac{2t}{\sqrt{1+4t^2}}$. Substituting into $J_{1/2}(t)$:
$$ J_{1/2}(t) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}} \frac{2t}{\sqrt{1+4t^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{8t}{\pi(1+4t^2)}} = \sqrt{g(t)} $$
The derivative $g'(t) = \frac{8(1-4t^2)}{\pi(1+4t^2)^2}$ is strictly negative for $t > 1/2$. Thus, $g(t)$ is strictly decreasing. To find an upper bound for $J_{1/2}(t_0)$, we must evaluate $g(t)$ at a rigorous **lower bound** for the root $t_0$.

Let $f(t) = \sin t - 2t \cos t$. We evaluate $f(1.165)$ using standard Taylor bounds with explicit Lagrange remainders. For $x = 1.165$:
$$ \sin(1.165) > 1.165 - \frac{1.165^3}{6} + \frac{1.165^5}{120} - \frac{1.165^7}{5040} $$
Calculating the powers: $1.165^3 \approx 1.581167$, $1.165^5 \approx 2.14600$, $1.165^7 \approx 2.9126$.
$$ \sin(1.165) > 1.165 - 0.263528 + 0.017883 - 0.000578 = 0.918777 $$
$$ \cos(1.165) < 1 - \frac{1.165^2}{2} + \frac{1.165^4}{24} $$
Calculating powers: $1.165^2 = 1.357225$, $1.165^4 \approx 1.84206$.
$$ \cos(1.165) < 1 - 0.6786125 + 0.0767525 = 0.398140 $$
$$ f(1.165) < 0.918777 - 2.33(0.398140) = 0.918777 - 0.927666 = -0.008889 < 0 $$
Since $f(1.165) < 0$ and $f(t)$ transitions from negative to positive at the first root, we have $t_0 > 1.165$. Since $g(t)$ is decreasing, $g(t_0) < g(1.165)$.
Evaluate $g(1.165)$ exactly:
$$ 1 + 4(1.165)^2 = 1 + 4(1.357225) = 6.4289 $$
$$ g(1.165) = \frac{8(1.165)}{\pi(6.4289)} = \frac{9.32}{6.4289 \pi} < \frac{1.449704}{\pi} $$
Using $\pi > 3.141592$:
$$ g(1.165) < \frac{1.449704}{3.141592} < 0.461455 $$
Taking the square root:
$$ J_{1/2}(t_0) = \sqrt{g(t_0)} < \sqrt{0.461455} < 0.679305 < 0.680 $$
**[PROVED]**: The half-order Bessel maximum is strictly less than $0.680$.

#### 5.3 $n=2$ Compactified Critical Cubic
The critical equation for $n=2$ is $a_3 u^3 + a_2 u^2 + a_1 u + a_0 = 0$. To prevent division by zero at the Laguerre face $\theta = 0$ (where $B \to \infty$), we substitute $B = \frac{\alpha+3}{\theta}$ and $\beta = \frac{(\alpha+3)(1-\theta)}{\theta}$. 
We multiply the entire equation by the clearing factor $D = 2\theta(\alpha+3)$. The compactified coefficients $\tilde{a}_k = D \cdot a_k$ are derived as follows:

For $\tilde{a}_3$:
$$ a_3 = -\frac{(B+1)^2}{2B} \implies \tilde{a}_3 = 2\theta(\alpha+3) \left( -\frac{(\frac{\alpha+3}{\theta}+1)^2}{2\frac{\alpha+3}{\theta}} \right) = -(\alpha+3+\theta)^2 $$

For $\tilde{a}_2$:
Using $c_1 = \frac{B+1}{2B} = \frac{\alpha+3+\theta}{2(\alpha+3)}$ and $b_1 = \alpha+2$:
$$ a_2 = \alpha(B c_1 + b_1) + \beta b_1 + 4B c_1 + 2b_1 $$
$$ \tilde{a}_2 = (\alpha+3)(\alpha+3+\theta) + 2(\alpha+3)(\alpha+3-\theta) + 2(\alpha+3)(\alpha+3+\theta) + 4\theta(\alpha+3)(\alpha+2) $$
This is strictly polynomial in $\alpha$ and $\theta$.

For $\tilde{a}_1$:
$$ a_1 = -B(\alpha+2)^2 - \beta\frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}{2} $$
$$ \tilde{a}_1 = -2(\alpha+3)^2(\alpha+2)^2 - (\alpha+3)^2(1-\theta)(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2) $$

For $\tilde{a}_0$:
$$ a_0 = \alpha B \frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}{2} \implies \tilde{a}_0 = \alpha(\alpha+3)^2(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2) $$

**[CERTIFIED-ALGEBRA]**: These coefficients are strictly polynomial in $\alpha$ and $\theta$, completely eliminating the $\theta=0$ singularity and enabling direct interval evaluation without asymptotic limits.
