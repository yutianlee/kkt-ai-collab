Detailed reasoning:

## 1. Formal theorem statement for the lemma bank

### Theorem L16.1: Conditional KKT endpoint proof from first-lobe amplitude

Assume the following imported modules.

1. **Central-contour clearance.** In the branch-safe central region, the strong KKT target holds.

2. **Weighted-energy clearance.** In the energy-dominated parameter region, the strong KKT target holds.

3. **Small-exponent theorem.** The right endpoint cap is controlled for $0\le\alpha\le1/2$, and by symmetry the left endpoint cap is controlled for $0\le\beta\le1/2$.

4. **Left-right symmetry.** The identity

$$
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(-x)=(-1)^nP_n^{(\beta,\alpha)}(x)
$$

transfers right endpoint statements to left endpoint statements.

5. **Right endpoint cap reduction.** In the residual right endpoint case,

$$
n\ge1,\qquad \alpha>1/2,\qquad \beta\ge0,
$$

the unresolved cap is

$$
0\le u\le u_\sigma=\frac{nB}{B+n-1}\le n.
$$

6. **Forbidden-zone ascent.** If $u_0$ is the first zero of $K_B$ in the cap, then the regular endpoint solution has no local maximum on $0<u<u_0$.

7. **Sonin ordering.** On $K_B>0$, local extrema are nonincreasing in amplitude as $u$ increases from the endpoint toward the central boundary.

8. **Exact dynamic oscillator.** With

$$
\tau=\int^u\frac{ds}{p_B(s)}
=
\log\frac{u}{B-u},
$$

one has

$$
H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0.
$$

Assume the remaining finite-$B$ first-lobe amplitude hypothesis:

### Hypothesis H16.1: finite-$B$ first-lobe amplitude

Let

$$
u_0
$$

be the first zero of $K_B$ in the cap, and let

$$
u_1>u_0
$$

be the first critical point of $H$ after $u_0$, if such a point exists. Then

$$
|H(u_1)|
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)B}{(n+\alpha+1)(B-\alpha)}
\right)^{1/4}.
$$

Then the strong KKT estimate holds for all $\alpha,\beta\ge0$ and all $x\in[-1,1]$.

### Proof of Theorem L16.1

The imported global modules remove the central region, the energy region, small right endpoint exponents, and the symmetric left endpoint cases. Thus it remains to consider a right endpoint residual cap with

$$
n\ge1,\qquad \alpha>1/2,\qquad \beta\ge0.
$$

On this cap,

$$
0\le u\le u_\sigma\le n.
$$

The endpoint equation and product monotonicity are exact. Since $K_B(0)=-\alpha^2/4<0$, either $K_B$ has no zero in the cap or it has a first zero $u_0$.

If $K_B$ has no zero in the cap, then $K_B<0$ throughout the cap. The forbidden-zone ascent argument applies on the whole cap: the regular solution is positive and increasing. Hence the cap maximum occurs at $u_\sigma$, which is already controlled by the central-contour boundary estimate.

If $K_B$ has a first zero $u_0$ but $u_0=u_\sigma$, then the allowed region is absent. Again the cap maximum is at the central boundary and is controlled.

If $K_B$ has a first zero $u_0<u_\sigma$, then the forbidden-zone ascent theorem excludes any local maximum on $(0,u_0)$. On $(u_0,u_\sigma)$, the Sonin functional

$$
S_B(u)=H(u)^2+\frac{p_B(u)H'(u)^2}{q_B(u)}
$$

satisfies

$$
S_B'(u)
=
-\frac{K_B'(u)}{q_B(u)^2}H'(u)^2
\le0.
$$

Therefore local extrema after $u_0$ are nonincreasing in amplitude as $u$ increases toward $u_\sigma$. If there is no critical point after $u_0$ before $u_\sigma$, then $H$ remains monotone and the cap maximum is again at $u_\sigma$, already controlled.

If the first critical point $u_1$ exists, every later critical point has no larger amplitude by Sonin ordering. Hence the cap is controlled by Hypothesis H16.1.

The left endpoint follows by symmetry. This proves the conditional theorem.

Status: **proved conditional on H16.1 and imported modules.**

### Supplement: explicit Sonin reduction details retained from the first A1 run

The conditional theorem rests on two local mechanisms that should remain visible in the lemma bank.

First, in the forbidden region $K_B<0$, write

$$
W(u)=p_B(u)H'(u).
$$

Since

$$
W'(u)=-q_B(u)H(u),
$$

and $q_B=K_B/p_B<0$, the regular branch satisfies $W'>0$ as long as $H>0$. Near the endpoint,

$$
H(u)\sim A_{n,\alpha,B}u^{\alpha/2},
\qquad A_{n,\alpha,B}>0,
$$

so $H>0$ and $W>0$ for sufficiently small $u>0$. A first-loss argument prevents either $H$ or $W$ from becoming nonpositive before the first zero of $K_B$. Thus there is no endpoint-side local maximum before $u_0$.

Second, on the allowed side $q_B>0$, the Sonin functional

$$
S_B(u)=H(u)^2+\frac{p_B(u)H'(u)^2}{q_B(u)}
$$

has derivative

$$
S_B'(u)
=-\frac{K_B'(u)}{q_B(u)^2}H'(u)^2.
$$

Because $K_B'(u)\ge K_B'(u_\sigma)\ge \alpha/2>0$ in the residual cap, $S_B$ is nonincreasing. At a local extremum of $H$, the derivative term vanishes and $S_B=H^2$. Therefore later allowed extrema cannot exceed the first allowed extremum. This is why H16.1 is exactly the missing endpoint inequality, rather than a broad global Laguerre statement.


## 2. Exact remaining analytic hypothesis

Conditional on the imported global modules, the only active endpoint analytic gap is H16.1. Written without prose:

For

$$
n\ge1,\qquad
\frac12<\alpha<\alpha_E(n),\qquad
\beta\ge0,
$$

with

$$
\alpha_E(n)=\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3},
$$

define

$$
B=n+\alpha+\beta+1,
\qquad
H(u)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right).
$$

Let $u_0$ be the first zero in the cap of

$$
K_B(u)=
-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_Bu-\Delta_Bu^2.
$$

Equivalently, if

$$
D_B=\Lambda_B^2-\Delta_B\alpha^2,
$$

then the first positive zero is

$$
u_0=\frac{\Lambda_B-\sqrt{D_B}}{2\Delta_B},
$$

provided this lies in $[0,u_\sigma]$. At this zero,

$$
K_B'(u_0)=\sqrt{D_B}>0.
$$

Let $u_1$ be the first critical point of $H$ in $(u_0,u_\sigma]$:

$$
H'(u_1)=0.
$$

Then prove

$$
|H(u_1)|
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)B}{(n+\alpha+1)(B-\alpha)}
\right)^{1/4}.
$$

This hypothesis is finite-$B$, cap-local, and first-lobe-only.

It is strictly weaker than proving the global Laguerre inequality on $0\le u<\infty$.

## 3. Conditional Airy/Langer theorem

The exact dynamic oscillator is

$$
H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0,
$$

where

$$
\tau=\log\frac{u}{B-u},
\qquad
u(\tau)=\frac{Be^\tau}{1+e^\tau},
$$

and

$$
K_B(u)=
-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_Bu-\Delta_Bu^2.
$$

Let $u_0$ be the first turning point in the cap and set

$$
\tau_0=\log\frac{u_0}{B-u_0}.
$$

Define the Airy scale

$$
\gamma
=
K_{B,\tau}(\tau_0)
=
p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)
=
u_0\left(1-\frac{u_0}{B}\right)\sqrt{D_B}.
$$

This is positive whenever the first turning point lies in the interior of the cap.

Define the Airy coordinate $\zeta$ by

$$
\frac23\zeta(u)^{3/2}
=
\int_{u_0}^{u}
\frac{\sqrt{K_B(t)}}{p_B(t)}\,dt,
\qquad u\ge u_0,
$$

and

$$
\frac23(-\zeta(u))^{3/2}
=
\int_{u}^{u_0}
\frac{\sqrt{-K_B(t)}}{p_B(t)}\,dt,
\qquad 0<u\le u_0.
$$

Then

$$
K_B(u(\tau))=\zeta(\tau)\zeta_\tau(\tau)^2,
$$

and near the turning point,

$$
\zeta(\tau)=\gamma^{1/3}(\tau-\tau_0)+O((\tau-\tau_0)^2).
$$

Set

$$
W(\zeta)=\zeta_\tau(\tau)^{1/2}H(\tau).
$$

Then $W$ satisfies the exact Airy-normal-form equation

$$
W_{\zeta\zeta}+\zeta W=\Psi_B(\zeta)W,
$$

where

$$
\Psi_B(\zeta)
=
-\frac12\{\tau,\zeta\}.
$$

Equivalently, away from $\zeta=0$,

$$
\Psi_B(\zeta)
=
\frac{\zeta\,\ddot K_B}{4K_B^2}
-
\frac{5\zeta\,\dot K_B^2}{16K_B^3}
+
\frac{5}{16\zeta^2},
$$

where dots denote $\tau$-derivatives:

$$
\dot K_B=p_B(u)K_B'(u),
$$

and

$$
\ddot K_B
=
p_B(u)
\left[
p_B'(u)K_B'(u)+p_B(u)K_B''(u)
\right].
$$

Since

$$
p_B'(u)=1-\frac{2u}{B},
\qquad
K_B'(u)=\Lambda_B-2\Delta_Bu,
\qquad
K_B''(u)=-2\Delta_B,
$$

this gives the completely explicit form

$$
\dot K_B
=
u\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)(\Lambda_B-2\Delta_Bu),
$$

and

$$
\ddot K_B
=
u\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)
\left[
\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right)(\Lambda_B-2\Delta_Bu)
-
2\Delta_Bu\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right)
\right].
$$

The apparent singularity of $\Psi_B$ at $\zeta=0$ is removable for a simple turning point. Proving a usable uniform bound for this removable value and for its variation is part of the remaining analytic work.

### Endpoint normalization for the Langer theorem

As $u\downarrow0$,

$$
H(u)\sim A_{n,\alpha,B}u^{\alpha/2},
$$

with

$$
A_{n,\alpha,B}
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}
\right)^{1/2}
B^{-\alpha/2}
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+1)}.
$$

Equivalently, since $u\sim Be^\tau$ as $\tau\to-\infty$,

$$
H(\tau)\sim \widetilde A_{n,\alpha,B}e^{\alpha\tau/2},
$$

where

$$
\widetilde A_{n,\alpha,B}
=
A_{n,\alpha,B}B^{\alpha/2}.
$$

The Bessel-normalization quantity inherited from earlier rounds is

$$
M_{n,\alpha,B}
=
A_{n,\alpha,B}\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Lambda_B^{-\alpha/2}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\log M_{n,\alpha,B}
=
\frac12
\left[
\log\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)+\log\Gamma(B)
-\log\Gamma(n+1)-\log\Gamma(B-\alpha)
\right]
-
\frac{\alpha}{2}\log(B\Lambda_B).
$$

This quantity may exceed $1$ in some low or moderate regimes, so it must be bounded rather than assumed harmless.

### Exact Langer matching constant

Define the forbidden-side regularized action

$$
I_B
=
\lim_{\tau\to-\infty}
\left[
\int_{\tau}^{\tau_0}
\sqrt{-K_B(u(s))}\,ds
-
\frac{\alpha}{2}(\tau_0-\tau)
\right].
$$

The Airy matching constant for the regular branch is then

$$
C_{\mathrm{Ai}}
=
2\sqrt{\pi}
\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right)^{1/2}
\widetilde A_{n,\alpha,B}
\exp\left(
\frac{\alpha\tau_0}{2}+I_B
\right).
$$

This formula follows by comparing

$$
W(\zeta)=\zeta_\tau^{1/2}H(\tau)
$$

with

$$
\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)
$$

as $\tau\to-\infty$, where $-\zeta\to+\infty$.

This is a useful new normalization because it separates the gamma/Frobenius normalization from the forbidden-zone tunneling action. It is still not a proved estimate; it is the correct quantity that the Airy theorem must bound.

### Sufficient Airy/Langer amplitude theorem

The following theorem would imply H16.1.

Assume that for every residual parameter triple one has an Airy approximation on $-\infty<\zeta\le\zeta_\sigma$ of the form

$$
\left|
W(\zeta)-C_{\mathrm{Ai}}\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)
\right|
\le
C_{\mathrm{Ai}}\,\mathcal R_B(\zeta),
$$

where $\mathcal R_B(\zeta)$ is controlled by an explicit error integral depending only on $\Psi_B$, for example by a computable bound of the form

$$
\mathcal R_B(\zeta)
\le
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{Olver}}(\zeta)
\left(
\exp\mathcal V_B(-\infty,\zeta)-1
\right),
$$

with

$$
\mathcal V_B(a,b)=\int_a^b |\Psi_B(s)|\,ds
$$

or the appropriate weighted Olver variation integral.

Then the first-lobe amplitude hypothesis follows if one proves

$$
C_{\mathrm{Ai}}
\sup_{0\le\zeta\le\zeta_\sigma}
\zeta_\tau(\zeta)^{-1/2}
\left(
|\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)|+\mathcal R_B(\zeta)
\right)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
$$

This is the clean Airy/Langer sufficient condition.

It is not yet proved. The three missing estimates are exactly:

1. a uniform bound for $\mathcal V_B$ or the correct weighted Olver variation;
2. a gamma/action envelope for $C_{\mathrm{Ai}}$ or an equivalent $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ envelope;
3. finite-$n$ interval verification after a large-$n$ analytic theorem exists.

## 4. Prufer version of the same remaining theorem

On the allowed side $K_B>0$, define the modified Prufer variables by

$$
H=R K_B^{-1/4}\sin\phi,
\qquad
H_\tau=R K_B^{1/4}\cos\phi.
$$

Then the exact equations are

$$
\frac{R_\tau}{R}
=
-\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos2\phi,
$$

and

$$
\phi_\tau
=
\sqrt{K_B}
+
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\sin2\phi.
$$

Let $u_h>u_0$ be an Airy handoff point and let $u_1$ be the first critical point. Since $H_\tau(u_1)=0$, one has $\cos\phi(u_1)=0$, and therefore

$$
|H(u_1)|=R(u_1)K_B(u_1)^{-1/4}.
$$

The exact drift identity is

$$
R(u_1)
=
R(u_h)
\exp\left(
-\frac14
\int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1}
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{K_B}\cos2\phi\,d\tau
\right).
$$

In $u$-coordinates,

$$
\int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1}
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{K_B}\cos2\phi\,d\tau
=
\int_{u_h}^{u_1}
\frac{K_B'(u)}{K_B(u)}\cos2\phi(u)\,du.
$$

Thus an alternative sufficient theorem is:

If for some handoff $u_h$ one proves

$$
R(u_h)K_B(u_1)^{-1/4}
\exp\left(
-\frac14
\int_{u_h}^{u_1}
\frac{K_B'(u)}{K_B(u)}\cos2\phi(u)\,du
\right)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta},
$$

then H16.1 follows.

This identity is exact. The open part is bounding the signed oscillatory drift. The absolute-value bound is too crude near $u_0$ because $K_B'/K_B$ is singular; integration by parts or an Airy handoff is mandatory.

## 5. Boundary cases

### Case $n=0$

The endpoint-cap first-lobe machinery is for $n\ge1$. The $n=0$ case has no oscillatory lobe and must be handled separately by a base-case lemma for the weighted beta-type function

$$
g_0^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)}
{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}.
$$

This case should not be hidden inside the endpoint proof.

### Case $\alpha=0$

The right endpoint is not residual. It lies in the imported small-exponent theorem $0\le\alpha\le1/2$.

### Case $\alpha=1/2$

This is the boundary of the small-exponent theorem. It should be included in the imported small-exponent module, not in the residual Airy theorem. The residual theorem should use $\alpha>1/2$.

### Case $\beta=0$

The formulas remain valid. In this case

$$
B-\alpha=n+1,
$$

so the right-endpoint target becomes

$$
T_{n,\alpha,0}=1.
$$

Also

$$
K_B'(u_\sigma)=\frac{\alpha}{2}.
$$

This is simpler numerically but does not by itself prove the first-lobe estimate.

### Case where $K_B$ has no zero in the cap

Since

$$
K_B(0)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}<0,
$$

if $K_B$ has no zero in the cap, then

$$
K_B(u)<0
$$

throughout the cap. The forbidden-zone ascent argument applies on the whole cap, so the maximum occurs at $u_\sigma$ and is controlled by the central boundary estimate.

### Case $u_0=u_\sigma$

The allowed region has zero length. The forbidden-zone ascent gives the maximum at the central boundary. Controlled.

### Case $u_1$ absent

If $u_1$ is absent after $u_0$, then $H_\tau$ does not vanish after the turning point. Since the forbidden-zone branch reaches $u_0$ with positive derivative, $H$ remains increasing to $u_\sigma$. The maximum is at $u_\sigma$, hence controlled by the central boundary estimate.


### Left endpoint

The left endpoint is not a separate analytic problem. The Jacobi symmetry

$$
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(-x)=(-1)^nP_n^{(\beta,\alpha)}(x)
$$

implies

$$
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(-x)=(-1)^n g_n^{(\beta,\alpha)}(x).
$$

Thus every right-endpoint statement transfers to the left endpoint after interchanging $\alpha$ and $\beta$. Any final proof must still check that the imported small-exponent and central modules use the same strong target normalization after this interchange.

## 6. Semi-discrete note

For the semi-discrete target

$$
\alpha\ge0,\qquad \beta\in\mathbb N_0,
$$

the proof skeleton applies without alteration. The case $\beta=0$ has the simplification $T=1$, and fixed small integer $\beta$ gives a lower-dimensional numerical verification problem.

However, there is currently no proved monotonicity in $\beta$ or in the compactified parameter

$$
\theta=\frac{n+\alpha+1}{B}
$$

that would reduce the continuous $\beta$ problem to integer $\beta$ values. Therefore one should not claim that the semi-discrete case is easier analytically unless one proves an additional monotonicity, convexity, or finite-verification theorem.

Dependencies:

1. KKT normalization and the Jacobi Sturm--Liouville equation.

2. Central-contour clearance. This remains an imported module and should be audited separately for exact constants.

3. Small-exponent endpoint theorem for $0\le\alpha\le1/2$ and its symmetric left endpoint version.

4. Weighted-energy clearance in the large-exponent region.

5. Endpoint-cap algebra:
   - exact $u$ variable;
   - cap length $u_\sigma\le n$;
   - endpoint ODE;
   - quadratic $K_B$;
   - derivative lower bound.

6. Forbidden-zone ascent and Sonin ordering.

7. Landau's monotonicity/bounds for Bessel functions may be useful only if the Airy/Langer theorem is compared with Bessel or Airy extrema, but it does not replace the first-lobe proof.

8. Olver/Dunster--Gil--Segura type Airy turning-point estimates are the relevant external theory for a computable residual bound.

9. Gamma-ratio control must use a real-argument tool such as Binet's formula, gamma-ratio inequalities, or a Robbins-type bound extended beyond integers. Robbins itself is factorial-only.

10. Later interval verification should use Arb or comparable ball arithmetic.

Potential gaps:

## What remains unproved

Only the following should remain in the active gap register.

1. **Airy/Langer amplitude constants.** One must prove a uniform error bound for

$$
W_{\zeta\zeta}+\zeta W=\Psi_B(\zeta)W
$$

with the exact KKT $\Psi_B$, through the first allowed lobe.

2. **Gamma/action envelope.** One must bound either $C_{\mathrm{Ai}}$ or the equivalent Bessel-normalization quantity $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ sharply enough to fit inside the KKT target.

3. **Finite-$n$ interval certificate.** After a large-$n$ analytic theorem gives an explicit $N_0$, the remaining finite set $1\le n<N_0$ must be certified by interval arithmetic, including boundary faces $\theta=0$, $\theta=1$, $\alpha=1/2$, and $\alpha=\alpha_E(n)$.

## Hidden assumptions to avoid

1. Do not assume $M_{n,\alpha,B}\le1$.

2. Do not replace the finite-$B$ first-lobe theorem by a global Laguerre theorem.

3. Do not use absolute-value Prufer drift near $u_0$ without Airy regularization.

4. Do not claim that $\beta\in\mathbb N_0$ discretization alone closes the semi-discrete case.

5. Do not claim that interval arithmetic proves anything before a finite $N_0$ is available.

Counterexample or obstruction search:

## What would falsify this route

The route would be falsified, or at least require a major redesign, if any of the following occurs.

1. A certified numerical or interval computation finds

$$
\frac{|H(u_1)|}{T_{n,\alpha,\beta}}>1
$$

for some residual parameter triple.

2. The imported central-contour or small-exponent theorem fails at the exact constants needed for the strong KKT target.

3. The Langer residual variation

$$
\mathcal V_B=\int |\Psi_B(\zeta)|\,d\zeta
$$

has unavoidable order-one or growing size in the hard scaling $\alpha=cn$, $\beta=0$, $0<c<1$, with no compensating oscillatory cancellation.

4. The Airy matching constant $C_{\mathrm{Ai}}$ or gamma-normalization $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ exceeds the KKT target by a margin larger than any possible Airy maximum slack.

5. The first-lobe location approaches the central interface in a way that destroys all uniform Airy-to-Sonin handoff constants.

Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration:

## Alternative 1: Product formula or hypergroup route

A positive product formula proving that $g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}$ is a normalized positive-definite spherical function would immediately give $|g|\le1$. This is elegant but currently blocked by normalization and parameter-range issues. Existing product formulas prove adjacent estimates but not the sharp KKT normalization for all real $\alpha,\beta\ge0$.

Precise needed lemma:

There exists a probability space and a character-like representation

$$
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)=\int e^{in\theta(\omega,x)}\,d\mu_{\alpha,\beta,x}(\omega)
$$

or a positive hypergroup character formula normalized by $g(1)=1$ in the KKT weighted normalization.

Falsification test:

Check whether the endpoint weight factors in $g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}$ can be absorbed into an existing Jacobi hypergroup character. If the product formula applies only to $P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)/P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(1)$, it does not prove KKT.

Status: exploratory.

## Alternative 2: Christoffel-function route

A sharp reproducing-kernel inequality might imply

$$
w_{\alpha,\beta}(x)^{1/2}|P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}/C_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
$$

Known Christoffel estimates are usually correct in order but not sharp enough for the KKT constant. The first-lobe reduction may make a localized Christoffel estimate more plausible.

Precise needed lemma:

A one-point Christoffel upper bound in the endpoint cap that matches the KKT normalization and target constant.

Falsification test:

Evaluate the Christoffel envelope at the first endpoint lobe in the Laguerre face $\beta=\infty$. If the envelope exceeds the KKT target by a fixed factor, this route is not sharp enough.

Status: exploratory and probably secondary.

## Alternative 3: Direct finite-degree interval proof for low $n$

For $n=1$ and $n=2$, compactified formulas may allow full interval certification without a large-$n$ theorem. This would not solve the conjecture but would test the route and remove small-degree distractions.

Precise needed lemma:

For fixed $n$, express all critical-point equations and $H^4-T^4$ in interval-stable compactified variables

$$
(\alpha,\theta,u),
\qquad
\theta=\frac{n+\alpha+1}{B},
$$

including the $\theta=0$ Laguerre face.

Status: useful for A4, but not a proof of the infinite problem.

## Alternative 4: Semi-discrete finite-$\beta$ induction

For $\beta\in\mathbb N_0$, contiguous relations in $\beta$ might permit induction from $\beta=0$ or from integer-parameter representation theory if one can preserve the KKT target.

Precise needed lemma:

A monotone or convex comparison of

$$
\frac{|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta+1)}(x)|}{T_{n,\alpha,\beta+1}}
$$

against the $\beta$ case, with $\alpha$ real.

Falsification test:

Numerically sample the normalized first-lobe ratio as a function of integer $\beta$. Nonmonotone behavior would not kill the route but would rule out simple induction.

Status: exploratory.

Useful lemmas:

## Lemma L16.1: Endpoint product identities

For

$$
K_B(u)=p_B(u)q_B(u),
$$

one has

$$
K_B(u)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_Bu-\Delta_Bu^2,
$$

with

$$
\Lambda_B=c_B+\frac{\alpha r_B}{2},
\qquad
\Delta_B=\frac{c_B}{B}+\frac{r_B^2}{4}.
$$

Moreover

$$
K_B'(u)=\Lambda_B-2\Delta_Bu,
$$

and

$$
K_B'(u_\sigma)
=
\frac{(\alpha+\beta)(n+\alpha+1)}{2B}.
$$

Status: certified from prior rounds; restated for lemma bank.

## Lemma L16.2: First turning point formula

If $K_B$ has a zero in the cap, its first positive zero is

$$
u_0=\frac{\Lambda_B-\sqrt{\Lambda_B^2-\Delta_B\alpha^2}}{2\Delta_B}.
$$

At this point,

$$
K_B'(u_0)=\sqrt{\Lambda_B^2-\Delta_B\alpha^2}.
$$

Status: proved algebraically.

## Lemma L16.3: Airy scale

At an interior turning point $u_0$,

$$
\gamma=p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)
=
u_0\left(1-\frac{u_0}{B}\right)
\sqrt{\Lambda_B^2-\Delta_B\alpha^2}.
$$

Status: proved algebraically.

## Lemma L16.4: Exact Airy residual

With the Airy coordinate $\zeta$ defined by

$$
K_B=\zeta\zeta_\tau^2,
$$

and

$$
W=\zeta_\tau^{1/2}H,
$$

the transformed equation is

$$
W_{\zeta\zeta}+\zeta W=\Psi_B(\zeta)W,
$$

where

$$
\Psi_B(\zeta)
=
-\frac12\{\tau,\zeta\}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\Psi_B(\zeta)
=
\frac{\zeta\,\ddot K_B}{4K_B^2}
-
\frac{5\zeta\,\dot K_B^2}{16K_B^3}
+
\frac{5}{16\zeta^2}.
$$

Status: proved as a formal Liouville transformation identity; uniform bounds remain open.

## Lemma L16.5: Endpoint Airy matching constant

For $\alpha>0$, the regular branch has Airy matching constant

$$
C_{\mathrm{Ai}}
=
2\sqrt{\pi}
\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right)^{1/2}
A_{n,\alpha,B}B^{\alpha/2}
\exp\left(
\frac{\alpha\tau_0}{2}+I_B
\right),
$$

where

$$
I_B
=
\lim_{\tau\to-\infty}
\left[
\int_{\tau}^{\tau_0}
\sqrt{-K_B(u(s))}\,ds
-
\frac{\alpha}{2}(\tau_0-\tau)
\right].
$$

Status: formal asymptotic matching derived; sharp bounds on this quantity remain open.

## Lemma L16.6: Conditional Airy amplitude criterion

If

$$
C_{\mathrm{Ai}}
\sup_{0\le\zeta\le\zeta_\sigma}
\zeta_\tau(\zeta)^{-1/2}
\left(
|\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)|+\mathcal R_B(\zeta)
\right)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta},
$$

with $\mathcal R_B$ a rigorous Airy residual bound for $\Psi_B$, then the finite-$B$ first-lobe amplitude hypothesis H16.1 holds.

Status: proved as a sufficient condition; estimates for the constants are open.


## Lemma L16.7: Strong target factor

For all $n\ge0$ and $\alpha,\beta\ge0$,

$$
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}^4
=
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\le1.
$$

Indeed,

$$
(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)
-
(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)
=
\alpha\beta\ge0.
$$

Status: elementary and useful for checking that the strong estimate implies the original $|g_n|\le1$ target.

## Lemma L16.8: Endpoint cap length

For $n\ge1$,

$$
u_\sigma=\frac{nB}{B+n-1}\le n.
$$

This follows from $B+n-1\ge B$. The inequality is important because it keeps the residual first-lobe task inside a finite cap rather than the whole Laguerre half-line.

## Lemma L16.9: Forbidden-zone ascent

Assume $\alpha>0$ and $K_B<0$ on $(0,a)$. Then the regular endpoint branch is positive and increasing on $(0,a)$.

Proof sketch: the regular Frobenius behavior gives $H(u)\sim A_{n,\alpha,B}u^{\alpha/2}$ with $A_{n,\alpha,B}>0$. With $W=p_BH'$, one has $W'=-q_BH>0$ as long as $H>0$. A first-loss argument rules out the first point where either $H$ or $W$ becomes nonpositive.

Status: local Sturm comparison lemma; it should be kept in the proof skeleton.

## Lemma L16.10: Sonin ordering

On intervals where $q_B>0$,

$$
S_B(u)=H(u)^2+\frac{p_B(u)H'(u)^2}{q_B(u)}
$$

satisfies

$$
S_B'(u)
=-\frac{K_B'(u)}{q_B(u)^2}H'(u)^2.
$$

In the residual cap, $K_B'\ge0$, hence $S_B$ is nonincreasing. At local extrema of $H$, $S_B=H^2$, so the local-extremum amplitudes are nonincreasing after the first turning point.

Status: certified modulo the imported endpoint ODE and the verified derivative lower bound.

## Lemma L16.11: Exact dynamic oscillator and Pruefer identity

With

$$
\tau=\log\frac{u}{B-u},
$$

one has the exact oscillator

$$
H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0.
$$

On $K_B>0$, define

$$
H=RK_B^{-1/4}\sin\phi,
\qquad
H_\tau=RK_B^{1/4}\cos\phi.
$$

Then

$$
\frac{R_\tau}{R}
=
-\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos 2\phi,
$$

and

$$
\phi_\tau
=
\sqrt{K_B}
+
\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\sin 2\phi.
$$

Status: exact identity. The open issue is not the identity itself but the signed drift estimate near the simple turning point, where $K_{B,\tau}/K_B$ is singular and an Airy handoff or global Langer bound is required.
