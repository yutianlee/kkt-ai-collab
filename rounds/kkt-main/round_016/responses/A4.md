# Exact derivations

### 1. The $n=1$ Critical Quadratic
For $n=1$, $B = \alpha+\beta+2$. The Jacobi polynomial is $P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}(1-2u/B) = \alpha+1-u$.
The function is $H_1(u) = C_{1,\alpha,\beta} (u/B)^{\alpha/2} (1-u/B)^{\beta/2} (\alpha+1-u)$.
Setting the logarithmic derivative to zero yields:
$$ \frac{\alpha}{2u} - \frac{\beta}{2(B-u)} - \frac{1}{\alpha+1-u} = 0 $$
Clearing denominators by multiplying by $2u(B-u)(\alpha+1-u)$ gives:
$$ \alpha(B-u)(\alpha+1-u) - \beta u(\alpha+1-u) - 2u(B-u) = 0 $$
Expanding the terms systematically:
$$ \alpha [ B(\alpha+1) - (B+\alpha+1)u + u^2 ] - \beta [ (\alpha+1)u - u^2 ] - 2Bu + 2u^2 = 0 $$
Grouping by powers of $u$:
$$ u^2 (\alpha + \beta + 2) - u [ \alpha(B+\alpha+1) + \beta(\alpha+1) + 2B ] + \alpha B(\alpha+1) = 0 $$
Since $B = \alpha+\beta+2$, the leading coefficient is exactly $B$. The exact quadratic is:
$$ B u^2 - [\alpha(B+\alpha+1) + \beta(\alpha+1) + 2B] u + \alpha B(\alpha+1) = 0 $$
The roots are $u = \frac{C_1 \pm \sqrt{C_1^2 - 4BD_1}}{2B}$, where $C_1 = \alpha(B+\alpha+1) + \beta(\alpha+1) + 2B$ and $D_1 = \alpha B(\alpha+1)$. The first critical point $u_1$ is the smaller root, provided it lies in $(0, u_\sigma)$.

### 2. The $n=2$ Critical Cubic
For $n=2$, we use the stable hypergeometric form. Let $\theta = \frac{\alpha+3}{B}$.
$$ P_2^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right) = \frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}{2} \left[ 1 - \frac{2u}{\alpha+1} + \frac{1 + \frac{\theta}{\alpha+3}}{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)} u^2 \right] $$
Let $A = \frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}{2}$, $b_1 = \alpha+2$, and $c_1 = \frac{1}{2} \left( 1 + \frac{\theta}{\alpha+3} \right)$.
Then $P_2(u) = A - b_1 u + c_1 u^2$, and $P_2'(u) = -b_1 + 2c_1 u$.
The logarithmic derivative condition $\frac{H_2'}{H_2} = 0$ is:
$$ \frac{\alpha}{2u} - \frac{\beta}{2(B-u)} + \frac{-b_1 + 2c_1 u}{A - b_1 u + c_1 u^2} = 0 $$
Multiplying by $2u(B-u)(A - b_1 u + c_1 u^2)$ yields a cubic equation $E_3 u^3 + E_2 u^2 + E_1 u + E_0 = 0$.
Expanding the terms systematically:
Term 1: $\alpha(B-u)(A - b_1 u + c_1 u^2) = \alpha A B - \alpha(A+B b_1)u + \alpha(B c_1 + b_1)u^2 - \alpha c_1 u^3$.
Term 2: $-\beta u(A - b_1 u + c_1 u^2) = -\beta A u + \beta b_1 u^2 - \beta c_1 u^3$.
Term 3: $2u(B-u)(-b_1 + 2c_1 u) = -2B b_1 u + 2(2B c_1 + b_1)u^2 - 4c_1 u^3$.
Summing the coefficients:
$E_3 = -c_1(\alpha+\beta+4)$.
$E_2 = B c_1(\alpha+4) + b_1 B$.
$E_1 = -A(\alpha+\beta) - B b_1^2$.
$E_0 = \alpha A B$.
This cubic is exact and its coefficients are polynomials in $\alpha, \beta, B$ and rational functions of $\theta$. This provides a stable basis for interval root isolation for $n=2$.

### 3. Compactified Hypergeometric Stability
The general polynomial is:
$$ P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right) = \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!} \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{(-n)_k}{(\alpha+1)_k k!} \left[ \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \left(1+\frac{j\theta}{n+\alpha+1}\right) \right] u^k $$
At the Laguerre face $\theta=0$, the product $\prod_{j=0}^{k-1} (1+0) = 1$. The sum reduces exactly to the Laguerre polynomial $L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)$ up to normalization. There is no $1^\infty$ indeterminacy because the product is finite and explicitly evaluated for each $k \le n$. This representation is [PROVED] to be numerically stable for interval arithmetic.

# Explicit error-bound audit

The Prufer drift integral is given by:
$$ I_{drift} = \int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1} \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B} \cos 2\phi \, d\tau $$
where $\tau_h$ is the Airy handoff point and $\tau_1$ is the first critical point.
A naive absolute-value bound $\int |\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}| d\tau$ diverges logarithmically at the turning point $\tau_0$ where $K_B(\tau_0)=0$.
To regularize, we apply integration by parts. Let $W(\tau) = \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{8 K_B^{3/2}}$.
Using the phase equation $\phi_\tau = \sqrt{K_B} + \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B} \sin 2\phi$, we compute the exact derivative:
$$ \frac{d}{d\tau} \left( W(\tau) \sin 2\phi \right) = W'(\tau) \sin 2\phi + 2 W(\tau) \phi_\tau \cos 2\phi $$
Substituting $\phi_\tau$:
$$ 2 W \phi_\tau \cos 2\phi = 2 W \sqrt{K_B} \cos 2\phi + 2 W \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B} \sin 2\phi \cos 2\phi $$
Since $2 W \sqrt{K_B} = \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}$ and $2 W \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B} \sin 2\phi \cos 2\phi = \frac{K_{B,\tau}^2}{32 K_B^{5/2}} \sin 4\phi$, we obtain the exact identity:
$$ \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B} \cos 2\phi = \frac{d}{d\tau} \left( W(\tau) \sin 2\phi \right) - W'(\tau) \sin 2\phi - \frac{K_{B,\tau}^2}{32 K_B^{5/2}} \sin 4\phi $$
Integrating from $\tau_h$ to $\tau_1$:
$$ I_{drift} = \left[ W(\tau) \sin 2\phi \right]_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1} - \int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1} W'(\tau) \sin 2\phi \, d\tau - \int_{\tau_h}^{\tau_1} \frac{K_{B,\tau}^2}{32 K_B^{5/2}} \sin 4\phi \, d\tau $$
At $\tau_1$, $H_\tau = 0$, which implies $\cos \phi(\tau_1) = 0$, so $\sin 2\phi(\tau_1) = 0$. The upper boundary term vanishes.
The lower boundary term at $\tau_h$ is $-W(\tau_h) \sin 2\phi(\tau_h)$.
If $\tau_h$ is chosen as $\tau_0 + a \gamma^{-1/3}$ where $\gamma = p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)$, then $K_B(\tau_h) \approx a \gamma^{2/3}$ and $K_{B,\tau}(\tau_h) \approx \gamma$.
Thus $W(\tau_h) \approx \frac{\gamma}{8 (a \gamma^{2/3})^{3/2}} = \frac{1}{8 a^{3/2}}$.
This boundary term is $O(a^{-3/2})$. It does not vanish as $n \to \infty$ unless $a \to \infty$, but if $a \to \infty$, the Airy approximation error grows.
Therefore, any claim that the drift is uniformly $O(n^{-4/3})$ without explicitly bounding the Airy Cauchy data at $\tau_h$ and the residual integral $\int |W'(\tau)| d\tau$ is [LIKELY-FALSE]. The boundary term $O(a^{-3/2})$ represents a hard structural obstruction to naive Prufer bounds.

# Worst-case vulnerability check

The hardest regime is the transition strip $\alpha \approx \alpha_E(n)$ with $\beta=0$ ($\theta=1$).
We audit the gamma normalization $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ in the $\alpha = cn$ regime.
Using Robbins' bounds, $\log M_{n,\alpha,B} = \frac{1}{2} [ \log \Gamma(n+\alpha+1) - \log \Gamma(n+1) + \log \Gamma(B) - \log \Gamma(B-\alpha) ] - \frac{\alpha}{2} \log(B\Lambda_B)$.
For $\beta=0$, $B = n+\alpha+1$. The terms $\log \Gamma(B) - \log \Gamma(n+\alpha+1)$ cancel exactly.
We are left with $\frac{1}{2} [ \log \Gamma(n+\alpha+1) - \log \Gamma(n+1) - \log \Gamma(B-\alpha) ] - \frac{\alpha}{2} \log(B\Lambda_B)$.
Since $B-\alpha = n+1$, $\log \Gamma(B-\alpha) = \log \Gamma(n+1)$.
Thus $\log M = \frac{1}{2} [ \log \Gamma(n+\alpha+1) - 2\log \Gamma(n+1) ] - \frac{\alpha}{2} \log(B\Lambda_B)$.
Applying Stirling's approximation, the leading order is:
$\frac{1}{2} [ (n+\alpha+1/2)\log(n+\alpha) - (n+\alpha) - 2(n+1/2)\log n + 2n ] - \frac{\alpha}{2} \log(n^2)$.
This simplifies to $\frac{1}{2} [ (n+\alpha)\log(1+\alpha/n) + \alpha \log n - \alpha ] - \alpha \log n$.
The leading term is actually negative for large $c = \alpha/n$.
Specifically, $f(c) = (1+c)\log(1+c) - c - \frac{c}{2}\log(1+c+c^2/2)$.
For $c \in (0, 1]$, $f(c) < 0$. This implies $M_{n,\alpha,B}$ decays exponentially in the deep transition strip.
However, the Robbins remainder terms $r(x)$ must be tracked. The sum of remainders is bounded by $\frac{1}{2} ( \frac{1}{12(n+\alpha)} + \frac{2}{12n} ) = O(1/n)$.
Thus $M_{n,\alpha,B} \le 1 + C/(n+1)$ is [DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS] pending the exact evaluation of the $O(1)$ constant in the intermediate regime $\alpha = O(\sqrt{n})$.

# Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

**Parameter Derivative Route**: Consider $\partial_\beta H_n(u)$. If one could prove that the first-lobe maximum is monotone in $\beta$ for fixed $n, \alpha$, the problem would reduce entirely to the Laguerre face $\beta \to \infty$ and the $\beta=0$ face. However, the $\beta$-derivative of the Jacobi polynomial involves a sum over lower degrees with alternating signs:
$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} c_k P_k^{(\alpha,\beta+1)}(x) $$
The coefficients $c_k$ do not have a uniform sign, making a direct positivity proof for the envelope [LIKELY-FALSE].

**Christoffel-Darboux Kernel Route**: An alternative is to bound $H_n(u_1)^2$ using the reproducing kernel $K_n(x,x)$. However, the KKT target requires a sharp single-polynomial bound, not an integrated kernel bound. The Christoffel function $\lambda_n(x)$ gives an average, which is too lossy for the pointwise first-lobe maximum. This route is abandoned.

**Rational Coordinate Langer Transform**: Instead of the affine $\tau$ coordinate, use the rational coordinate $v = \frac{Bu}{B-u}$. The invariant product is $v \widehat{q}_B(v) = K_B(u(v))$. A Langer transform mapping $v$ to an Airy variable might absorb the geometric amplitude factor natively. However, the Schwarzian derivative of the Mobius transform is zero, meaning it does not introduce a new curvature correction. It is algebraically equivalent to the $\tau$ coordinate and does not bypass the $O(a^{-3/2})$ boundary obstruction.
