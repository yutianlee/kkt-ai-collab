## Claims that look correct

The following statements are **certified algebraically** or rest on well‑audited formulas from previous rounds; they can be moved toward the lemma bank.

1. **Endpoint ODE and quadratic product**  
   The affine endpoint equation $(p_BH')'+q_BH=0$ with $p_B(u)=u(1-u/B)$, $q_B(u)$ as given, and $K_B(u)=p_Bq_B=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_B u-\Delta_B u^2$ is correct. The coefficients $\Lambda_B$, $\Delta_B$, $r_B$, $c_B$ are consistent across all three outputs.

2. **Cap length and monotonicity**  
   $u_\sigma = \frac{nB}{B+n-1}\le n$ (for $n\ge1$) and $K_B'(u_\sigma)=\frac{(\alpha+\beta)(n+\alpha+1)}{2B}\ge\frac\alpha2$. Consequently $K_B'(u)\ge\frac\alpha2>\frac14$ in the residual strip $\alpha>1/2$.

3. **Dynamic oscillator and Prüfer identities**  
   With $\tau=\log\frac{u}{B-u}$, one obtains $H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0$. On $K_B>0$, the modified Prüfer variables $H=R K_B^{-1/4}\sin\phi$, $H_\tau=R K_B^{1/4}\cos\phi$ satisfy  
   $$
   \frac{R_\tau}{R}= -\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos2\phi,\qquad
   \phi_\tau = \sqrt{K_B}+\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\sin2\phi .
   $$  
   These are exact and are independent of any coordinate‑artefact amplitude factor.

4. **Turning point and Airy scale**  
   If $u_0$ is a simple zero of $K_B$, then $K_{B,\tau}(\tau_0)=p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)$ defines the correct local Airy scaling, and the scaled coordinate $\zeta$ introduced by A1 and A2 is consistent with standard Langer theory.

5. **Upper boundary term vanishing**  
   At the first critical point $u_1$, $H_\tau(u_1)=0$ forces $\cos\phi(u_1)=0$, hence $\sin2\phi(u_1)=0$. Therefore any boundary term proportional to $\sin2\phi$ evaluated at the upper limit of a Prüfer drift integral vanishes. A2’s proof of this is clean and correct.

6. **$n=1$ and $n=2$ critical‑point equations**  
   A4’s quadratic for $n=1$ and cubic for $n=2$, including the stable coefficients in terms of $\theta$, are algebraically verified. The compactified hypergeometric polynomial  
   $$
   P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\!\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right)=
   \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}\sum_{k=0}^n \frac{(-n)_k}{(\alpha+1)_k k!}
   \Bigl[\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\Bigl(1+\frac{j\theta}{n+\alpha+1}\Bigr)\Bigr] u^k
   $$  
   is correct and avoids $1^\infty$ instability at $\theta=0$.

7. **Landau’s Bessel maximum**

## Claims that need proof

The following central assertions are plausible but remain **open analytic gaps**; they should not be treated as established.

1. **First‑lobe amplitude inequality (H16.1)**  
   $$
   |H(u_1)|\le\Bigl(\frac{(n+1)B}{(n+\alpha+1)(B-\alpha)}\Bigr)^{1/4}
   $$  
   for all $n\ge1$, $\frac12<\alpha<\alpha_E(n)$, $\beta\ge0$. No agent proves this. A1’s conditional theorem simply restates it as a hypothesis; A2 and A4 explore mechanisms but do not close the gap.

2. **Global Langer residual boundedness**  
   A2 and A4 give exact algebraic expressions for $\psi(\zeta)$; what is missing is a uniform bound for $\int|\psi(\zeta)|\zeta^{-1/2}\,d\zeta$ (or the appropriate Olver variation integral) that is smaller than the KKT slack. The scaling heuristics $\mathcal O(n^{-4/3})$ are not yet rigorous theorems.

3. **Airy handoff boundary constant**  
   A2’s analysis suggests that the lower boundary term in the piecewise Airy‑to‑Prüfer scheme is $\mathcal O(a^{-3/2})$ and cannot be suppressed without violating the linear Airy approximation. This is a strong warning, but it is not a proof that every dynamic approach fails – only that the naive piecewise handoff is obstructed. A rigorous theorem would require explicit bounds on both the boundary term and the Airy error, which are not supplied.

4. **Gamma‑ratio uniform upper bound**  
   $$
   M_{n,\alpha,B}=
   \sqrt{\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B)}{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}}(B\Lambda_B)^{-\alpha/2}

   needs a one‑sided inequality strong enough to leave room for later error terms. A1’s suggested $1+C/(n+1)$ and A2’s Binet‑Robbins expansion are promising starts, but neither agent produces a finished estimate with explicit constants and a regime split. A2’s claim that the remainder sum $E_\mu$ is strictly negative rests on a hand‑wavy sign argument; Robbins’ formulas give **positive** remainders, and the sum could be positive depending on the arguments.

5. **Interval certificate for finite degrees**  
   A4 describes a plausible interval‑arithmetic plan for $n=1$ and $n=2$, but no actual computation is executed. Until the prototype yields rigorous boxes, outward‑rounded evaluations, and verified negative margins, this remains a plan.

## Possible errors or hidden assumptions

1. **A1’s Airy matching constant $C_{\mathrm{Ai}}$**  
   The formula  
   $$
   C_{\mathrm{Ai}}=
   2\sqrt{\pi}\Bigl(\frac{\alpha}{2}\Bigr)^{1/2}
   \widetilde A_{n,\alpha,B}
   \exp\Bigl(\frac{\alpha\tau_0}{2}+I_B\Bigr)
   $$  
   is stated without a detailed derivation. The factor $2\sqrt{\pi}$ and the exponent may be off by a constant if the standard mapping of the Frobenius branch to $\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)$ is not correctly normalized. A rigorous check of the Liouville–Green amplitude normalisation is needed before this quantity can be used in any bound.  

2. **A2’s sign of the gamma‑ratio remainder**  
   A2 claims “the sum of the remainders $E_\mu$ is strictly negative for all valid parameters”. With Robbins’ bounds $1/(12x+1)<\mu(x)<1/(12x)$, each $\mu$ is positive. The combination $\mu(n+\alpha+1)+\mu(B)-\mu(n+1)-\mu(B-\alpha)$ can be positive or negative; a simple “denominator dominance” argument is not rigorous. Any proof of the gamma‑ratio envelope must track these signs explicitly or use a different tool (e.g. Gautschi’s inequalities).  

3. **A4’s Prüfer drift identity – extra $\sin4\phi$ term**  
   A4 gives  
   $$
   \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos2\phi
   =\frac{d}{d\tau}\bigl(W\sin2\phi\bigr)
   -W'\sin2\phi
   -\frac{K_{B,\tau}^2}{32 K_B^{5/2}}\sin4\phi .
   $$  
   This identity is algebraically correct and refines earlier IBP expressions that omitted the $\sin4\phi$ term. Any subsequent bound on the drift **must** account for this extra oscillatory term. A1’s and A2’s sketches of the IBP do not discuss it, which could lead to an underestimate of the drift if absolute‑value bounds are used.  

4. **Turning‑point layer and the linear approximation**  
   A2’s obstruction analysis assumes that the handoff point $\tau_h$ can be placed at a distance $a\gamma^{-1/3}$ where the Airy approximation error is small. Yet the required suppression of the boundary term forces $a$ to be large, pushing the handoff away from the turning point, where the Taylor expansion $K_B(\tau)\approx\gamma(\tau-\tau_0)$ loses accuracy. The precise error incurred by the quadratic term $\frac12 K_{B,\tau\tau}(\tau_h-\tau_0)^2$ has not been bounded. Any proof relying on an Airy handoff must explicitly control this error, not just argue heuristically.  

5. **Over‑reliance on asymptotic scaling**  
   A2 and A4 frequently use $O$, $\mathcal O$, and $\approx$ without explicit error constants. While these heuristics are valuable for selecting the route, they do not constitute a proof of the sharp KKT inequality, which requires numerical constants and absolute error bounds.
