## 1. Most valuable input from others

**A1** contributes the most important formal object of the round: a conditional finite-cutoff Langer theorem that replaces the earlier vague amplitude problem with a precisely defined inequality chain.  
The theorem’s value lies not in proving the inequality but in making every constant \((\mathfrak C_A,\varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}},\mathcal V_A,d_1,D_1)\) explicit and checkable. The scalar identity  

\[
|W(\zeta_1)|
=
\frac{\|Z(\zeta_1)\|_\infty}{\pi D_1}\,,
\qquad
D_1=\max\bigl(|a'(\zeta_1)-d_1a(\zeta_1)|,\,
|b'(\zeta_1)-d_1b(\zeta_1)|\bigr),
\]

derived from the first-critical-point condition \(H_\tau(\tau_1)=0\) and the Airy Wronskian, is algebraically exact and shows that the crude \(|\operatorname{Ai}|+|\operatorname{Bi}|\) factor can be replaced by a scalar denominator. While the practical slack gained may be small, the theorem sets clear measurement targets and keeps the proof skeleton consistent.

A1 also correctly distinguishes the crude matrix-norm version from a weighted Olver/Dunster–Gil–Segura (DGS) version and provides boundary-case clauses (no turning point, absent first critical point, finite cutoff definition). The Frobenius‑to‑Airy normalization candidate \(\mathfrak C_A^0 = \sqrt{2\pi\alpha}\,A_{n,\alpha,B}\,e^{\mathcal C_B}\) is properly flagged as “not yet certified”.

**A2** provides the strongest obstruction analysis. The derivation of the limiting Laguerre‑face residual

\[
\Psi_B(0)\;\sim\;\frac{4^{2/3}}{140}\,\alpha^{-4/3}
\qquad(\theta=0,\;B\to\infty)
\]

is a genuine advance: it shows that the Langer residual at the turning point does **not** vanish as \(n\) grows for fixed \(\alpha\). The computed slack‑margin at \(\alpha=1/2\) (multiplicative room \(\approx 1.47\)) is a useful diagnostic, and the Prüfer‑phase buffer condition

\[
u-u_0 \gtrsim \frac{\alpha^{4/3}}{4^{4/3}n}
\qquad
\longrightarrow\; \mathcal O(n^{1/3})\;\text{when }\alpha=cn
\]

quantifies the failure of a naive Airy‑to‑Prüfer handoff. A2’s recommendation to use DGS weight functions and to explore a static Bessel–Volterra fallback for small \(\alpha\) is well founded.

**A4** delivers the first concrete low‑degree analytic closures. The exact degree‑one computations for the Laguerre face \((\theta=0)\) and the finite face \((\beta=0,\theta=1)\) are correct and exhibit massive margins (\(0.1715\ll 0.8\) and \(0.2488\ll 1\), respectively). The Riccati Taylor coefficients up to third order are derived exactly and will be essential for validated interval‑ODE certificates. A4’s correction of earlier target‑formula and normalisation errors is valuable; without it, the numerical diagnostics of Rounds 16–17 were unreliable.

The Arb C‑API blueprint, though not executed, gives a concrete protocol for the still‑missing interval certificate. A4’s Langer variation diagnostic table, though correctly labelled “[HEURISTIC]”, illustrates the dramatic difference between crude and weighted error norms and supports the urgent need to instantiate DGS‑type error theorems.

## 2. Claims that look correct

I list here the claims that are either already certified in the state, derived correctly in this round, or consistent with the cumulative lemma bank.  

1. **Endpoint‑cap geometry and first‑lobe reduction.**  
   All agents accept \(0\le u\le u_\sigma\le n\), the exact ODE \((p_BH')'+q_BH=0\), the concave quadratic \(K_B=p_Bq_B\), the cap derivative bound \(K_B'(u)>\frac14\) for \(\alpha>\frac12\), forbidden‑zone ascent, and Sonin ordering that reduces the cap to the first critical point \(u_1\). This architecture is now stable enough to be treated as certified conditional on the imported central‑contour, energy, small‑exponent, and symmetry modules.

2. **Exact dynamic oscillator and Langer transformation.**  
   The identities  

   \[
   H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0,\qquad \tau=\log\frac{u}{B-u},
   \]

   and the Langer coordinate defined by \(K(\tau)=\zeta(\tau)\zeta_\tau(\tau)^2\) together with \(H=\zeta_\tau^{-1/2}W\) giving  

   \[
   W_{\zeta\zeta}+\zeta W=\Psi_B(\zeta)W,
   \qquad
   \Psi_B(\zeta)=\frac{\zeta K_{\tau\tau}}{4K^2}
   -\frac{5\zeta K_\tau^2}{16K^3}+\frac{5}{16\zeta^2}
   \]

   are verified in previous rounds and accepted by all three agents. The removable‑value formula  

   \[
   \Psi_B(0)=\frac{10\gamma K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)-9K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0)^2}
                 {140\gamma^{8/3}},\qquad
   \gamma=K_\tau(\tau_0)=p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)
   \]

   is likewise confirmed.

3. **Liouville normal‑form sign \(K_B+1/4\).**  
   With \(Y_u=p_B^{1/2}H\), one has  

   \[
   Y_u''+\frac{K_B(u)+1/4}{p_B(u)^2}Y_u=0.
   \]

   All agents uphold the plus sign; any appearance of \(K_B-1/4\) is rejected. This distinction is important because the Liouville‑normal turning point \(K_B=-\frac14\) differs from the Sturm/Sonin turning point \(K_B=0\).

4. **Riccati equation and Taylor coefficients.**  
   The equation  

   \[
   p_B v_u+v^2+K_B=0,\qquad v=p_B\frac{H'}{H},
   \]

   and the first four coefficients  

   \[
   v_0=\frac{\alpha}{2},\quad
   v_1=-\frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1},
   \]
   \[
   v_2=\frac{1}{\alpha+2}
        \Bigl(-\frac{\Lambda_B}{B(\alpha+1)}-
               \frac{\Lambda_B^2}{(\alpha+1)^2}+
               \Delta_B\Bigr),
   \]
   \[
   v_3=\frac{2v_2}{\alpha+3}
        \Bigl(\frac1B+\frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1}\Bigr)
   \]

   are derived by power‑matching in the Riccati equation and are ready for a validated Taylor‑model initialisation. A2 and A4 give essentially identical expressions; minor discrepancies in the coefficient of \(v_{uu}(0)\) (A2’s form for \(v_2\) is the same) are not conflicts but different normalisation conventions.

5. **A2’s Laguerre‑face \(\Psi_B(0)\) leading‑order scaling.**  
   In the limit \(\beta\to\infty\) (\(B\to\infty,\theta=0\)), the potential simplifies and the turning‑point residual tends to  

   \[
   \Psi_\infty(0)=\frac{4^{2/3}}{140}\alpha^{-4/3}.
   \]

   The algebraic simplification from \(K_\infty(u)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_\infty u-\frac{u^2}{4}\), obtaining \(\gamma\approx\alpha^2/4\) and the derivatives, is sound. This proves that the residual is an \(\mathcal O(1)\) quantity independent of \(n\) at leading order, a crucial diagnostic.

6. **Prüfer phase‑derivative buffer.**  
   From \(\phi_\tau=\sqrt{K_B}+(K_{B,\tau}/(4K_B))\sin 2\phi\) one obtains the condition \(\sqrt{K_B}>|K_{B,\tau}|/(4K_B)\) for a guaranteed positive lower bound. Linearising near the turning point yields a minimum physical distance \(u-u_0 \approx \frac{\alpha^{4/3}}{4^{4/3}n}\). This is a necessary condition for a stable Prüfer integration and highlights why a handoff near the turning point is invalid.

7. **A4’s degree‑one boundary‑face closures.**  
   For \(n=1\), the Laguerre face and finite face are certified with exact formulas and large margins. The corrected Laguerre critical quadratic  

   \[
   u^2-(2\alpha+3)u+\alpha(\alpha+1)=0,
   \]

   the physical root \(u_1=\frac{2\alpha+3-\sqrt{8\alpha+9}}{2}\), and the evaluation  

   \[
   \ell_1^{(0.5)}(u_1)^4\approx0.1715<0.8
   \]

   are algebraically correct. The finite‑face formula  

   \[
   H_1(u_1)^4=\Bigl(\frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{(\alpha+2)^2}\Bigr)^{2\alpha}
               \frac{16(\alpha+1)^4}{(\alpha+2)^4}
   \]

   and the maximum \(0.2488<1\) are likewise verified. These closures supersede earlier erroneous numerical rows.

## 3. Claims that need proof

1. **Instantiation of a weighted Airy error theorem (Dunster–Gil–Segura 2020).**  
   Every agent recognises that the crude \(\infty\)-norm \(\Omega_A\) is too large, especially on the forbidden side. The alternative is the DGS framework with explicit modulus/weight functions that respect the recessive Frobenius branch. However, no agent has yet extracted the precise weight functions, mapped them to the KKT residual \(\Psi_B\), and verified the required hypotheses (bounded variation of the error‑control function, continuity after removing the turning‑point singularity, etc.). Until this is done, the claim that a weighted variation \(\mathcal V_*\) “restores decay” or “fits inside the slack” is a heuristic, not a theorem.

2. **Gamma‑ratio (and Langer‑normalisation) upper envelope.**  
   The matching constants  

   \[
   M_{n,\alpha,B}=
   \sqrt{\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(B)}
              {\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(B-\alpha)}}
   (B\Lambda_B)^{-\alpha/2}
   \]

   and the Langer normalisation \(\mathfrak C_A\) require rigorous finite‑\(n\) upper bounds. The leading entropy negativity \(f(c)<0\) for \(\alpha=cn\) is useful asymptotic evidence, but a uniform theorem must employ Binet’s formula or real‑argument Robbins/Kershaw inequalities, split into regimes (\(\alpha=O(1)\), \(\alpha=O(\sqrt n)\), \(\alpha=cn\)), and carry explicit remainders. No such theorem is provided in Round 19.

3. **Finite‑cutoff mismatch \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}}\).**  
   A1 defines \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}}\) but supplies no bound. The cutoff location \(u_{\mathrm{cut}}=\rho u_0\) must be chosen to balance Frobenius accuracy (small \(\rho\)) against Airy‑matrix conditioning (large \(\rho\)). An explicit estimate for the deviation between the exact Frobenius solution and the Airy Cauchy data at \(\zeta_{\mathrm{cut}}\) is missing. This is not a fatal gap but a necessary measurement for any finite‑cutoff certificate.

4. **Complete low‑degree interval certificates.**  
   A4 has proved only the two boundary faces \((\theta=0\) and \(\theta=1)\) for \(n=1\). The whole compactified domain \(\theta\in[0,1]\) and the full \(n=2\) case require outward‑rounded interval verification. The Arb blueprint is a plan, not a certificate. Failure‑box logs are mandatory.

5. **Static Bessel‑Volterra fallback with explicit constants.**  
   A2 sketches a Frobenius‑to‑Bessel comparison, suggesting that the Volterra error integral behaves like \(\mathcal O(\alpha^3/n^2)\). For this to become a theorem, the Liouville normal form must be expanded, the error kernel bounded, and the resulting integral evaluated with explicit constants valid on the residual strip. None of this is done.

6. **Frobenius‑to‑Airy connection theorem.**  
   A1’s candidate \(\mathfrak C_A^0 = \sqrt{2\pi\alpha}\,A_{n,\alpha,B}\,e^{\mathcal C_B}\) involves the unevaluated limit \(\mathcal C_B\) and unverified branch conventions. Connecting the recessive Airy branch at \(\zeta_{\mathrm{cut}}\) to the true endpoint solution requires either Olver’s matching theorem with a stated error or a direct interval evaluation of \(Z_{\mathrm{cut}}\). The latter is feasible for low degrees; for large \(n\) a rigorous analytic bound is needed and is still absent.

7. **Regime‑split threshold.**  
   A2’s heuristic \(\alpha_{\mathrm{thresh}}\sim n^{3/5}\) is derived from a demand for fractional‑power error decay. Whether the actual weighted variation crosses a practical threshold depends on numerical constants that have not been enclosed. The threshold remains conjectural.

## 4. Possible errors or hidden assumptions

1. **A1’s scalar envelope is not a guaranteed improvement.**  
   The identity \(|W|=\|Z\|_\infty/(\pi D_1)\) is exact, but the overall bound  

   \[
   |H(u_1)|\le
   \zeta_\tau(\tau_1)^{-1/2}
   \frac{\mathfrak C_A(1+\varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}})e^{\mathcal V_A}}
        {\pi D_1T_{n,\alpha,\beta}}
   \]

   still depends crucially on the variation \(\mathcal V_A\) and the coefficient norm at the cutoff. In the oscillatory region (\(\zeta_1>0\)) the denominator \(D_1\) is typically comparable to \(|\operatorname{Ai}|+|\operatorname{Bi}|\); the scalar factor may therefore give negligible practical advantage over the crude envelope. A1’s theorem is algebraically correct but should not be presented as a decisive sharpening. The real battle is won or lost in the variation integral and the gamma normalisation.

2. **A2’s Laguerre‑face limit uses formal asymptotics without finite‑\(B\) error control.**  
   The derivation of \(\Psi_B(0)\sim\frac{4^{2/3}}{140}\alpha^{-4/3}\) assumes the turning point scales as \(u_0\approx\alpha^2/(4\Lambda_\infty)\) and that higher‑order terms are negligible. For a rigorous obstruction one would need to bound the error in this local model over the full \(B\) range. The conclusion that the residual “is \(\mathcal O(1)\) and does not vanish” is directionally correct but should be labelled a **derived‑under‑assumptions** warning, not a proved theorem.

3. **A2’s slack‑margin calculation is not a proof.**  
   The estimate \(e^{\mathcal V_A}\approx1.046\) uses a crude model for \(\Psi_B(0)\) and an assumed integration width \(\Delta\zeta\approx1\). The actual weighted variation could be larger (or smaller after DGS weighting). The “room” argument is only a diagnostic; it must not be mistaken for a closure.

4. **A4’s refutation of the critical‑point sharpening overstates the case.**  
   A4 claims that the sharpening is “conceptually flawed” because “\(\zeta_1>0\) is in the oscillatory regime” and the Bi component is not eliminated. In fact, A1’s derivation does **not** claim to eliminate the Bi component; it uses the critical condition to relate the coefficients and then applies the Airy Wronskian, obtaining an exact bound. Whether this bound is practically sharp is a separate question. A4’s statement that “the true resolution is the recessive initial condition combined with Olver weights” is correct, but that does not make A1’s algebra wrong. The criticism should be downgraded to a **heuristic warning**: the scalar sharpening does not circumvent the need to control the variation integral.

5. **A4’s variation table is labelled “HEURISTIC” but the text sometimes speaks of “proving” that Olver weights restore decay.**  
   The table shows that the crude \(\mathcal V_A\) diverges or stagnates while the “Olver‑weighted” version becomes small. These numbers are derived from an unspecified quadrature implementation and are not certified interval enclosures. The claim that “Olver weights restore the necessary decay” is a diagnostic hypothesis, not a proved statement. The careful separation between experimental evidence and certified bounds, demanded by the state, is not fully maintained.

6. **A4’s semi‑discrete contiguous‑relation dismissal is plausible but not fully justified.**  
   The conclusion that the relation is “[LIKELY‑FALSE]” because “sign irregularities prevent induction” is a qualitative observation. A rigorous obstruction would require stating exactly where the sign changes and why a bound cannot be transferred. Until then, this is an exploratory observation, not a proved impossibility.

7. **Hidden assumption in the finite‑cutoff theorem: integrability of the forbidden tail.**  
   A1’s theorem uses a finite \(\zeta_{\mathrm{cut}}\), but the underlying Volterra argument implicitly assumes bounded variation and a well‑behaved coefficient matrix. On the forbidden side, \(\operatorname{Bi}(-\zeta)\) grows exponentially, and the DGS framework assigns weights that keep the variation finite. Any instantiation must verify that \(\Psi_B\) satisfies the required smoothness and integrability conditions; otherwise the variation integral may diverge. This is not yet checked.

8. **Terminology around the Laguerre face.**  
   A4 correctly states that \(\theta=0\) is the Laguerre face and \(\theta=1\) is the finite face (\(\beta=0\)). However, A4’s “finite‑face” computation for \(n=1\) actually uses \(\beta=0\) (i.e. \(\theta=1\)), which is correctly labelled. The confusion from earlier rounds is now resolved. No agent repeats the mistake of calling \(\beta=0\) the Laguerre face.

## 5. Suggested synthesis

The Round 19 outputs converge on a **split certification programme** whose backbone is the finite‑cutoff Langer/Airy theorem, but with the crude matrix norm replaced by a properly instantiated DGS weighted error theorem. The critical‑point scalar envelope can be retained as a minor refinement, but the crucial task is to measure the weighted variation \(\mathcal V_*\) and the gamma‑ratio envelope. The programme should consist of four tracks.

**Track A: Weighted Langer/Airy analytic theorem (A1, A3).**  
Adopt A1’s finite‑cutoff theorem, but explicitly invoke DGS 2020 (or Olver 1974 with modern weights) to define \(\Omega_*(\zeta)\) and the corresponding coefficient bound. Verify that \(\Psi_B\) meets the hypotheses (removable singularity, bounded variation on \([\zeta_{\mathrm{cut}},\zeta_1]\)). State the resulting sufficient inequality in terms of the measurable constants \(\mathfrak C_A,\varepsilon_{\mathrm{tail}},\mathcal V_*\). **Do not** claim closure until \(\mathcal V_*\) is enclosed for the residual strip.

**Track B: Static Bessel‑Volterra fallback for small‑\(\alpha\)/Laguerre‑face regime (A2, A3).**  
For parameter regions where the Langer residual is numerically too large or the forbidden tail is problematic, provide a separate certificate. Expand the Liouville normal form \(Y_u''+Q_uY_u=0\) around the Bessel core, bound the remainder, and estimate the Volterra kernel. The goal is a theorem of the form “for \(\alpha\le C\sqrt n\) (or \(\alpha\le n^{3/5}\) after measurement), the first‑lobe bound follows from a Bessel comparison with certified error \(\le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{Bessel}}\).” The constants must be explicit.

**Track C: Gamma‑ratio and Langer‑normalisation envelope (A3).**  
Apply Binet’s formula or real‑argument Robbins/Kershaw inequalities to \(\log M_{n,\alpha,B}\) (and similar integrals for \(\mathfrak C_A\)). Split regimes: \(\alpha=O(1)\), \(\alpha=O(\sqrt n)\), \(\alpha=cn\); \(\theta=0\), \(\theta=1\), interior \(\theta\). Deliver at least one rigorous finite‑\(n\) inequality in the \(\alpha=cn\) regime (where leading entropy negativity is strongest) and map the error to the other regimes.

**Track D: Low‑degree interval and Riccati certificates (A4).**  
Execute full outward‑rounded interval certificates for \(n=1\) and \(n=2\) using the corrected critical equations and, for validation, the Riccati IVP. The certificates must cover the whole compactified domain \(\theta\in[0,1]\), not just boundary faces. Report unresolved boxes, reproduce precision and subdivision policies. The Riccati IVP should be initialised with the exact Taylor coefficients (up to \(v_3\)) at \(u=\epsilon\) and integrated with an interval ODE solver until the first zero of \(v\); the result must be compared with the polynomial critical root.

**Common requirement:** All constants must be stated with their exact algebraic definitions, and every theorem must list the imported modules and parameter ranges on which it relies. The semi‑discrete \(\beta\in\mathbb N_0\) case should be kept only as a long‑term exploratory note; it has not yielded a sign‑regular proof.

## 6. Research strategy adjustments

- **Stop adding architecture.** Round 19 has collected enough conditional theorems and diagnostics. Round 20 must be an execution round: the panel must produce measured constants and executed certificates, not new proof skeletons.
- **Force computational execution.** A4’s next output must include actual Arb (or equivalent) interval logs. Plans and pseudo‑code are no longer sufficient. Failure boxes are legitimate outputs and must be reported.
- **Audit the weighted error theorem before use.** A2 (or A1/A3) must extract the exact weight functions from Dunster–Gil–Segura (2020) and verify that the hypotheses are satisfied by the KKT residual \(\Psi_B\). This is the single most important missing analytic piece.
- **Adopt regime splitting as a working hypothesis.** A2’s Laguerre‑face analysis raises a genuine warning that a monolithic Langer theorem may not close the full strip. The panel should proceed with a **bulk Langer track** and a **small‑\(\alpha\) or boundary‑face fallback track**. The exact split can be refined after measurements.
- **Downgrade the critical‑point sharpening.** The scalar denominator is algebraically valid, but it does not reduce the main difficulty (the variation integral). The panel should not expend further effort refining it; the scarce analytic resources should go to instantiated DGS weights and gamma‑ratio remainders.
- **Treat A2’s asymptotic obstruction as a strong warning, not a no‑go theorem.** The limit \(\Psi_B(0)\sim\frac{4^{2/3}}{140}\alpha^{-4/3}\) demonstrates a non‑vanishing residual, but the actual constant is small. The final verdict depends on the weighted integral, which may still be acceptably bounded.
