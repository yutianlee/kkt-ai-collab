### 1. Analytic Closure of $n=1$ Boundary Faces
For $n=1$, the critical quadratic is:
$$
(\alpha+\beta+2)u^2 - [\alpha(B+\alpha+1)+\beta(\alpha+1)+2B]u + \alpha B(\alpha+1) = 0.
$$

**Laguerre Face ($\theta=0$, $\beta \to \infty$):**
The normalized function is $\ell_1^{(\alpha)}(u) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha+1}} u^{\alpha/2} e^{-u/2} (\alpha+1-u)$.
Setting the derivative to zero yields the quadratic equation for the critical point:
$$
u^2 - (2\alpha+3)u + \alpha(\alpha+1) = 0.
$$
*(Note: The previous round incorrectly reported $u_1 = \alpha+1 - \sqrt{\alpha+1}$. The correct discriminant is $D = (2\alpha+3)^2 - 4\alpha(\alpha+1) = 8\alpha+9$.)*
The physical root is $u_1 = \frac{2\alpha+3 - \sqrt{8\alpha+9}}{2}$.
For $\alpha \in [0.5, 1.2]$, this function is strictly maximized at $\alpha=0.5$:
$$
u_1 = \frac{4 - \sqrt{13}}{2} \approx 0.19722.
$$
Evaluating the fourth power at $u_1$:
$$
\ell_1^{(0.5)}(u_1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1.5}} (0.19722)^{0.25} e^{-0.09861} (1.5 - 0.19722) \approx 0.8165 \times 0.6673 \times 0.9061 \times 1.3028 \approx 0.6435.
$$
$$
\ell_1^{(0.5)}(u_1)^4 \approx 0.1715.
$$
The target is $T^4 = \frac{2}{\alpha+2}$. At $\alpha=0.5$, $T^4 = \frac{2}{2.5} = 0.8$.
Since $0.1715 \le 0.8$, the Laguerre face is **[PROVED]** closed with a margin of $>0.62$.

**Finite Face ($\theta=1$, $\beta=0$, $B=\alpha+2$):**
The normalization constant is $C_{1,\alpha,0}^2 = \frac{\Gamma(2)\Gamma(\alpha+2)}{\Gamma(\alpha+2)\Gamma(2)} = 1$.
The polynomial is $P_1^{(\alpha,0)}(1-2u/B) = \alpha+1-u$.
The normalized function is $H_1(u) = (u/B)^{\alpha/2} (\alpha+1-u)$.
*(Note: The previous round incorrectly included a factor of $\sqrt{\alpha+1}$ and missed a factor of 16 in the fourth power. This derivation is exact.)*
Setting $H_1'(u) = 0$ yields $u_1 = \frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{\alpha+2}$.
Substituting $u_1$ and $B=\alpha+2$:
$$
H_1(u_1) = \left( \frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{(\alpha+2)^2} \right)^{\alpha/2} \left( \alpha+1 - \frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{\alpha+2} \right) = \left( \frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{(\alpha+2)^2} \right)^{\alpha/2} \frac{2(\alpha+1)}{\alpha+2}.
$$
The fourth power is:
$$
H_1(u_1)^4 = \left( \frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{(\alpha+2)^2} \right)^{2\alpha} \frac{16(\alpha+1)^4}{(\alpha+2)^4}.

$$
H_1^{(0.5)}(u_1)^4 = \left( \frac{0.75}{6.25} \right)^1 \frac{16 \times 1.5^4}{2.5^4} = 0.12 \times \frac{81}{39.0625} = 0.12 \times 2.0736 = 0.248832.
$$
The target is $T^4 = \frac{2B}{(\alpha+2)(B-\alpha)} = \frac{2(\alpha+2)}{(\alpha+2)(2)} = 1$.
Since $0.248832 \le 1$, the finite face is **[PROVED]** closed with a massive margin of $>0.75$.

### 2. Exact Riccati Taylor Initialization
Let $v(u) = p_B(u) \frac{H'(u)}{H(u)}$. The Riccati equation is:
$$
p_B(u) v_u + v^2 + K_B(u) = 0.
$$
We expand $v(u) = v_0 + v_1 u + v_2 u^2 + v_3 u^3 + O(u^4)$.
Using $p_B(u) = u - u^2/B$ and $K_B(u) = -\alpha^2/4 + \Lambda_B u - \Delta_B u^2$, we match powers of $u$:
- $u^0$: $v_0^2 - \alpha^2/4 = 0 \implies v_0 = \alpha/2$.
- $u^1$: $v_1 + 2v_0 v_1 + \Lambda_B = 0 \implies v_1(1+\alpha) = -\Lambda_B \implies v_1 = -\frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1}$.
- $u^2$: $2v_2 - v_1/B + v_1^2 + 2v_0 v_2 - \Delta_B = 0 \implies v_2(2+\alpha) = v_1/B - v_1^2 + \Delta_B$.
$$
v_2 = \frac{1}{\alpha+2} \left( -\frac{\Lambda_B}{B(\alpha+1)} - \frac{\Lambda_B^2}{(\alpha+1)^2} + \Delta_B \right).
$$
- $u^3$: $3v_3 - 2v_2/B + 2v_0 v_3 + 2v_1 v_2 = 0 \implies v_3(3+\alpha) = 2v_2(1/B - v_1)$.
$$
v_3 = \frac{2v_2}{\alpha+3} \left( \frac{1}{B} + \frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1} \right).
$$
This exact third-order coefficient allows us to initialize the interval IVP at $u = \epsilon$ with a rigorous Taylor model bound.

### 3. Refutation of Critical-Point Airy Sharpening
The previous round claimed that the condition $H_\tau(\tau_1)=0$ eliminates the $\operatorname{Bi}$ component at the peak $\zeta_1$. This is **[LIKELY-FALSE]**.
At the first critical point $u_1$, we are in the allowed zone, so $\zeta_1 > 0$. The Airy functions $\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta_1)$ and $\operatorname{Bi}(-\zeta_1)$ are both oscillatory and of comparable magnitude $O(\zeta_1^{-1/4})$.
The ratio $c_2/c_1$ is fixed by the Cauchy data at $\zeta_{\text{cut}} < 0$ (the forbidden zone). In the forbidden zone, $\operatorname{Bi}(-\zeta)$ grows exponentially and $\operatorname{Ai}(-\zeta)$ decays. Because the physical Frobenius solution is recessive as $u \to 0$ ($\zeta \to -\infty$), $c_2$ must be exponentially small relative to $c_1$ to prevent exponential blowup.
Thus, $c_2/c_1 \sim e^{-\frac{4}{3}|\zeta_{\text{cut}}|^{3/2}}$. This exponential smallness persists into the allowed zone $\zeta_1 > 0$.
The critical point condition $W_\zeta(\zeta_1) = \kappa W(\zeta_1)$ is merely a linear relation between $c_1$ and $c_2$ at $\zeta_1$; it does NOT force $c_2$ to be small. The failure of the crude $\infty$-norm $\Omega_A$ is that it bounds the absolute variation of the coefficient vector without respecting the exponentially small initial value of $c_2$. The correct resolution is to use Olver's modulus and weight functions, which assign a weight of $e^{-\frac{4}{3}|\zeta|^{3/2}}$ to the $\operatorname{Bi}$ component in the forbidden zone.
