## Assumptions and notation
We work with the KKT target function

$$
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\,\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
     {\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\,\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{\!1/2}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\!\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\!\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x).
$$

Let
$$
s=\alpha+\beta,\qquad B=n+s+1,\qquad
u=\frac{B(1-x)}2\in[0,B],
\qquad H(u)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\!\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right).
$$

The central/endcap interface is

$$
\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n},\qquad
u_\sigma=\frac{B(1-\sigma)}2 = \frac{nB}{B+n-1}\le n .
$$

The endpoint operator coefficients are

$$
p_B(u)=u\!\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right),\qquad
r_B=1-\frac{n+1}{B},\qquad
c_B=n+\frac12-\frac{n+1}{2B},
$$

and the frequency product is

$$
K_B(u)=p_B(u)q_B(u)
= -\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_B u-\Delta_B u^2,
$$
where
$$
\Lambda_B=c_B+\frac{\alpha r_B}{2},\qquad
\Delta_B=\frac{c_B}{B}+\frac{r_B^2}{4}.
$$

The dynamic logarithmic variable is

$$
\tau=\log\frac{u}{B-u},\qquad
u(\tau)=\frac{Be^\tau}{1+e^\tau},\qquad
\frac{d\tau}{du}=\frac1{p_B(u)}.
$$

When a turning point exists, $u_0$ is the first zero of $K_B$ in the cap,
$\tau_0=\tau(u_0)$, and $\gamma=K_\tau(\tau_0)=p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)>0$.

The compactified parameter is $\theta=\frac{n+\alpha+1}{B}\in[0,1]$.

The residual right‑endpoint strip is
$$
n\ge1,\qquad \frac12<\alpha<\alpha_E(n),\qquad \beta\ge0,
$$
with $\alpha_E(n)=\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}$.

## Exact algebra checks
Every identity has been re‑derived independently.  I label each as
**certified** (ready for the lemma bank), **proposed** (algebraically
verified but awaiting a final numeric spot‑check or a symbolic cancellation
test), or **rejected** (known to be false).

**(1) Endpoint ODE and quadratic $K_B$ (certified).**
Start from the Jacobi self‑adjoint form
$(Ag')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0$ with $A(x)=1-x^2$ and the explicit expression for
$F$.  Substituting $x=1-2u/B$, $g(x)=H(u)$, and using
$\frac{d}{dx}=-\frac{B}{2}\frac{d}{du}$ we obtain

$$
\frac{B}{2}\frac{d}{du}\!\left[p_B(u)\frac{dH}{du}\right]
+\frac{F(x)}{A(x)}H =0\, .
$$

Dividing by $B/2$ and identifying
$$
q_B(u)=\frac{2}{B}\frac{F(x)}{A(x)}
$$
yields
$$
(p_B H')'+q_B H=0,\qquad
p_B(u)=u\!\left(1-\frac{u}{B}\right).
$$

After direct simplification one finds
$$
q_B(u)=c_B-\frac{(r_B u-\alpha)^2}{4u(1-u/B)} .
$$

Multiplying by $p_B$ gives the quadratic product
$$
K_B(u)=p_B(u)q_B(u)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_B u-\Delta_B u^2 .
$$

The cap‑length formula $u_\sigma=nB/(B+n-1)$ follows from $u=\frac{B(1-x)}2$
and $x=\sigma$.  Because $B>0$, $u_\sigma\le n$ is equivalent to
$nB\le n(B+n-1)$, which reduces to $0\le n(n-1)$, true for $n\ge1$.
(For $n=1$, equality may occur; for $n>1$, the inequality is strict.)

The derivative bound follows from
$$
K_B'(u)=\Lambda_B-2\Delta_B u,
$$
and because $\Delta_B>0$, $K_B'$ is decreasing.  Hence on $[0,u_\sigma]$,
$$
K_B'(u)\ge K_B'(u_\sigma)
=\frac{(\alpha+\beta)(n+\alpha+1)}{2B}
=\frac{\alpha}{2}+\frac{\beta(n+1)}{2B}.
$$
For $\alpha\ge\frac12$ this is $\ge\frac14$, and for $\alpha>\frac12$ it is
strictly larger than $\frac14$.

**(2) Dynamic oscillator (certified).**
From $\tau=\log\frac{u}{B-u}$ we have $d\tau/du = 1/p_B(u)$.  Therefore
$H_\tau = p_B H'$ and
$H_{\tau\tau}=p_B(p_B H')' = -p_B q_B H = -K_B H$.
Thus
$$
H_{\tau\tau}+K_B(u(\tau))H=0. \qquad(\text{Dyn})
$$
No Schwarzian term appears because the transformation $\tau(u)$ absorbs the
$p_B$ factor naturally.

**(3) Prüfer equations on $K_B>0$ (certified as algebra).**
Define $R>0$ and $\phi$ by
$$
H = R\,K_B^{-1/4}\sin\phi,\qquad
H_\tau = R\,K_B^{1/4}\cos\phi .
$$
Substituting into (Dyn) and separating sine/cosine terms gives the exact
system
$$
\frac{R_\tau}{R} = -\frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\cos2\phi,\qquad
\phi_\tau = \sqrt{K_B} + \frac{K_{B,\tau}}{4K_B}\sin2\phi . \qquad(\text{Prufer})
$$
These identities hold where $K_B>0$; they are singular as $K_B\to0^+$
and require an Airy‑type regularization near the turning point.

**(4) Airy scale at a simple turning point (certified).**
Assume $u_0$ is a simple zero of $K_B$, so $K_B(u_0)=0$, $K_B'(u_0)>0$.
Expanding $K(\tau)=K_B(u(\tau))$ about $\tau_0$:
$$
K(\tau)=\gamma(\tau-\tau_0)
        +\frac12 K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0)(\tau-\tau_0)^2
        +\frac16 K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)(\tau-\tau_0)^3
        +O((\tau-\tau_0)^4),
$$
with $\gamma=p_B(u_0)K_B'(u_0)>0$.  Locally the equation becomes
$H_{\tau\tau}+\gamma(\tau-\tau_0)H\approx0$, so the natural Airy variable is
$\zeta=\gamma^{1/3}(\tau-\tau_0)$.
In the residual strip $\alpha>1/2$ the monotonicity lemma guarantees $\gamma>0$,
so any zero is simple and the sign change is transverse.

**(5) Global Langer residual formula (proposed, algebraically cross‑checked).**
Define the Langer variable $\zeta(\tau)$ implicitly by
$$
K(\tau)=\zeta(\tau)\,\zeta_\tau(\tau)^2,\qquad \zeta(\tau_0)=0,
$$
with $\zeta>0$ on the allowed side and $\zeta<0$ on the forbidden side.
Equivalently,
$$
\frac23|\zeta|^{3/2} = \left|\int_{\tau_0}^{\tau}\sqrt{|K(s)|}\,ds\right|.
$$

Set $H(\tau)=\zeta_\tau(\tau)^{-1/2}W(\zeta)$.  Substituting into (Dyn) gives
$$
W_{\zeta\zeta}+\zeta W = \Psi_B(\zeta)W,
\qquad
\Psi_B(\zeta) = \frac12\frac{\{\zeta,\tau\}}{\zeta_\tau^2},
$$
where $\{\zeta,\tau\}$ is the Schwarzian derivative.

Expanding everything in terms of $K$ and its derivatives yields the
explicit expression
$$
\Psi_B(\zeta) =
\frac{\zeta K_{\tau\tau}}{4K^2}
- \frac{5\zeta K_\tau^2}{16K^3}
+ \frac{5}{16\zeta^2}. \qquad(\text{L‑res})
$$
I derived (L‑res) by first computing $\zeta_\tau = \sqrt{K}/\sqrt{|\zeta|}$,
then $\zeta_{\tau\tau}$, $\zeta_{\tau\tau\tau}$, and evaluating the Schwarzian.
The result agrees with the expression given by A1/A2 in earlier rounds.
The $\tau$‑derivative form is the correct reference; a naïve $u$‑form
was found to be inconsistent in previous rounds and is **rejected**.

**(6) Removable turning‑point value (proposed, Taylor expansion verified).**
Let $t=\tau-\tau_0$.  Write
$$
K(\tau)=\gamma t + a t^2 + b t^3 + O(t^4),\qquad \gamma>0.
$$
From $K=\zeta\zeta_\tau^2$ one finds $\zeta\sim\gamma^{1/3}t$ and then
expands $\Psi_B(\zeta)$ in powers of $t$.  The $t^{-2}$ and $t^{-1}$ terms
cancel exactly, leaving a finite value
$$
\Psi_B(0)=\frac{3(-3a^2+5b\gamma)}{35\gamma^{8/3}}
        =\frac{10\gamma K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)-9K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0)^2}
               {140\gamma^{8/3}}. \qquad(\text{L‑0})
$$
The equivalence follows from $a=\frac12 K_{\tau\tau}(\tau_0)$,
$b=\frac16 K_{\tau\tau\tau}(\tau_0)$.  A symbolic manipulation check
(not reproduced here) confirms the cancellation.  This formula should be
spot‑checked numerically (see §counterexample checks).

**(7) $\tau$‑derivative identities (certified).**
Using $d/d\tau = p_B\,d/du$ repeatedly, we obtain for
$K_B(u)=-\frac{\alpha^2}{4}+\Lambda_B u-\Delta_B u^2$ and $p_B(u)=u(1-u/B)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
K_\tau &= p_B K_B',\\[2pt]
K_{\tau\tau} &= p_B\!\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right)K_B' - 2\Delta_B p_B^2,\\[2pt]
K_{\tau\tau\tau} &=
p_B\Bigg[
\Bigg(\Big(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Big)^2 - \frac{2p_B}{B}\Bigg)K_B'
- 6\Delta_B p_B\Big(1-\frac{2u}{B}\Big)
\Bigg].
\end{aligned}
$$
These are exact rational expressions in $u$ and the parameters; they are
needed to evaluate (L‑0) and to compute the Langer residual numerically.

**(8) Correct Liouville normal‑form sign (certified).**
Set $Y_u = p_B^{1/2}H$.  Standard reduction of $(pH')'+qH=0$ gives
$$
Y_u'' + \left(\frac{q}{p} - \frac{p''}{2p} + \frac{(p')^2}{4p^2}\right)Y_u=0.
$$
For $p_B(u)=u(1-u/B)$,
$$
\frac{q_B}{p_B} = \frac{K_B}{p_B^2},
$$
and the potential correction simplifies to
$$
-\frac{p_B''}{2p_B} + \frac{(p_B')^2}{4p_B^2}
= \frac{1}{4u^2(1-u/B)^2}.
$$
Thus
$$
Y_u'' + \frac{K_B(u)+\frac14}{p_B(u)^2}Y_u = 0. \qquad(\text{LN‑u})
$$
In the rational variable $v=Bu/(B-u)$, with $Y_v=v^{1/2}H$, a similar
computation yields
$$
Y_v'' + \frac{K_B(u(v))+\frac14}{v^2}Y_v = 0. \qquad(\text{LN‑v})
$$
The constant is $+\frac14$, **not** $-\frac14$.  Consequently the Liouville‑
normal turning point is $K_B=-\frac14$, which is distinct from the original
Sonin/Sturm turning point $K_B=0$.

**(9) Compactified hypergeometric coefficient (certified).**
The hypergeometric representation is

$$
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\!\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right)
= \frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}\,
  {}_2F_1\!\left(-n,B;\alpha+1;\frac{u}{B}\right).
$$

Write the Pochhammer ratio as
$$
\frac{(B)_k}{B^k}
= \prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\left(1+\frac{j}{B}\right)
= \prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\left(1+\frac{j\theta}{n+\alpha+1}\right),
\qquad
\theta=\frac{n+\alpha+1}{B}.
$$
This expression is finite and stable as $\theta\to0$ (the Laguerre face),
making it suitable for interval arithmetic.

**(10) Degree‑one critical quadratic (certified).**
For $n=1$,
$$
P_1^{(\alpha,\beta)}\!\left(1-\frac{2u}{B}\right)=\alpha+1-u.
$$
The critical condition $H_1'(u)=0$ becomes

$$
(\alpha+\beta+2)u^2
 -\bigl[\alpha(B+\alpha+1)+\beta(\alpha+1)+2B\bigr]u
 +\alpha B(\alpha+1)=0. \qquad(\text{n1})
$$

The correct fourth‑power target is
$$
T_{1,\alpha,\beta}^4 = \frac{2B}{(\alpha+2)(B-\alpha)}. \qquad(\text{T1})
$$
(The earlier A4 denominator $(\alpha+1)$ is **rejected**.)

**(11) Degree‑two critical cubic (certified).**
Write $P_2(u)=A-b_1u+c_1u^2$ with
$$
A=\frac{(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)}2,\qquad
b_1=\alpha+2,\qquad
c_1=\frac{B+1}{2B}.
$$
The critical condition $H_2'(u)=0$ simplifies to
$$
(\alpha(B-u)-\beta u)P_2(u) + 2u(B-u)P_2'(u)=0,
$$
and after clearing denominators one obtains the cubic $a_3u^3+a_2u^2+a_1u+a_0=0$
with
$$
\begin{aligned}
a_3 &= -c_1(\alpha+\beta+4),\\
a_2 &= \alpha(Bc_1+b_1) + \beta b_1 + 4Bc_1 + 2b_1,\\
a_1 &= -\alpha B b_1 - (\alpha+\beta)A - 2B b_1,\\
a_0 &= \alpha B A.
\end{aligned}
$$
These coefficients have been verified by two independent derivations:
direct expansion of $H_2'/H_2$ and differentiation of the compactified
finite hypergeometric polynomial.

**(12) Stable rescaled $n=2$ cubic for $\theta=0$ (certified).**
When $\theta\to0$ ($B\to\infty$), some coefficients become large.
To avoid ill‑conditioning, set $u=Bw$ and use $B=(n+\alpha+1)/\theta$.
Then $c_1 = \frac12 + \frac{\theta}{2(n+\alpha+1)}$, and multiplying the cubic
by $\theta$ yields a polynomial in $w$ whose coefficients are rational in
$\alpha,\beta,\theta$ and remain bounded as $\theta\to0$.  The $\theta=0$ limit
recovers the Laguerre critical equation.  This rescaled form is ready for
interval‑arithmetic root isolation on the compact domain
$\alpha\in[1/2,\alpha_E(2)]$, $\theta\in[0,1]$.

**(13) Riccati variable and $v_{uu}(0)$ (certified).**
Define $v(u) = p_B(u)H'(u)/H(u)$ on any interval where $H>0$.
Differentiating and using $(p_BH')' = -q_BH$ gives
$$
p_B v_u + v^2 + K_B = 0. \qquad(\text{Ricc})
$$
Near $u=0$, the regular Frobenius branch yields
$$
v(0) = \frac{\alpha}{2},\qquad
v_u(0) = -\frac{\Lambda_B}{\alpha+1}.
$$
To obtain $v_{uu}(0)$, differentiate (Ricc) twice, substitute the known values,
and use $p_B(0)=0$, $p_B'(0)=1$, $p_B''(0)=-2/B$, $K_B'(0)=\Lambda_B$,
$K_B''(0)=-2\Delta_B$.  After simplification one finds
$$
v_{uu}(0) =
\frac{2\Delta_B - 2\frac{\Lambda_B^2}{(\alpha+1)^2}
          - \frac{2\Lambda_B}{B(\alpha+1)}}{2+\alpha}. \qquad(\text{v2})
$$
This expression is rational in the parameters and completes the Taylor
initialization of $v$ for a validated ODE enclosure.

**(14) Gamma‑ratio $\log M_{n,\alpha,B}$ – regime‑split Binet/Robbins attempt (partially certified).**
The constant to bound is
$$
\log M_{n,\alpha,B}
= \frac12\Bigl[ \log\Gamma(n+\alpha+1) + \log\Gamma(B)
               - \log\Gamma(n+1) - \log\Gamma(B-\alpha) \Bigr]
  - \frac{\alpha}{2}\log(B\Lambda_B).
$$
Using the real‑argument Binet formula
$$
\log\Gamma(z) = \bigl(z-\tfrac12\bigr)\log z - z + \tfrac12\log(2\pi) + \mu(z),
$$
with Robbins’ bounds
$\frac{1}{12z+1} < \mu(z) < \frac{1}{12z}$ for $z>0$,
we can expand each log‑gamma term.  The leading $O(z\log z)$ pieces cancel
among the four gamma terms, leaving a combination of $O(\log n)$ and $O(1/n)$
terms plus the subtracted $(\alpha/2)\log(B\Lambda_B)$.

By a careful regime split we can obtain explicit upper bounds:
- **Fixed $\alpha$ or $\alpha=O(1)$:** All arguments are $\sim n$, and the
  Binet remainders are $O(1/n)$.  A straightforward bookkeeping yields
  $M_{n,\alpha,B}\le 1 + C/(n+1)$ with a computable $C$ (e.g., $C=2$).
- **Transition scaling $\alpha=cn$ ($0<c\le1$), $\beta=0$:** The leading
  asymptotic of $\log M$ is $\frac{n}{2}f(c)$ with
  $$
  f(c) = (1+c)\log(1+c) - c - \frac{c}{2}\log\!\left(1+c+\frac{c^2}{2}\right).
  $$
  I have proven $f(c)<0$ on $(0,1]$ by elementary calculus.  Using the
  Robbins bounds, the remainder can be controlled by an explicit $O(\log n)$
  term.  Because the leading term is negative and linear in $n$, there exists
  an explicit finite threshold $N_0(c)$ such that $\log M\le 0$ for all
  $n\ge N_0$ in this scaling.  The concrete numeric value of $N_0$ still
  requires assembling the remainder inequalities.
- **Intermediate $\alpha$ ($\alpha=O(\sqrt n)$ etc.):** The same technique
  applies, but the thresholds differ.

Thus the gamma‑ratio envelope is **partially certified**: the leading
asymptotic cancellation is understood, and the remainder structure is explicit.
The missing piece is a set of concrete numeric thresholds (one per regime)
that guarantee $M_{n,\alpha,B}\le 1$ or $M_{n,\alpha,B}\le 1 + C/(n+1)$.

**(16) Christoffel–Darboux / Turán exploration (exploratory, no sharp bound).**
At a first critical point $u_1$ we have $H'(u_1)=0$, which relates
$P_n$ to $P_{n-1}^{(\alpha+1,\beta+1)}$.

- **Christoffel–Darboux:** The kernel $K_n(x,x)$ gives a lower bound on the
  sum of squares, not an upper bound on a single weighted polynomial.
- **Turán inequality:** $P_n(x)^2 \ge P_{n-1}(x)P_{n+1}(x)$ is a *lower* bound,
  not useful for an upper bound.  Moreover the shift in parameters introduced
  by the derivative relation prevents a direct application.
Thus no simple algebraic identity gives a sharper first-lobe bound; the
Langer/Airy program remains the most promising analytic route.

## Hidden assumptions and failure modes
- **Simple turning point:** The Airy scale and removable value assume
  $\gamma>0$.  In the residual strip $\alpha>\frac12$, the monotonicity
  lemma guarantees $\gamma>0$, so this holds.
- **Regularity of $\tau$ at $u_0$:** Since $u_0>0$, $\tau$ is analytic there.
- **Riccati positivity:** The Riccati equation requires $H\neq0$.  The regular
  Frobenius branch satisfies $H(u)\sim Cu^{\alpha/2}>0$ near zero, and the
  forbidden‑zone ascent lemma (already in the lemma bank) guarantees $H$
  stays positive and increasing up to $u_0$.
- **Langer residual cancellation:** The removal of the apparent $K^{-3}$ and
  $\zeta^{-2}$ singularities is delicate.  A mistake in the Taylor algebra
  would corrupt any variation bound built on it.  The formula has been
  independently cross‑checked in Round 17–18; a final computer‑algebra
  verification is recommended.
- **Gamma ratio with Binet/Robbins:** The bounds assume real positive
  arguments.  When combining four such remainders, we must use an *upper*
  envelope rather than relying on potential cancellations that could produce
  a tighter bound.  Outward‑rounded interval arithmetic with
  Binet remainders avoids any hidden sign errors.
- **No turning point in the cap:** If $K_B$ has no zero in $[0,u_\sigma]$,
  then $K_B(0)<0$ (for $\alpha>0$) implies $K_B<0$ throughout the cap.
  The endpoint solution is then monotone increasing, and the maximum is
  attained at $u_\sigma$, where the central contour estimate already controls
  it.  This case is already handled by the imported modules and does not
  require the Langer analysis.
- **Turning point at the cap boundary ($u_0=u_\sigma$):** Then the allowed
  region $K_B>0$ is empty inside the cap.  The maximum is again at $u_\sigma$
  and covered by the central estimate.
- **No first critical point ($u_1$ absent):** The Sonin monotonicity then
  forces the maximum to occur at $u_\sigma$, again controlled.

## Counterexample or obstruction search
**(a) Test case $(n,\alpha,\beta)=(10,5,0)$:** All computed quantities are
exact rationals, no inconsistency appears.  The turning point is well inside
the cap, $\gamma>0$, and the removable value is small.

**(b) Boundary $\alpha=1/2$:** The small‑exponent theorem covers the endpoint,
but we check the monotonicity lemma.  At $\alpha=1/2$,
$K_B'(u_\sigma) = \frac14 + \frac{\beta(n+1)}{2B} \ge \frac14$,
so the bound still holds.  The forbidden‑zone ascent also works because
$\alpha>0$.  A limiting argument extends the proof continuously to
$\alpha=1/2$.

**(c) Sample $\alpha$ very close to $\alpha_E(n)$:** Here the energy estimate
already provides a bound stronger than the KKT target, so the endpoint cap
is not even reached; the residual strip is empty in that region.

**(d) Large $\beta$ ($\theta\to0$):** The compactified hypergeometric
representation remains stable, and the rescaled critical cubics are
well‑conditioned.  No algebraic obstruction to interval certification is
seen.

**(e) Sign of $K_B(0)$ for $\alpha>0$:** always $-\alpha^2/4<0$, so the
forbidden zone extends from $u=0$ until the first positive zero.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration
I examined whether a Christoffel–Darboux or Turán identity could replace
the entire Langer/Prufer machinery by giving a direct pointwise upper
bound at the first critical point $u_1$.

- **Christoffel–Darboux kernel:** The reproducing kernel $K_n(x,x)$ is a
  sum of squares of orthogonal polynomials, each with positive weight.
  This gives a *lower* bound for the sum, not an upper bound for a single
  polynomial.  The external root factors $(1-x)^{\alpha/2}(1+x)^{\beta/2}$
  are not part of the kernel weight, so the kernel does not directly bound
  $g_n$.
- **Turán inequality:** For Jacobi polynomials,
  $P_n(x)^2 \ge P_{n-1}(x)P_{n+1}(x)$.  This is again a lower bound, and
  it involves a parameter shift that does not align with the derivative
  relation at a critical point.  No simple conversion to an upper bound
  appears.
- **Product‑formula / hypergroup positivity:** If a positive product formula
  for the normalized $g_n$ existed for real parameters, the unitarity
  argument might be extended.  However, no such formula with the exact KKT
  normalization and required real‑parameter range has been supplied.
- **Parameter interpolation / log‑convexity:** Earlier rounds already
  exposed the flaw in a log‑concavity argument (convexity would be needed
  for an upper bound from endpoint values).  I did not find any new
  interpolation identity.

Therefore I return to the main route.  The 20% exploration is recorded as:
