## Verdict on A2-3, A3-3, and my own prior route

The third-round files do **not** complete the proof. They contain useful fragments, but both "global closure" claims fail at specific algebraic points.

KKT's object is the normalized weighted Jacobi expression \(g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\), and the target is the refined bound
\[
|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]
KKT prove this for integer \(\alpha,\beta\), note that they could not find the noninteger case in the literature, and state Conjecture 6.1 for all \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\). The uploaded review says the same: integer parameters are proven, while the full real-parameter statement remains the conjectural target.

---

# 1. Evaluation of A2-3.tex

A2-3 has one useful idea and one major unsupported jump.

The useful part is the **local shifted Bessel comparison**. Near the right endpoint, with
\[
x=\cos\theta,\qquad
v(\theta)=\sqrt{\sin\theta}\,g(\cos\theta),
\]
one has
\[
v''+Q(\theta)v=0,
\]
where
\[
Q(\theta)
=
N^2
+
\frac{1/4-\alpha^2}{4\sin^2(\theta/2)}
+
\frac{1/4-\beta^2}{4\cos^2(\theta/2)},
\qquad
N=n+\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{2}.
\]
A2-3's idea is to write
\[
Q(\theta)
\ge
\widetilde N^2+\frac{1/4-\alpha^2}{\theta^2}
\]
on a finite right-endpoint interval, absorbing the finite negative part into \(\widetilde N^2\). That is a legitimate comparison template.

But A2-3 does **not** prove the needed global inequalities. The failure is in its Lemma 4, where it asserts a "slack match"
\[
B_R\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
\]
No proof is supplied. The asymptotic statement "the denominator smoothly absorbs the numerator" is not a proof, and it is precisely the hard part. Worse, \(\widetilde N\) is defined using the unknown lobe endpoint \(\theta_1\), so the comparison is partly circular unless one separately proves
\[
\frac{j_{\alpha,1}}{\widetilde N}\le \theta_1
\]
or replaces \(\theta_1\) by an explicit a priori interval length.

So A2-3 is useful as a **local endpoint-comparison ansatz**, not as a completed proof.

---

# 2. Evaluation of A3-3.tex

A3-3 is not usable as a proof. The shifted Sonin functional is algebraically wrong.

A3-3 defines
\[
S_\lambda(x)
=
g(x)^2+\frac{A(x)^2g'(x)^2}{F(x)+\lambda A(x)^2},
\qquad A=1-x^2,
\]
and claims
\[
S_\lambda'
=
-\frac{A^2(F'-2\lambda xA)}
{(F+\lambda A^2)^2}g'^2.
\]

The correct derivative is different. Put
\[
D(x)=F(x)+\lambda A(x)^2.
\]
Using
\[
(Ag')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0,
\]
one gets
\[
\boxed{
S_\lambda'
=
\frac{2\lambda A^2gg'}{D}
-
\frac{A^2\bigl(F'-4\lambda xA\bigr)}{D^2}g'^2.
}
\]
There are two fatal differences:

1. There is an extra mixed term
   \[
   \frac{2\lambda A^2gg'}{D},
   \]
   with no fixed sign.

2. The coefficient of the \(\lambda xA\) term is \(4\), not \(2\).

So \(S_\lambda\) is not a Sonin monotonicity functional.

There is also a second fatal issue: A3-3 says \(D=F+\lambda A^2\) can be made strictly positive on \([-1,1]\). But
\[
A(\pm1)=0,
\]
while
\[
F(1)=-\alpha^2,\qquad F(-1)=-\beta^2.
\]
Thus for \(\alpha,\beta>0\),
\[
D(1)=-\alpha^2<0,\qquad D(-1)=-\beta^2<0,
\]
regardless of how large \(\lambda\) is. The denominator cannot be made positive on the whole interval by adding \(\lambda A^2\).

The claimed "magic" shift
\[
\lambda^*
=
\frac{(\alpha+\beta+1)^2}
{4\left(n+\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{2}\right)^2}
\]
is unsupported and, given the derivative identity above, cannot provide the claimed monotonicity mechanism.

So A3-3 should be discarded except for its general intuition that the raw \(F^{-1}\) Sonin quotient has to be regularized near turning points.

# 3. Evaluation of my own prior route

The following pieces remain valid:
\[
(A g')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0,
\]
where
\[
A=1-x^2,
\]
\[
F(x)
=
\kappa(1-x^2)
-\frac14\bigl(\beta-\alpha-(\alpha+\beta)x\bigr)^2,
\]
\[
\kappa=n(n+\alpha+\beta+1)+\frac{\alpha+\beta}{2}.
\]

On \(F>0\), the direct Sonin functional
\[
S_g(x)=g(x)^2+\frac{A(x)^2g'(x)^2}{F(x)}
\]
satisfies
\[
\boxed{
S_g'(x)
=
-\frac{A(x)^2F'(x)}{F(x)^2}g'(x)^2.
}
\]
Since \(F\) is a concave quadratic, \(S_g\) decreases on the left half of the oscillatory interval and increases on the right half. Hence the largest local extrema of \(|g|\) are forced toward the two outer sides of the oscillatory interval.

The regularized identity is also valid:
\[
\boxed{
W(x):=F(x)g(x)^2+A(x)^2g'(x)^2,
\qquad
W'(x)=F'(x)g(x)^2.
}
\]
This is the right way to cross turning points, not A3's shifted quotient.

My previous endpoint-Bessel criterion is valid as a **conditional reduction**, but it does not close the full proof until the scalar endpoint inequalities are proved. The earlier small-square proof
\[
0\le\alpha,\beta\le\frac12
\]
and the large-balanced proof
\[
\alpha,\beta\ge2n
\]
remain genuine subcase proofs. The adjustable-frequency endpoint comparison remains a tool, not a complete argument.

# 4. New progress: a real-parameter central-region theorem

The best way forward is to import the sharp part of the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull contour proof, but only where its contour is legitimate for noninteger parameters.

Haagerup-Schlichtkrull define the same \(g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\) and prove a uniform Bernstein estimate for all real \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\). In their integer-parameter section, they obtain exactly the KKT refined constant as their preliminary bound. Their own text explains why the circle contour is automatic for integer parameters but, for noninteger parameters, must not enclose \(z=\pm1\).

That observation gives a genuine new analytic lemma.

## Central contour theorem

Let
\[
n\ge1,\qquad \alpha,\beta\ge0,\qquad s=\alpha+\beta.
\]
Assume \(s>0\), and define
\[
\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n}.
\]
Then for every
\[
\boxed{
|x|<\sigma
}
\]
one has
\[
\boxed{
|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
}
\]

### Proof

Set
\[
a=\frac{\alpha}{n},\qquad b=\frac{\beta}{n}.
\]
The Haagerup-Schlichtkrull contour radius is
\[
r=\sqrt{\frac{1-x^2}{a+b+1}}.
\]
For real, noninteger parameters, the Cauchy/Rodrigues contour is valid provided the circle centered at \(x\) of radius \(r\) does not enclose the branch points \(1\) and \(-1\). This is exactly the condition
\[
r<1-x,\qquad r<1+x.
\]
The first inequality is equivalent to
\[
\frac{1-x^2}{a+b+1}<(1-x)^2
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
x<\frac{a+b}{a+b+2}.
\]
The second is equivalent to
\[
x>-\frac{a+b}{a+b+2}.
\]
Since
\[
\frac{a+b}{a+b+2}
=
\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n}
=
\sigma,
\]
the contour is legitimate for all real \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\) whenever \(|x|<\sigma\).

On that contour, the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull saddle estimate gives
\[
|I_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|\le e^{nf(t_0)}.
\]
Their gamma-ratio estimate is
\[
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+s+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}
\left(
\frac{(a+1)^{a+1}(b+1)^{b+1}}
{(a+b+1)^{a+b+1}}
\right)^n
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/2},
\]
which is their inequality (19). Inserting this into the definition of \(g\) gives exactly
\[
|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]
This is the desired bound in the central branch-safe interval.

# 5. Combining the central theorem with Sonin localization

Recall the direct turning points:
\[
x_\pm
=
\frac{ds\pm4\sqrt{\kappa(\kappa+\alpha\beta)}}
{s^2+4\kappa},
\]
where
\[
d=\beta-\alpha,
\qquad
\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac{s}{2}.
\]

For \(\alpha,\beta>0\),
\[
F(x)>0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
x\in(x_-,x_+).
\]
Every nonzero interior extremum of \(g\) lies in \([x_-,x_+]\). Therefore:

## Corollary

If
\[
\boxed{
-\sigma\le x_-<x_+\le \sigma,
\qquad
\sigma=\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n},
}
\]
then the full refined KKT inequality holds for these parameters:
\[
\boxed{
|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}
\quad
(-1\le x\le1).
}
\]

Reason: all possible interior extrema lie in the central interval, where the contour theorem applies. Endpoint values are zero if \(\alpha,\beta>0\); if one parameter is zero, the corresponding endpoint value is \(1\), and the refined constant is also \(1\).

This is a real advance: it proves the sharp bound on the entire central oscillatory regime for arbitrary real parameters, without representation theory.

---

# 6. What remains after this advance

The proof is now reduced to the two endpoint-overflow cases:
\[
\boxed{
x_+>\sigma
}
\]
or
\[
\boxed{
x_-<-\sigma.
}
\]

These are exactly the cases where the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull saddle circle would enclose a branch point, so the real-parameter contour proof is no longer available without extra endpoint analysis.

For the right overflow \(x_+>\sigma\), the correct remaining tool is the endpoint Bessel comparison. For the left overflow \(x_-<-\sigma\), swap \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\).

---

# 7. Correct endpoint criterion, now non-circular

For the right endpoint, set
\[
x=\cos\theta,
\qquad
v(\theta)=\sqrt{\sin\theta}\,g(\cos\theta).
\]
Then
\[
v''+Q(\theta)v=0,
\]
with
\[
Q(\theta)
=
N^2
+
\frac{1/4-\alpha^2}{4\sin^2(\theta/2)}
+
\frac{1/4-\beta^2}{4\cos^2(\theta/2)}.
\]

Choose an explicit endpoint cutoff
\[
\Theta_R=\arccos\sigma
=
\arccos\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n}.
\]
This is not the unknown lobe endpoint; it is explicit.

Define
\[
c_\alpha=\frac14-\alpha^2,\qquad c_\beta=\frac14-\beta^2,
\]
\[
B(\theta)=\frac1{4\sin^2(\theta/2)}-\frac1{\theta^2},
\qquad
C(\theta)=\frac1{4\cos^2(\theta/2)}.
\]
On \(0<\theta\le\Theta_R\), both \(B\) and \(C\) are increasing. Therefore
\[
Q(\theta)\ge M_R^2+\frac{1/4-\alpha^2}{\theta^2},
\]
where
\[
M_R^2
=
N^2
+
m_\alpha(\Theta_R)
+
m_\beta(\Theta_R),
\]
with
\[
m_\alpha(\Theta_R)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{1/4-\alpha^2}{12}, & 0\le\alpha\le\frac12,\\[1.2ex]
(1/4-\alpha^2)B(\Theta_R), & \alpha>\frac12,
\end{cases}
\]
and
\[
m_\beta(\Theta_R)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{1/4-\beta^2}{4}, & 0\le\beta\le\frac12,\\[1.2ex]
(1/4-\beta^2)C(\Theta_R), & \beta>\frac12.
\end{cases}
\]

If
\[
M_R>0
\]
and
\[
\frac{j_{\alpha,1}}{M_R}\le \Theta_R,
\]
then singular Sturm comparison gives
\[
0\le v(\theta)
\le
K_R\sqrt{\theta}J_\alpha(M_R\theta)
\]
through the relevant right endpoint lobe, with
\[
K_R
=
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
{\Gamma(n+1)M_R^\alpha}.
\]

Consequently,
\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le
K_R
\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
J_\alpha(M_R\theta).
\]

Using
\[
J_\alpha(z)
\le
\frac{(z/2)^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha+1)}
\exp\!\left(-\frac{z^2}{4(\alpha+1)}\right)
\qquad
(0\le z\le j_{\alpha,1}),
\]
one obtains the explicit endpoint bound
\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le
D_R\theta^\alpha e^{-c_R\theta^2},
\]
where
\[
D_R
=
\frac{
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)
}
{
\Gamma(n+1)2^\alpha\Gamma(\alpha+1)
},
\]
and
\[
c_R
=
\frac{M_R^2}{4(\alpha+1)}
-
\rho(\Theta_R),
\]
with
\[
\rho(\Theta_R)
=
\sup_{0<t\le\Theta_R}
\frac{1}{2t^2}\log\frac{t}{\sin t}.
\]

If \(c_R>0\), then
\[
\max_{\theta\ge0}\theta^\alpha e^{-c_R\theta^2}
=
\left(\frac{\alpha}{2ec_R}\right)^{\alpha/2}.
\]
Thus the right endpoint lobe is bounded by
\[
\boxed{
E_R
=
D_R
\left(\frac{\alpha}{2ec_R}\right)^{\alpha/2}.
}
\]

So the remaining right endpoint task is the explicit scalar inequality
\[
\boxed{
E_R
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta},
}
\]
where
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

The left endpoint is the same statement with \(\alpha,\beta\) interchanged.

# 8. Current proof status

The proof is now genuinely complete in these regions:
\[
\boxed{
0\le\alpha,\beta\le\frac12
}
\]
by endpoint Bessel comparison;

\[
\boxed{
\alpha,\beta\ge2n
}
\]
by the weighted-energy argument;

\[
\boxed{
[x_-,x_+]\subseteq[-\sigma,\sigma],
\qquad
\sigma=\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n}
}
\]
by the new central contour theorem plus Sonin localization.

The only remaining cases are the endpoint-overflow regimes:
\[
x_+>\sigma
\quad\text{or}\quad
x_-<-\sigma.
\]

A2-3 and A3-3 do not prove these. The concrete remaining work is to prove the two scalar endpoint inequalities
\[
E_R\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta},
\qquad
E_L\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
\]

This is now a much smaller problem than the original conjecture: the central oscillatory region is closed, the balanced-large region is closed, and the small-parameter endpoint regime is closed. The unresolved part is the asymmetric endpoint-overflow zone.
