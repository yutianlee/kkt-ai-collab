According to the documents from May 28, 2026, I do **not** accept the eighth-round claimed closures as a complete proof. A2-8 and A3-8 both still jump over the hard scalar endpoint inequality. A4-8 is the most reliable of the three because it identifies the same obstruction rather than declaring it solved.

## 1. Evaluation of A2-8, A3-8, A4-8

### A2-8

**Useful input.** A2-8 correctly emphasizes the overflow-confinement phenomenon: right endpoint overflow \(x_+>\sigma\) cannot persist for arbitrarily large right endpoint exponent \(\alpha\). Its algebraic idea is valuable. With
\[
s=\alpha+\beta,\qquad
\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n},
\]
one computes
\[
F(\sigma)=\frac{\Phi_R(n,\alpha,\beta)}{s+2n},
\]
where
\[
\Phi_R
=
\beta(-\alpha^2+2n\alpha+3n^2+2n)
+
(-\alpha^3+3n^2\alpha+2n\alpha+2n^3+2n^2).
\]
Hence
\[
x_+>\sigma
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\Phi_R>0.
\]
If
\[
\alpha\ge n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n},
\]
then both the coefficient of \(\beta\) and the remaining constant term are nonpositive, so right overflow is impossible. This matches the useful part of A2-8's "topological confinement" claim. A2-8 explicitly presents this confinement as a core theorem and concludes that large-order overflow is eliminated.

**Fatal gap.** A2-8 then overuses the confinement. The bound
\[
\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}
\]
is not a compact bound independent of \(n\). It still leaves an unbounded transition region as \(n\to\infty\), and it does not eliminate the limit
\[
\beta\to\infty
\]
with fixed or moderately sized \(\alpha\). A2-8 claims that a dual envelope
\[
E_{\rm best}=\min(E_{\rm exp},E_{\rm Landau})
\]
satisfies \(E_{\rm best}\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}\), but the actual proof is replaced by asymptotic language:
\[
K_R \approx
\left(\frac{\sqrt{(n+1)(n+\beta+1)}}{M_R}\right)^\alpha,
\]
followed by "the base is strictly \(<1\)." That is not a rigorous inequality, and it gives no controlled lower bound for \(M_R\).

There is also a concrete misextension: A2-8 says the low-order exponential argument covers \(\alpha\le1\), but the previously validated endpoint theorem only covers
\[
0\le \alpha\le \frac12
\]
on the right lobe, and symmetrically \(0\le\beta\le\frac12\) on the left. A4-8 correctly records that the \(\alpha>1/2\) case remains unproved.

**Verdict.** Keep the overflow polynomial \(\Phi_R\). Reject the claimed dual-bound closure.

---

### A3-8

**Useful input.** A3-8 has the right analytic skeleton. It restates the normalized Jacobi function
\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
=
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
\]
and correctly uses the self-adjoint equation
\[
(Ag')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0,
\qquad A=1-x^2,
\]
with the Sonin functional
\[
S=g^2+\frac{A^2g'^2}{F},
\qquad
S'=-\frac{A^2F'}{F^2}g'^2.
\]
This is the correct localization mechanism. A3-8 also sets up the endpoint Liouville transform and obtains the Bessel comparison envelope
\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le
K_R
\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
J_\alpha(M_R\theta).
\]
Those are useful components.

**Fatal gap.** The proof reduces the remaining endpoint case to inequality (9):
\[
\frac{\sqrt{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}}
{\sqrt{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}}
\frac{1}{M_R^\alpha}
\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\,c\,\alpha^{-1/3}
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
\]
A3-8 then says this follows from Stirling bounds and monotonicity, but the appendix is only a sketch. It contains phrases such as "explicit rational function," "controlled error factor," and "the left-hand side is a decreasing function," without actually giving the function, the error factor, or the derivative sign proof.

There is a sharper concrete problem. A3-8 uses the asserted lower bound
\[
M_R\ge \frac12\left(n+\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{2}\right).
\]
This is false in right-overflow regimes with large \(\beta\). For example, with
\[
n=2,\qquad \alpha=1,\qquad \beta=1000,
\]
one has right overflow:
\[
x_+\approx0.99983265>\sigma\approx0.99601990,
\]
but the shifted comparison frequency is only
\[
M_R\approx50.10,
\]
whereas the claimed lower bound gives
\[
\frac12\left(n+\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{2}\right)=251.5.
\]
So the scalar closure in A3-8 cannot be accepted.

**Verdict.** Keep the Sonin/Bessel architecture. Reject the final algebraic closure.

---

### A4-8

**Useful input.** A4-8 is the most honest of the three. It correctly identifies that Landau's bound and the geometric factor give a legitimate sufficient estimate of the form
\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le
1.23\,C\,K_R\alpha^{-1/3},
\]
but it also correctly says the hard part is proving that the gamma-ratio coefficient \(K_R\alpha^{-1/3}\) stays below \(T\).

A4-8 also correctly rejects proof by asymptotic: the fact that \(K_R\sim(2/e)^\alpha\) in one limit does not control the intermediate regime, especially near \(\alpha=1/2\) or for small \(n\).

**Weakness.** A4-8 tries to continue with gamma-ratio estimates but eventually admits that the full rigorous proof would still require careful constant control and regime splitting. Its last stage remains a proof sketch, not a proof.

**Verdict.** Adopt A4-8's diagnostic stance and its proposed target. Do not claim completion.

---

## 2. Strategy I adopt after reading the three files

The validated strategy is now:

1. Keep the direct Sonin localization.
2. Keep the branch-safe central contour theorem.
3. Keep the weighted-energy estimate.
4. Keep the small endpoint exponent theorem:
   \[
   0\le\alpha\le\frac12
   \quad\text{on the right lobe},\qquad
   0\le\beta\le\frac12
   \quad\text{on the left lobe}.
   \]
5. Keep the overflow-confinement polynomial \(\Phi_R\), but do **not** treat it as closing the proof.
6. Replace the vague "large-order Bessel closure" with a precise remaining target: a sharp endpoint Bessel inequality, whose limiting form is a sharp Laguerre inequality.

The KKT paper defines the normalized \(g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\) in equation (4.4), proves \(|g|\le1\) and the refined estimate only for integer \(\alpha,\beta\), and explicitly states that they could not find the noninteger estimates in the literature. Their Conjecture 6.1 is exactly that (4.8) and Lemma 4.3 extend to all \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\).

## 3. My suggested proof, with details

I will write the proof as a rigorous reduction plus the parts that are actually proved. I do **not** claim the last endpoint lemma is solved.

### 3.1. Setup

Let
\[
s=\alpha+\beta,\qquad d=\beta-\alpha,
\]
and
\[
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}^2
=
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+s+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}.
\]
Set
\[
g(x)
=
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x).
\]
The target is
\[
|g(x)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta},
\]
where
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

The Jacobi differential equation gives
\[
(Ag')'+\frac{F(x)}{A(x)}g=0,
\qquad
A(x)=1-x^2,
\]
where
\[
F(x)
=
\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2,
\qquad
\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac{s}{2}.
\]

### 3.2. Sonin localization

On \(F>0\), define
\[
S(x)=g(x)^2+\frac{A(x)^2g'(x)^2}{F(x)}.
\]
Direct differentiation gives
\[
S'(x)
=
-\frac{A(x)^2F'(x)}{F(x)^2}g'(x)^2.
\]
Since \(F\) is a concave quadratic, \(F'\) changes sign once. Thus \(S\) is monotone toward the vertex and away from it on the two sides.

At a nonzero local extremum of \(g\), \(g'=0\), so
\[
S(x_*)=g(x_*)^2.
\]
Also, a nonzero interior extremum must lie in \(F\ge0\): at a positive local maximum or negative local minimum, the ODE forces \(F\ge0\). Therefore the largest interior extrema are pushed to the two outer endpoint lobes.

The turning points are the roots of \(F\):
\[
x_\pm
=
\frac{ds\pm4\sqrt{\kappa(\kappa+\alpha\beta)}}
{s^2+4\kappa}.
\]

Hence the global proof reduces to bounding the right lobe near \(x_+\) and the left lobe near \(x_-\).

### 3.3. Central contour theorem

Let
\[
\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n}.
\]
The Haagerup-Schlichtkrull saddle contour has radius
\[
r=\sqrt{\frac{1-x^2}{1+s/n}}.
\]
For real noninteger parameters, the contour must avoid the branch points \(\pm1\). The two conditions
\[
r<1-x,\qquad r<1+x
\]
are equivalent to
\[
|x|<\sigma.
\]
On this branch-safe interval, the same saddle estimate gives
\[
|g(x)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
\]

Therefore, if
\[
[x_-,x_+]\subseteq[-\sigma,\sigma],
\]
then all possible interior extrema are already in the central region, and the KKT bound follows.

This is a genuine closed region.

### 3.4. Weighted-energy region

For \(\alpha,\beta>0\), the following identities hold:
\[
\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{g(x)^2}{1-x}\,dx=\frac1\alpha,
\qquad
\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{g(x)^2}{1+x}\,dx=\frac1\beta,
\]
and
\[
\int_{-1}^{1}g(x)^2\,dx=\frac{2}{2n+s+1}.
\]
Also,
\[
\int_{-1}^{1}A(x)g'(x)^2\,dx
=
n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)}
=:E.
\]
Since
\[
\frac1{1-x^2}
=
\frac12\left(\frac1{1-x}+\frac1{1+x}\right),
\]
we get
\[
J:=
\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{g(x)^2}{1-x^2}\,dx
=
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta}.
\]
A two-sided Cauchy-Schwarz argument from the two endpoints gives
\[
|g(x)|^4\le JE.
\]
Thus the KKT bound follows whenever
\[
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta}
\left(
n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)}
\right)
\le
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}.
\]
In particular, this includes the large balanced region
\[
\alpha,\beta\ge2n.
\]

### 3.5. Small endpoint exponents

For right overflow, pass to
\[
x=\cos\theta,
\qquad
v(\theta)=\sqrt{\sin\theta}\,g(\cos\theta).
\]
Then
\[
v''+Q(\theta)v=0,
\]
where
\[
Q(\theta)
=
N^2
+
\frac{1/4-\alpha^2}{4\sin^2(\theta/2)}
+
\frac{1/4-\beta^2}{4\cos^2(\theta/2)},
\qquad
N=n+\frac{s+1}{2}.
\]
On
\[
0<\theta\le\Theta_R:=\arccos\sigma,
\]
one constructs \(M_R>0\) so that
\[
Q(\theta)
\ge
M_R^2+\frac{1/4-\alpha^2}{\theta^2}.
\]
Sturm comparison with
\[
z(\theta)=\sqrt{\theta}J_\alpha(M_R\theta)
\]
gives
\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le
K_R\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
J_\alpha(M_R\theta).
\]

For \(0\le\alpha\le1/2\), the Hadamard product bound for \(J_\alpha\), the corrected geometric estimate
\[
\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
\le
\exp\left(\frac{3\theta^2}{32}\right),
\]
and Wendel-Gautschi gamma-ratio estimates give
\[
|g(\cos\theta)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\]
on the right lobe. Symmetrically, the left lobe is controlled for \(0\le\beta\le1/2\). This is the strongest fully validated endpoint theorem so far.

### 3.6. Exact overflow confinement

Right overflow is equivalent to
\[
F(\sigma)>0.
\]
The exact computation gives
\[
F(\sigma)=\frac{\Phi_R}{s+2n},
\]
where
\[
\Phi_R
=
\beta(-\alpha^2+2n\alpha+3n^2+2n)
+
(-\alpha^3+3n^2\alpha+2n\alpha+2n^3+2n^2).
\]
Therefore
\[
x_+>\sigma
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
\]
Similarly,
\[
x_-<-\sigma
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\beta<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
\]

This is useful but not decisive. The bound grows like \(3n\), and \(\beta\to\infty\) with fixed \(\alpha\) remains a real endpoint problem.

### 3.7. The unavoidable Laguerre endpoint limit

Take
\[
\beta\to\infty,\qquad
x=1-\frac{2u}{\beta}.
\]
Then
\[
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
=
\left(\frac{u}{\beta}\right)^{\alpha/2},
\]
\[
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}
\to e^{-u/2},
\]
and the Jacobi-to-Laguerre limit gives
\[
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
\to
L_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]
The normalization satisfies
\[
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}^2
\sim
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}\beta^\alpha.
\]
Therefore
\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
\to
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\]
where
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]
Also,
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}^4
\to
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}.
\]
Thus the endpoint proof must contain, at minimum, the sharp Laguerre inequality
\[
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4},
\qquad
u\ge0.
\tag{LAG}
\]

This is the clean limiting obstruction.

### 3.8. What I can prove in the Laguerre limit

Let
\[
y(u)=\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]
Then
\[
(uy')'
+
\left(
n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2}
-\frac{u}{4}
-\frac{\alpha^2}{4u}
\right)y=0.
\]
The standard normalized Laguerre identities give
\[
\int_0^\infty y(u)^2\,du=1,
\]
\[
\int_0^\infty \frac{y(u)^2}{u}\,du=\frac1\alpha,
\]
and
\[
\int_0^\infty u\,y'(u)^2\,du=\frac{2n+1}{4}.
\]
Cauchy-Schwarz yields
\[
|y(u)|^4
\le
\left(\int_0^\infty \frac{y(t)^2}{t}\,dt\right)
\left(\int_0^\infty t\,y'(t)^2\,dt\right)
=
\frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}.
\]
Therefore \((\mathrm{LAG})\) follows whenever
\[
\frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}
\le
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1},
\]
i.e.
\[
\alpha\ge
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]
Combined with the small endpoint theorem, the remaining Laguerre strip is
\[
\boxed{
\frac12<\alpha<
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
}
\]

This is a genuine narrowing, but it is not the full conjecture.

## 4. Status of my proof

**Status: not complete.**

The proof is rigorous in the following regimes:
\[
[x_-,x_+]\subseteq[-\sigma,\sigma]
\]
by Sonin localization plus the central contour theorem;

\[
0\le\alpha\le\frac12
\]
for the right endpoint lobe, and
\[
0\le\beta\le\frac12
\]
for the left endpoint lobe;

\[
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta}
\left(
n+\frac12-\frac1{2(2n+s+1)}
\right)
\le
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\]
by weighted energy, including \(\alpha,\beta\ge2n\);

and in the Laguerre endpoint limit for
\[
\alpha\ge
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]

The exact remaining obstruction is:
\[
\boxed{
K_R
\sup_{0<\theta\le\Theta_R}
\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
J_\alpha(M_R\theta)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
}
\tag{BES}
\]
for the right overflow case with \(\alpha>1/2\), outside the energy and central regions, and its symmetric left version with \(\beta>1/2\).

Equivalently, in the endpoint blow-up limit, one must prove
\[
\boxed{
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4}
}
\tag{LAG}
\]
in the remaining strip
\[
\frac12<\alpha<
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}.
\]

That is the current honest endpoint. A2-8 and A3-8 do not close it; A4-8 correctly points toward sharper Bessel maxima plus explicit Stirling/interval verification, but does not execute it.
