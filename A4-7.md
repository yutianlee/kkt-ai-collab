## Part 1: The Conjecture and the Shared Proof Architecture

KKT Conjecture 6.1 (Koornwinder-Kostenko-Teschl, 2018) states that for all
\[
\alpha,\beta\ge0,\qquad n\ge1,\qquad x\in[-1,1],
\]
one has
\[
|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
:=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

All three AI responses converge on the same modular proof architecture. I believe the established parts of this architecture are correct.

| Module | Content | Status |
| --- | --- | --- |
| M1: Sonin-Polya localization | The ODE \((Ag')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0\), with Sonin functional \(S_g=g^2+A^2g'^2/F\), shows that the global maximum of \(g\) lies in the outermost lobes near the turning points \(x_\pm\). | Rigorous |
| M2: Central contour theorem | On the branch-safe region \(\lvert x\rvert<\sigma\), with \(\sigma=(\alpha+\beta)/(\alpha+\beta+2n)\), the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull contour estimate gives the desired KKT bound. | Rigorous |
| M3: Small endpoint exponent theorem | The right endpoint lobe is controlled when \(0\le\alpha\le1/2\); by symmetry, the left endpoint lobe is controlled when \(0\le\beta\le1/2\). | Rigorous |
| M4: Weighted-energy region | A weighted-energy estimate controls a large balanced region, including \(\alpha,\beta\ge2n\). | Rigorous |
| M5: Large-order endpoint lobe | The remaining case is an overflowing endpoint with exponent \(>1/2\): \(\alpha>1/2\) on the right or \(\beta>1/2\) on the left. | Sole remaining obstruction |

Thus the central unresolved problem is the **large-order endpoint Bessel-envelope lemma**.

## Part 3: Attempting to Complete the Proof

I now try to close the gap rigorously, building on A1-6's framework and A2-6's Landau-bound idea.

### Precise Endpoint Lemma Needed

The desired large-order endpoint lemma is:

For all
\[
n\ge1,\qquad \alpha>\frac12,\qquad \beta\ge0,
\]
in the right-overflow regime \(x_+>\sigma\), prove
\[
K_R
\sup_{0<\theta\le\Theta_R}
\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
J_\alpha(M_R\theta)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
\tag{L}
\]
The left endpoint case is symmetric, with \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) interchanged.

### Step 1: Use Landau's Bessel Bound

For \(\alpha\ge1/2\), Landau's bound gives
\[
\sup_{x\ge0}|J_\alpha(x)|
\le
c_L\alpha^{-1/3},
\qquad
c_L=
\frac{2^{1/3}}{3^{2/3}\Gamma(2/3)}
\approx0.6749.
\]

Also, since \(0<\Theta_R\le\pi/2\),
\[
\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
\le
\sqrt{\frac\pi2}.
\]

Thus it would suffice to prove
\[
\sqrt{\frac\pi2}\,c_L\,\alpha^{-1/3}K_R
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
\tag{*}
\]

### Step 2: Expand \(K_R/T\)

Recall
\[
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}^2
=
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}.
\]
The endpoint matching constant is
\[
K_R
=
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
{\Gamma(n+1)M_R^\alpha}.
\]
Hence
\[
K_R
=
\frac1{M_R^\alpha}
\left(
\frac{
\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)
}
{
\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)
}
\right)^{1/2}.
\]

Therefore \((*)\) becomes
\[
\sqrt{\frac\pi2}\,c_L\,\alpha^{-1/3}
\frac1{M_R^\alpha}
\left(
\frac{
\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)
}
{
\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)
}
\right)^{1/2}
\le
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

### Step 3: The Gamma-Ratio Problem

The hard factor is
\[
\left(
\frac{
\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)
}
{
\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)
}
\right)^{1/2}.
\]

A crude bound such as
\[
\frac{\Gamma(x+s)}{\Gamma(x)}\le (x+s)^s
\]
is too weak, because it gives
\[
K_R
\lesssim
\left(\frac{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}{M_R^2}\right)^{\alpha/2},
\]
and the numerator is often comparable to or larger than \(M_R^2\).

What is needed is a sharp Stirling-type estimate with explicit error terms, such as Robbins' bounds:
\[
\sqrt{2\pi}\,x^{x-1/2}e^{-x+\frac{1}{12x+1}}
\le
\Gamma(x)
\le
\sqrt{2\pi}\,x^{x-1/2}e^{-x+\frac{1}{12x}}.
\]

Those estimates should convert \((*)\) into an explicit inequality in \(n,\alpha,\beta\), but this conversion is still nontrivial.

## Part 4: Understanding the Overflow Region

The right-overflow condition is
\[
x_+>\sigma,
\]
where
\[
x_+
=
\frac{ds+4\sqrt{\kappa(\kappa+\alpha\beta)}}
{s^2+4\kappa},
\qquad
\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n},
\]
with
\[
s=\alpha+\beta,\qquad d=\beta-\alpha,
\qquad
\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac{s}{2}.
\]

Equivalently,
\[
\frac{ds+4\sqrt{\kappa(\kappa+\alpha\beta)}}{s^2+4\kappa}
>
\frac{s}{s+2n}.
\]
Cross-multiplication gives
\[
4(s+2n)\sqrt{\kappa(\kappa+\alpha\beta)}
>
s^3+4s\kappa-ds(s+2n).
\]
Since \(d=\beta-\alpha\), this is
\[
4(s+2n)\sqrt{\kappa(\kappa+\alpha\beta)}
>
s^3+4s\kappa+(\alpha^2-\beta^2)(s+2n).
\]

Squaring would yield a polynomial inequality in \(n,\alpha,\beta\). As \(\alpha\to\infty\) with \(n,\beta\) fixed, the left side grows like \(O(\alpha^4)\), whereas the squared right side grows like \(O(\alpha^6)\). Hence right overflow cannot persist for arbitrarily large \(\alpha\) at fixed \(n,\beta\).

This suggests the following useful structural lemma:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Right overflow with }\alpha>\frac12\text{ forces }\alpha\le C(n+\beta)
\text{ for an absolute constant }C.
}
\]

Such a lemma would restrict the remaining endpoint problem to a bounded transition region relative to \(n+\beta\).

## Part 5: Numerical Transition Checks

The difficult regime is not very large \(\alpha\), where asymptotic decay helps. It is the transition range just above \(\alpha=1/2\), especially when \(n\) is small and \(\beta\) is small.

## Part 6: Why Landau Alone Is Not the Final Proof

Landau's bound is sharp asymptotically but loose near \(\alpha=1/2\). For example,
\[
J_{1/2}(x)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi x}}\sin x,
\]
so
\[
\max_x J_{1/2}(x)=\frac{2}{\pi}\approx0.6366.
\]
Landau's bound at \(\alpha=1/2\) gives
\[
c_L(1/2)^{-1/3}\approx0.851,
\]
which is about \(34\%\) too large.

Therefore, a complete proof should use a sharper bound for the first Bessel maximum:
\[
J_\alpha(j'_{\alpha,1}),
\]
rather than the global Landau bound alone. A suitable refinement would have the form
\[
J_\alpha(j'_{\alpha,1})
\le
\frac{b}{(\alpha+a)^{1/3}}
\]
with constants strong enough at \(\alpha=1/2\) and asymptotically sharp as \(\alpha\to\infty\).

## Part 7: Proposed Completion Strategy

The following strategy appears most promising.

### Step 7.1: Prove an Overflow-Boundedness Lemma

Show that right overflow implies a bound of the form
\[
\alpha\le C(n+\beta)
\]
for an absolute constant \(C\). This prevents the hard regime from extending to arbitrary \(\alpha\) at fixed \(n,\beta\).

### Step 7.2: Use a Sharp Bessel-Maximum Bound

Replace Landau's global bound by a sharper large-order estimate, for example a Krasikov- or Elbert-type inequality:
\[
\max_{x\ge0}J_\alpha(x)
=
J_\alpha(j'_{\alpha,1})
\le
B(\alpha),
\]
where \(B(\alpha)\) is sharp enough for \(\alpha\in[1/2,2]\) and has the correct \(\alpha^{-1/3}\) asymptotics.

### Step 7.3: Use Explicit Stirling Bounds for \(K_R\)

Apply Robbins' Stirling inequalities to
\[
K_R
=
\frac1{M_R^\alpha}
\left(
\frac{
\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)
}
{
\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)
}
\right)^{1/2}.
\]
This should produce an explicit upper bound
\[
K_R\le K_R^{\mathrm{up}}(n,\alpha,\beta).
\]

### Step 7.4: Verify the Reduced Inequality

The desired endpoint lemma would follow from
\[
\left(\frac{\Theta_R}{\sin\Theta_R}\right)^{1/2}
B(\alpha)
K_R^{\mathrm{up}}(n,\alpha,\beta)
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}.
\]

The remaining verification should split into:

- a large-\(n\) analytic region, where Stirling bounds and overflow constraints give monotonicity;
- a finite small-\(n\) region, where interval arithmetic can rigorously verify the inequality.
