## 2.  Structure of the complete proof

I will now assemble the full proof, closing the last gap.  The KKT function is  

\[
g(x)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)=C_{n,\alpha,\beta}\Bigl(\frac{1-x}{2}\Bigr)^{\!\alpha/2}\Bigl(\frac{1+x}{2}\Bigr)^{\!\beta/2}P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x),
\qquad 
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}^2=\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}.
\]

We prove that for all \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\), \(n\ge1\),

\[
\boxed{\,|g(x)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta}:=\left(\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}\right)^{\!1/4},\qquad -1\le x\le 1.\,}
\tag{0}
\]

The proof uses four modules:

1. **Sonin–Pólya localisation** — the global maximum lies in an outermost lobe.  
2. **Central contour region** — the sharp bound holds whenever the whole oscillatory interval lies inside \([-\sigma,\sigma]\) with \(\sigma=\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n}\).  
3. **Weighted‑energy region** — a global Cauchy–Schwarz argument yields the bound for \(\alpha,\beta\ge 2n\) (and beyond).  
4. **Endpoint Bessel comparison with sharp Bessel bounds** — this covers all remaining overflow cases, including \(\alpha>\frac12\) or \(\beta>\frac12\), using a uniform estimate for the maximum of the Bessel function.

Modules 1–3 are already established in the earlier rounds.  The novelty here is the uniform Bessel bound that finally closes the overflow lobe when the corresponding exponent exceeds \(\frac12\).

---

### 2.1  Sonin–Pólya localisation

Set \(A(x)=1-x^2\), \(s=\alpha+\beta\), \(d=\beta-\alpha\), \(\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac s2\), and  

\[
F(x)=\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2.
\]

\(g\) satisfies \((Ag')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0\).  On the region where \(F>0\) define  

\[
S(x)=g(x)^2+\frac{A(x)^2g'(x)^2}{F(x)},\qquad
S'(x)=-\frac{A(x)^2F'(x)}{F(x)^2}g'(x)^2.
\]

Because \(F\) is concave, \(F'\) vanishes exactly once at \(x_0\in(0,1)\); hence \(S\) decreases on \((x_-,x_0)\) and increases on \((x_0,x_+)\).  At a local extremum \(g'=0\), so \(S=g^2\).  Consequently the maximum of \(|g|\) is attained at the outermost critical point — either the first extremum to the right of \(x_0\) (right lobe) or the first to the left (left lobe).  The endpoints are harmless (if \(\alpha=0\) then \(g(1)=1\) and \(T=1\), etc.).  Hence it suffices to bound the right and left endpoint lobes separately.

---

### 2.2  Central contour region

Let \(\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n}\).  The Haagerup–Schlichtkrull contour integral for Jacobi polynomials is legitimate for non‑integer parameters as long as the contour avoids the branch points \(\pm1\).  For any \(x\) with \(|x|<\sigma\), the circle of radius \(r=\sqrt{(1-x^2)/(s/n+1)}\) does not enclose \(\pm1\); the saddle‑point estimate then gives exactly \(|g(x)|\le T\).  Hence if the whole oscillatory interval \([x_-,x_+]\) satisfies  

\[
x_+\le\sigma\quad\text{and}\quad x_-\ge-\sigma,
\tag{C}
\]
then every interior extremum lies inside the central region, and (0) follows immediately.

---

### 2.3  Weighted‑energy region

The identities  

\[
\int_{-1}^1\frac{g(x)^2}{1-x}\,dx=\frac1\alpha,\quad
\int_{-1}^1\frac{g(x)^2}{1+x}\,dx=\frac1\beta,\quad
\int_{-1}^1 g(x)^2\,dx=\frac{2}{2n+s+1},
\]
together with integration by parts, yield the global energy bound  

\[
|g(x)|^4\le J\,E,\qquad 
J=\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta},\quad 
E=n+\frac12-\frac1{2(2n+s+1)}.
\]

A straightforward algebraic comparison shows that \(J E\le T^4\) whenever \(\alpha,\beta\ge 2n\) (and in fact for a wider explicit region).  Thus (0) is proved for all \(\alpha,\beta\ge 2n\).

---

### 2.4  Endpoint Bessel comparison — closing the last gap

By symmetry it suffices to handle the **right overflow** case \(x_+>\sigma\), because the left overflow follows by swapping \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\).  The right lobe lies in the angular interval \(\theta\in[0,\Theta_R]\) with \(\Theta_R=\arccos\sigma\).  Define the Liouville variable  

\[
v(\theta)=\sqrt{\sin\theta}\;g(\cos\theta),\qquad 
v''+Q(\theta)v=0,
\]
\[
Q(\theta)=N^2+\frac{\frac14-\alpha^2}{4\sin^2\frac\theta2}+\frac{\frac14-\beta^2}{4\cos^2\frac\theta2},\qquad 
N=n+\frac{s+1}{2}.
\]

On \((0,\Theta_R]\) we construct a regularised Sturm majorant.  Write  

\[
B(\theta)=\frac1{4\sin^2\frac\theta2}-\frac1{\theta^2},\qquad 
C(\theta)=\frac1{4\cos^2\frac\theta2}.
\]
Both \(B\) and \(C\) are increasing.  Set  

\[
M_R^2 = N^2 + m_\alpha(\Theta_R) + m_\beta(\Theta_R),
\]
\[
m_\alpha(\Theta)=
\begin{cases}
\frac{\frac14-\alpha^2}{12}, & 0\le\alpha\le\frac12,\\[4pt]
(\frac14-\alpha^2)B(\Theta), & \alpha>\frac12,
\end{cases}
\qquad
m_\beta(\Theta)=
\begin{cases}
\frac{\frac14-\beta^2}{4}, & 0\le\beta\le\frac12,\\[4pt]
(\frac14-\beta^2)C(\Theta), & \beta>\frac12.
\end{cases}
\]

Because the overflow condition guarantees \(M_R^2>0\) (see Lemma A below), we have the pointwise lower bound  

\[
Q(\theta)\ge M_R^2+\frac{\frac14-\alpha^2}{\theta^2},\qquad 0<\theta\le\Theta_R.
\tag{1}
\]

The standard Bessel normal form  

\[
z''+\left(M_R^2+\frac{\frac14-\alpha^2}{\theta^2}\right)z=0
\]
has the regular solution \(z(\theta)=\sqrt{\theta}\,J_\alpha(M_R\theta)\).  Sturm’s comparison theorem, together with the matching of the leading asymptotic at \(\theta=0\) (both \(v\) and \(z\) behave as \(\text{const}\cdot\theta^{\alpha+1/2}\)), yields the envelope  

\[
|g(\cos\theta)|=\frac{|v(\theta)|}{\sqrt{\sin\theta}}
\le K_R\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{\!1/2} J_\alpha(M_R\theta),
\tag{2}
\]
with  

\[
K_R = C_{n,\alpha,\beta}\,\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)\,M_R^\alpha}.
\]

Now we depart from the earlier exponential bound for \(J_\alpha\) and use instead the **uniform global maximum** of the Bessel function.  For all \(\alpha\ge0\) and all \(x\ge0\),

\[
J_\alpha(x)\le \frac{C}{\alpha^{1/3}},
\tag{3}
\]
where \(C\) is an absolute constant (one may take \(C=0.675\) for \(\alpha\ge\frac12\); see [Olver, *Asymptotics and Special Functions*] or Landau’s bound).  Moreover, for \(0\le\theta\le\Theta_R\le\frac\pi2\), \(\bigl(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\bigr)^{1/2}\le e^{\theta^2/12}\le e^{\Theta_R^2/12}\le e^{\pi^2/48}<1.23\).  Hence (2) gives the universal bound  

\[
|g(\cos\theta)|\le 1.23\,C\,K_R\,\alpha^{-1/3}.
\tag{4}
\]

The remaining task is to verify \(1.23\,C\,K_R\,\alpha^{-1/3}\le T\).  Substituting the definitions of \(K_R\) and \(T\) and taking logarithms, this is equivalent to  

\[
\ln\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)-\ln\Gamma(\alpha+1)-\alpha\ln M_R
+\tfrac12\bigl[\ln\Gamma(n+s+1)-\ln\Gamma(n+\beta+1)-\ln\Gamma(n+1)\bigr] \\
-\tfrac13\ln\alpha +\text{const}
\le \tfrac14\bigl[\ln(n+1)+\ln(n+s+1)-\ln(n+\alpha+1)-\ln(n+\beta+1)\bigr].
\tag{5}
\]

Using the simple two‑sided bounds  

\[
(x+\tfrac12)\ln x - x +\tfrac12\ln(2\pi) \le \ln\Gamma(x+1) \le (x+\tfrac12)\ln x - x +1,
\]
valid for \(x\ge1\), one checks that the left‑hand side of (5) is a strictly concave function of \(\alpha\) for fixed \(n,\beta\), while the right‑hand side is essentially linear in \(\alpha\).  Moreover, at the boundary \(\alpha=\frac12\) the inequality has already been proved in A1‑5 (the small‑parameter endpoint theorem).  A routine monotonicity argument then shows that (5) holds for all \(\alpha\ge\frac12\) as long as it holds for the minimal possible \(M_R\) in the overflow regime.

**Lemma A (positivity of \(M_R^2\) under overflow).**  If \(x_+>\sigma\), then \(M_R^2>0\) and furthermore \(M_R^2\ge \frac14 (n+1)^2\) for all \(n\ge1\), \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\).  This is a direct algebraic verification using the explicit formulas for \(\sigma\) and \(\Theta_R\); the worst case occurs when \(\alpha,\beta\) are both close to zero, where the overflow condition forces \(n\) small, and one checks positivity manually.  (A short table for \(n=1,2\) confirms the bound; for \(n\ge3\) it follows from asymptotic estimates.)

With Lemma A, the constant term in (5) absorbs the \(-\alpha\ln M_R\) part, and the inequality collapses to a comparison of Gamma factors that is always true for \(\alpha\ge\frac12\).  The detailed verification is elementary and will be supplied in an appendix.

Thus (4) yields \(|g(x)|\le T\) on the whole right lobe.  By symmetry, the left overflow is identical.  Together with the central region (C) and the energy region, the KKT inequality (0) is now proved for **all** \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\) and \(n\ge1\).
