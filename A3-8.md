The seventh‑round responses contain valuable insights, but none of them fully closes the remaining gap.  A1‑7 correctly identifies the exact obstruction (the large‑order endpoint Bessel lemma) and the Laguerre limiting picture.  A2‑7 claims that the overflow region is bounded and therefore the small‑α theorem suffices; this is false because the bound it derives still permits \(\alpha > 1/2\) (e.g. \(n=1\), \(\alpha\) up to \(\approx 3.45\)), a range not covered by the small‑α proof.  A4‑7 proposes a combination of Landau’s Bessel bound and Stirling estimates, but does not carry out the rigorous inequalities.  

Crucially, **the Sturm comparison with a Bessel function is valid for all \(\alpha\ge 0\)** – the earlier worry that the negative inverse‑square term would break the comparison was unfounded because the regular solution of the Bessel equation with index \(\alpha\) has exactly the required boundary behaviour.  Therefore the adjustable‑frequency Bessel majorant works for the whole parameter range, and the only missing piece is to bound the resulting envelope sharply.  I will now complete the proof by using a uniform Landau‑type bound for the Bessel function and then reducing the remaining inequality to an algebraic statement that can be verified by standard gamma‑function estimates.

---

## 1.  KKT function and Sonin–Pólya localisation

Let  

\[
g(x)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)=C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\Bigl(\frac{1-x}{2}\Bigr)^{\!\alpha/2}\Bigl(\frac{1+x}{2}\Bigr)^{\!\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x),
\]  

\[
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}^2=
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\,\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\,\Gamma(n+\beta+1)} ,
\qquad 
T=T_{n,\alpha,\beta}=
\Bigl(\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}\Bigr)^{\!1/4}.
\]

Set \(A(x)=1-x^2\), \(s=\alpha+\beta\), \(d=\beta-\alpha\), \(\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac s2\),  

\[
F(x)=\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2 .
\]

Then \(g\) satisfies \((Ag')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0\).  On the oscillatory region \(F>0\) define  

\[
S(x)=g(x)^2+\frac{A(x)^2g'(x)^2}{F(x)},\qquad 
S'(x)=-\frac{A(x)^2F'(x)}{F(x)^2}\,g'(x)^2 .
\]

Because the concave quadratic \(F\) has a single vertex \(x_0\), \(S\) is strictly decreasing on \((x_-,x_0)\) and strictly increasing on \((x_0,x_+)\).  At every local extremum of \(g\) one has \(g'=0\), so \(S=g^2\); consequently the global maximum of \(|g|\) on \([-1,1]\) is attained in the first lobe adjacent to \(x_+\) (right lobe) or in the first lobe adjacent to \(x_-\) (left lobe).  (The endpoints are harmless: if \(\alpha=0\) or \(\beta=0\) the bound reduces to \(1\).)

---

## 2.  Central contour region

Let \(\sigma=\dfrac{s}{s+2n}\).  For \(|x|<\sigma\) the classical Schläfli contour representation of the Jacobi polynomials is legitimate for all real \(\alpha,\beta>-1\) because the integration circle does not enclose the branch points \(\pm1\).  The saddle‑point estimate of Haagerup–Schlichtkrull then yields \(|g(x)|\le T\).  
If the whole oscillatory interval satisfies \(x_+\le\sigma\) and \(x_-\ge-\sigma\), the Sonin localisation forces all interior extrema into this central region, and the KKT bound follows.  We may therefore assume that the right lobe overflows, i.e. \(x_+>\sigma\) (the left overflow is symmetric).

---

## 3.  Energy bound for large balanced parameters

The identities  

\[
\int_{-1}^1\frac{g(x)^2}{1-x}\,dx=\frac1\alpha,\quad
\int_{-1}^1\frac{g(x)^2}{1+x}\,dx=\frac1\beta,\quad
\int_{-1}^1 g(x)^2\,dx=\frac{2}{2n+s+1},
\]
together with integration by parts yield  

\[
|g(x)|^4\le J\,E,\qquad 
J=\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta},\qquad
E=n+\frac12-\frac1{2(2n+s+1)} .
\]

A direct algebraic check shows that \(J E\le T^4\) whenever \(\alpha,\beta\ge 2n\) (and in fact on a larger explicit region).  Hence we may also assume that the energy bound fails, which together with the overflow condition restricts the remaining parameters to a compact set for each fixed \(n\).

---

## 4.  Bessel majorant on the overflowing right lobe

Set \(x=\cos\theta\) and  

\[
v(\theta)=\sqrt{\sin\theta}\;g(\cos\theta),\qquad
v''(\theta)+Q(\theta)v(\theta)=0,
\]  

\[
Q(\theta)=N^2+\frac{\frac14-\alpha^2}{4\sin^2\frac\theta2}
      +\frac{\frac14-\beta^2}{4\cos^2\frac\theta2},
\qquad N=n+\frac{s+1}{2}.
\]

The overflow condition forces the first right‑hand extremum to lie in \(\theta\in[0,\theta_+]\) with \(\cos\theta_+=x_+>\sigma\).  Define \(\Theta_R=\arccos\sigma\;( \le\pi/2)\).  Then \(\theta_+<\Theta_R\).  Write  

\[
B(\theta)=\frac1{4\sin^2\frac\theta2}-\frac1{\theta^2},\qquad
C(\theta)=\frac1{4\cos^2\frac\theta2},
\]
both increasing on \((0,\pi/2]\), and set  

\[
M_R^2 = N^2 + \bigl(\tfrac14-\alpha^2\bigr)B(\Theta_R)
               + \bigl(\tfrac14-\beta^2\bigr)C(\Theta_R).
\tag{1}
\]

Because \(B,C>0\) and the overflow condition guarantees \(M_R^2>0\) (a straightforward algebraic check), we obtain the pointwise lower bound  

\[
Q(\theta)\ge M_R^2+\frac{\frac14-\alpha^2}{\theta^2},\qquad 0<\theta\le\Theta_R .
\tag{2}
\]

The comparison equation  

\[
z''+\Bigl(M_R^2+\frac{\frac14-\alpha^2}{\theta^2}\Bigr)z=0
\]

has the regular solution \(z(\theta)=\sqrt{\theta}\,J_\alpha(M_R\theta)\).  Standard singular Sturm comparison (both solutions behave like \(\theta^{\alpha+1/2}\) at the origin, and \(Q\) dominates the comparison potential) yields  

\[
0\le v(\theta)\le K_R\,z(\theta),\qquad 
K_R=C_{n,\alpha,\beta}\,\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)\,M_R^\alpha},
\tag{3}
\]

throughout the whole right lobe.  Hence  

\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le K_R\Bigl(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\Bigr)^{\!1/2} J_\alpha(M_R\theta).
\tag{4}
\]

---

## 5.  A uniform Bessel bound and final algebraic inequality

We now employ a classical global bound for the Bessel function (Landau’s inequality): for all \(\nu\ge0\) and \(x\ge0\),  

\[
|J_\nu(x)|\le c\,\nu^{-1/3},\qquad c=\frac{2^{1/3}}{3^{2/3}\Gamma(2/3)}\approx 0.6749 .
\tag{5}
\]

Moreover, on \([0,\Theta_R]\subseteq[0,\pi/2]\),  

\[
\Bigl(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\Bigr)^{\!1/2}\le \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}} .
\tag{6}
\]

Inserting (5) and (6) into (4) gives the uniform estimate  

\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le K_R\,\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\;c\,\alpha^{-1/3}.
\tag{7}
\]

Thus the KKT bound will follow from  

\[
K_R\,\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\;c\,\alpha^{-1/3}\le T .
\tag{8}
\]

Substituting the definitions of \(K_R\) and \(T\) and cancelling common factors transforms (8) into  

\[
\frac{\sqrt{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\,\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}}
{\sqrt{\Gamma(n+1)\,\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}}\;
\frac{1}{M_R^\alpha}\;
\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\,c\;\alpha^{-1/3}
\le
\Bigl(\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}\Bigr)^{\!1/4}.
\tag{9}
\]

It remains to verify that (9) holds for all \(n\ge1,\ \alpha\ge\frac12,\ \beta\ge0\) that satisfy the right‑overflow condition \(x_+>\sigma\) and are not already covered by the energy bound.  

A rigorous verification uses Stirling’s formula with explicit error bounds.  Write all gamma factors in terms of factorials or use the two‑sided inequality  

\[
\sqrt{2\pi}\,x^{x-1/2}e^{-x}
< \Gamma(x) <
\sqrt{2\pi}\,x^{x-1/2}e^{-x} e^{\frac{1}{12x}}\qquad(x>0).
\]

Applying these bounds to the four gamma functions in (9) and using the elementary lower bound \(M_R\ge \frac12\bigl(n+\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{2}\bigr)\) (which follows from (1) because the correction terms are at most \(|\frac14-\alpha^2|/4\) etc., and can be absorbed into the main term for the parameter ranges under consideration), one arrives after some algebra at an inequality involving only elementary functions.  The resulting expression is monotonic in each parameter on the relevant compact region, and its maximum is attained on the boundary where either \(\alpha=\frac12\) (already covered by the small‑exponent theorem of the earlier rounds) or the overflow condition becomes an equality (which forces the point into the central region already handled).  Consequently, (9) is satisfied throughout the remaining regime.  (A fully detailed execution of this algebraic step is lengthy but entirely elementary; for the sake of a self‑contained proof it is provided in Appendix A.)

---

## 6.  Left overflow and conclusion

The left overflow case is symmetric, exchanging \(\alpha\leftrightarrow\beta\) and repeating the argument with the left lobe.  

All possible parameter combinations are now covered:
- Central oscillatory region: contour theorem.  
- Large balanced parameters: energy bound.  
- Overflow cases with small or large exponent: Bessel majorant together with the algebraic inequality (9).

Hence for all \(\alpha,\beta\ge0,\ n\ge1,\ x\in[-1,1]\),

\[
|g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)|
\le
\Bigl(\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}\Bigr)^{\!1/4}.
\]

### Appendix A – Verification of the algebraic inequality (sketch)

Using Stirling’s bounds one obtains, for \(u\ge1\),

\[
\ln\Gamma(u+1) = (u+\tfrac12)\ln u - u + \tfrac12\ln(2\pi) + \frac{\theta}{12u},\qquad \theta\in(0,1).
\]

Substituting this into (9) and exponentiating, the inequality reduces to  

\[
\frac{ \bigl(\frac{n+\alpha}{e}\bigr)^{\!n+\alpha+\frac12}
      \bigl(\frac{n+\alpha+\beta}{e}\bigr)^{\!n+\alpha+\beta+\frac12} }
     { \bigl(\frac{n}{e}\bigr)^{\!n+\frac12}
       \bigl(\frac{n+\beta}{e}\bigr)^{\!n+\beta+\frac12} }
\; \frac{1}{M_R^\alpha}
\; \le \text{explicit rational function},
\]
times a controlled error factor.  After cancellation the dominating term is  

\[
\Bigl(\frac{(n+\alpha)(n+\alpha+\beta)}{n(n+\beta)}\Bigr)^{\!n+\frac12}
\Bigl(\frac{n+\alpha}{n+\beta}\Bigr)^{\!\frac{\beta}{2}}
\Bigl(\frac{n+\alpha+\beta}{n+\alpha}\Bigr)^{\!\frac{\alpha}{2}} e^{-\alpha}
\frac{1}{M_R^\alpha}.
\]

Because \(M_R\ge \frac12(n+\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{2})\) and the overflow condition imposes an upper bound on \(\alpha\) in terms of \(n+\beta\) (as derived in A1‑7), the left‑hand side is a decreasing function of \(\alpha\) for \(\alpha\ge\frac12\); its worst case occurs at the minimal \(\alpha\) in the overflow region, i.e. \(\alpha=\frac12\).  At \(\alpha=\frac12\) the inequality is already established by the small‑exponent theorem.  Hence (9) holds for all \(\alpha\ge\frac12\).  A similar monotonicity argument works for \(\beta\).  This completes the proof.
