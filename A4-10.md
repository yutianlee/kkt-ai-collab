## 2. Proof Attempt: Completing the KKT Conjecture

I now give a proof attempt that combines the validated modules with a new proposed route through the remaining Laguerre strip.

### Phase 1: Validated Global Reductions

Let
\[
g(x)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
=
C_{n,\alpha,\beta}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x).
\]
The target is
\[
|g(x)|\le T_{n,\alpha,\beta},
\]
where
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
=
\left(
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right)^{1/4}.
\]

The validated modules are:

1. **Sonin-Polya localization.** The ODE
   \[
   (Ag')'+\frac{F}{A}g=0
   \]
   with Sonin functional
   \[
   S=g^2+\frac{A^2g'^2}{F}
   \]
   localizes the global maximum to endpoint lobes.

2. **Central contour theorem.** If
   \[
   [x_-,x_+]\subseteq[-\sigma,\sigma],
   \qquad
   \sigma=\frac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha+\beta+2n},
   \]
   then the KKT bound follows from the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull contour estimate.

3. **Weighted energy.** If
   \[
   \frac{s}{2\alpha\beta}
   \left(
   n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)}
   \right)
   \le
   \frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
   {(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)},
   \]
   then the KKT bound follows.

4. **Small endpoint exponent.** The right endpoint lobe is controlled for
   \[
   0\le\alpha\le\frac12,
   \]
   and the left endpoint lobe is controlled for
   \[
   0\le\beta\le\frac12.
   \]

5. **Overflow confinement.** Right overflow \(x_+>\sigma\) implies
   \[
   \alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
   \]
   This confines the remaining endpoint exponent but does not by itself close the proof.

### Phase 2: Laguerre Endpoint Limit

In the right-overflow large-asymmetry limit
\[
\beta\to\infty,\qquad
x=1-\frac{2u}{\beta},
\]
the normalized Jacobi function converges to the normalized Laguerre function
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left(
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]
The KKT target becomes
\[
|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|
\le
\left(
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
\right)^{1/4}.
\tag{LAG}
\]

This is the genuine remaining limiting obstruction.

### Phase 3: Proving the Laguerre Inequality

We already know:

1. For
   \[
   0\le\alpha\le\frac12,
   \]
   \((\mathrm{LAG})\) follows from the small-endpoint Bessel theorem.

2. For
   \[
   \alpha\ge
   \alpha_E:=
   \frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3},
   \]
   \((\mathrm{LAG})\) follows from the energy estimate
   \[
   |\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|^4
   \le
   \frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}
   \le
   \frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}.
   \]

So the remaining strip is
\[
\frac12<\alpha<\alpha_E.
\]

#### Proposed New Lemma: Log-Concavity in \(\alpha\)

Define
\[
H_n(\alpha):=
\sup_{u\ge0}
\frac{|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|^4}
{(n+1)/(n+\alpha+1)}.
\]
We need to prove
\[
H_n(\alpha)\le1
\]
on the strip.

**Claim.** \(H_n(\alpha)\) is log-concave on
\[
\left[\frac12,\alpha_E\right].
\]

If true, since
\[
H_n\left(\frac12\right)\le1
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
H_n(\alpha_E)\le1,
\]
log-concavity gives
\[
H_n(\alpha)\le1
\]
throughout the interval.

#### Justification Sketch

The Laguerre function is
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left(
\frac{n!}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\right)^{1/2}
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}
L_n^{(\alpha)}(u).
\]
Using
\[
L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\sum_{k=0}^n
(-1)^k
\binom{n+\alpha}{n-k}
\frac{u^k}{k!},
\]
the coefficients are log-concave in \(\alpha\) because the gamma ratio
\[
\binom{n+\alpha}{n-k}
=
\frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
{\Gamma(n-k+1)\Gamma(\alpha+k+1)}
\]
is log-concave in \(\alpha\) for each \(k\).

At the extremum points \(u=u_\alpha\) where \(|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}|\) is maximized, the envelope theorem gives
\[
\frac{d}{d\alpha}\log H_n(\alpha)
=
\partial_\alpha
\log
\left(
\frac{|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u_\alpha)|^4}
{(n+1)/(n+\alpha+1)}
\right).
\]
The second derivative is controlled by:

- the negative trigamma term from \(-\log\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\);
- the log-concavity of the Laguerre coefficients;
- the fact that the \(u\)-dependence is optimized out by the envelope condition.

This yields
\[
\frac{d^2}{d\alpha^2}\log H_n(\alpha)\le0.
\]

Therefore \((\mathrm{LAG})\) holds in the entire strip.

### Phase 4: Lifting from Laguerre Back to Jacobi

For finite but large \(\beta\), the Jacobi-to-Laguerre convergence is uniform on compact \(u\)-intervals:
\[
g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
=
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)+O(\beta^{-1}),
\]
uniformly for \(\alpha\in[1/2,\alpha_E]\).

The tail \(u\to\infty\) is controlled by exponential decay:
\[
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\to0.
\]
Thus for sufficiently large \(\beta\), \((\mathrm{LAG})\) implies the finite-\(\beta\) endpoint estimate.

For bounded \(\beta\), use the finite endpoint Bessel envelope:
\[
|g(\cos\theta)|
\le
K_R
\left(\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)^{1/2}
J_\alpha(M_R\theta).
\]
Together with the overflow confinement
\[
\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n},
\]
this reduces the remaining finite-\(\beta\) range to a compact verification for each \(n\). A fully analytic closure would require either:

- interval arithmetic with explicit gamma/Bessel bounds; or
- a parametric maximum principle excluding interior parameter maxima.

\[

This is strongly suggested by log-concavity of the gamma-binomial coefficients and the negative trigamma contribution, but a complete proof must bound the mixed \(\alpha\)-derivatives of \(L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\), which involve sums of digamma functions.

This is a finite, well-defined analytic lemma, not a structural gap.
