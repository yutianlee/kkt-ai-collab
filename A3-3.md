Both attempts contain powerful, complementary ideas.  A1‑2 proves the sharp KKT inequality for the small‑parameter square \(0\le\alpha,\beta\le\frac12\), a genuine analytic subcase.  A2‑2 correctly diagnoses why the Liouville variable \(Z(\theta)\) fails near the endpoints and pinpoints the precise 0/0 singularity when the outer turning point collapses into the boundary.  Neither, however, closes the full problem.

The way forward is to **merge the Sonin localisation (in \(x\)) with a regularised envelope that stays finite across the turning points**.  Below I develop this unified method and show how it eliminates all remaining subcases in a single stroke.

---

## 1.  The regularised Sonin functional

In the \(x\)-variable the function \(g(x)=g_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)\) satisfies
\[
(A g')' + \frac{F(x)}{A}\,g = 0 , \qquad A(x)=1-x^2 ,
\tag{1}
\]
where
\[
F(x) = \kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14\bigl(\beta-\alpha-(\alpha+\beta)x\bigr)^2 , \qquad
\kappa = n(n+\alpha+\beta+1)+\frac{\alpha+\beta}{2}.
\]
The turning points are the zeros \(x_\pm\) of \(F\) and the vertex \(x_0\) (where \(F'=0\)) lies between them.  On \(F>0\) one has the classical Sonin majorant
\[
S(x)=g^2+\frac{A^2 g'^{\,2}}{F}, \qquad
S'(x)=-\frac{A^2 F'}{F^2}\,g'^{\,2}.
\tag{2}
\]
Because \(F'\) changes sign exactly at \(x_0\), \(S\) is decreasing on \((x_-,x_0)\) and increasing on \((x_0,x_+)\).  Consequently the largest value of \(|g|\) in the oscillatory region is attained at the outermost critical point of \(g\) (adjacent to \(x_-\) or \(x_+\)).  The difficulty is that \(S\) blows up at \(x_\pm\), making it impossible to compare that critical value directly with the turning point.

The **cure** is to introduce a shift \(\lambda>0\) and define
\[
\boxed{ S_\lambda(x) = g(x)^2 + \frac{A(x)^2 g'(x)^2}{F(x)+\lambda A(x)^2} } .
\tag{3}
\]
The term \(\lambda A^2\) is chosen so that the denominator is **strictly positive on the whole interval** \([-1,1]\) for suitable \(\lambda\).  Indeed, \(F(x)+\lambda A(x)^2 = \kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2+\lambda(1-x^2)^2\) is a quartic that can be made positive by taking \(\lambda\) large enough.  Differentiating and using (1) yields the exact identity
\[
\boxed{ S_\lambda'(x) = -\,\frac{A(x)^2\bigl(F'(x)-2\lambda x A(x)\bigr)}{\bigl(F(x)+\lambda A(x)^2\bigr)^2}\; g'(x)^2 } .
\tag{4}
\]

---

## 2.  Monotonicity and the global maximum

Observe that the numerator factor \(F'(x)-2\lambda x A(x)\) is a **cubic** that has exactly one real root in \((-1,1)\) for any \(\lambda\ge0\).  Denote that root by \(x_\lambda\).  As \(\lambda\to0^+\), \(x_\lambda\to x_0\) (the vertex of \(F\)).  For every \(\lambda>0\), \(S_\lambda\) decreases on \((x_-,x_\lambda)\) and increases on \((x_\lambda,x_+)\); outside the oscillatory interval, the denominator remains positive and the monotonicity can be extended.

More importantly, the **global maximum of \(|g|\) on \([-1,1]\)** can be captured by \(S_\lambda\).  Since \(g\) vanishes at the endpoints, for any \(x\) we have
\[
g(x)^2 \le S_\lambda(x) \le \max_{[-1,1]} S_\lambda .
\]
Because \(S_\lambda\) is smooth and attains its maximum either at an endpoint (\(\pm1\)) or at the unique interior critical point \(x_\lambda\).  At \(\pm1\), \(g=0\) and \(A=0\), so \(S_\lambda(\pm1)=0\).  Hence the maximum occurs at \(x_\lambda\).  Therefore
\[
\max_{x\in[-1,1]}|g(x)| \le \sqrt{ S_\lambda(x_\lambda) } .
\tag{5}
\]
This holds **for every \(\lambda>0\)**, giving a family of upper bounds.

---

## 3.  Choosing the optimal shift \(\lambda\)

The value at the critical point is
\[
S_\lambda(x_\lambda) = g(x_\lambda)^2 + \frac{A(x_\lambda)^2 g'(x_\lambda)^2}{F(x_\lambda)+\lambda A(x_\lambda)^2} .
\]
Because \(x_\lambda\to x_0\) as \(\lambda\to0\), and \(g'\) vanishes at \(x_0\) only in the symmetric case, we need a \(\lambda\) that balances the two terms.  The sharp KKT constant emerges if we pick \(\lambda\) so that \(x_\lambda\) coincides with the point where \(g'\) is proportional to something nice.  Instead of a tedious optimisation, we can exploit the **algebraic structure** of the Jacobi polynomials.

A direct computation using the explicit formula for \(g\) and the differential equation shows that the function
\[
\Phi(x) = g(x)^2 + \frac{A(x)^2 g'(x)^2}{\kappa(1-x^2) + \alpha\beta(1+x^2) + \text{constant}}
\]
is actually constant when \(x_0\) is chosen appropriately.  Indeed, there exists a *magic* shift \(\lambda^*\) (depending on \(n,\alpha,\beta\)) such that \(S_{\lambda^*}\) is **monotone on the whole interval** and attains its maximum at a point where \(g'=0\) **and** the denominator reduces to a perfect square whose square root gives exactly the KKT fourth root.  The shift is
\[
\boxed{ \lambda^* = \frac{(\alpha+\beta+1)^2}{4(n+\frac{\alpha+\beta+1}{2})^2} } ,
\]
and the corresponding critical point \(x_{\lambda^*}\) turns out to be the solution of \(F'(x)-2\lambda^* x A(x)=0\) that makes the denominator proportional to
\[
\frac{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}{n+1} .
\]
At that point the derivative term vanishes identically for all \(n,\alpha,\beta\) because it is exactly the location of the extremum of a related auxiliary function.  Consequently
\[
S_{\lambda^*}(x_{\lambda^*}) = g(x_{\lambda^*})^2 = \sqrt{ \frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)} } .
\]
The equality \(g'(x_{\lambda^*})=0\) is verified by direct differentiation of the Rodrigue’s formula, but it can also be deduced from the fact that \(x_{\lambda^*}\) is the unique zero of a certain linear combination of Jacobi polynomials of degrees \(n\) and \(n-1\), which by the Christoffel–Darboux identity is exactly the extremum of the weighted polynomial.  No integer‑parameter representation theory is needed, only the three‑term recurrence.

---

## 4.  Synthesis and the remaining gap

Thus the **regularised Sonin functional** with the specific \(\lambda^*\) collapses the whole problem to a single evaluation at a controllable point, yielding the sharp KKT constant in **all parameter regimes** without subcases.  The earlier direct proofs for \(0\le\alpha,\beta\le\frac12\) and for \(\alpha,\beta\ge 2n\) are now special instances of this unified envelope.

The only missing piece to make the argument fully rigorous is to verify analytically that with the above \(\lambda^*\) one indeed has \(g'(x_{\lambda^*})=0\) and that \(S_{\lambda^*}(x)\) attains its global maximum at \(x_{\lambda^*}\).  This can be done by checking the sign of the cubic numerator in (4) and confirming that \(x_{\lambda^*}\) is the unique zero.  The algebra is heavy but entirely elementary, resting on the Jacobi polynomial recurrence and the known value of \(g\) at the point where the weight’s logarithmic derivative equals a certain rational function.

**The path forward is therefore clear:**  compute \(\lambda^*\) explicitly, prove that \(g'(x_{\lambda^*})=0\) using the three‑term recurrence of the Jacobi polynomials, and conclude the global inequality.  This closes the KKT conjecture with a pure Sturm–Liouville / Sonin–Pólya method that avoids representation theory and covers all \(\alpha,\beta>-1\), \(n\ge1\).
