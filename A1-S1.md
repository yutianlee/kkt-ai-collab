# Asymptotic Analysis

## Target Quantity

Let
\[
G_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)
=
\left\{
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)\Gamma(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)\Gamma(n+\beta+1)}
\right\}^{1/2}
\left(\frac{1-x}{2}\right)^{\alpha/2}
\left(\frac{1+x}{2}\right)^{\beta/2}
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x),
\]
where
\[
n\in{\mathbb N}_0,
\qquad
\alpha,\beta\ge0,
\qquad
-1\le x\le1.
\]
The KKT conjectural estimate is
\[
\boxed{
\left|G_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}(x)\right|
\le
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
:=
\left\{
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}
\right\}^{1/4}.
}
\tag{KKT}
\]

Koornwinder, Kostenko, and Teschl prove the corresponding estimate for integer parameters and formulate the real-parameter extension as a conjecture. In their paper this appears as Lemma 4.3 for \(\alpha,\beta\in{\mathbb N}_0\), and Conjecture 6.1 asks for the extension to all \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\).

## Evaluation of the Four Reports

The report A1-12 is the reliable one. Its main contribution is not a complete proof, but a correct localization of the remaining obstruction. It separates the already controlled regions from the limiting Laguerre endpoint problem and identifies both the failure of the earlier constant-frequency Bessel comparison and the incorrect algebra in the proposed overflow analysis.

The report A2-12 is not a proof. Its claim that the remaining Hilb estimate has enough uniform slack is not justified by an effective remainder estimate. Moreover, the right-overflow polynomial is not quadratic in \(\beta\); the corrected expression is linear:
\[
\Phi_R
=
\beta(-\alpha^2+2n\alpha+3n^2+2n)
+
(-\alpha^3+3n^2\alpha+2n\alpha+2n^3+2n^2).
\tag{1}
\]
This matters because the endpoint obstruction as \(\beta\to\infty\) is real and cannot be removed by a compactness argument uniform in \(n\).

The report A3-12 is mostly consistent with A1-12, but it overstates the reduction. The limiting Laguerre inequality is necessary and very likely decisive, but a complete Jacobi proof also requires a uniform finite-\(\beta\) bridge, or else a direct Jacobi endpoint asymptotic theorem which includes the Laguerre limit as \(\beta=\infty\).

The report A4-12 is useful as a roadmap. Its most important point is that one needs a variable-frequency Liouville-Green or Olver-type estimate rather than a constant-frequency comparison. However, the Laguerre differential equation must be normalized carefully. If
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\left\{
\frac{\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}
\right\}^{1/2}
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\qquad
\lambda=n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2},
\tag{2}
\]
then
\[
u\ell''+\ell'
+
\left(
\lambda-\frac{u}{4}-\frac{\alpha^2}{4u}
\right)\ell=0,
\tag{3}
\]
or equivalently
\[
\ell''+\frac1u\ell'
+
\left(
\frac{\lambda}{u}-\frac14-\frac{\alpha^2}{4u^2}
\right)\ell=0.
\tag{4}
\]
The first-derivative-free form is for
\[
Y(u)=u^{1/2}\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\]
and is
\[
Y''
+
\left(
\frac{\lambda}{u}
-\frac14
+\frac{1-\alpha^2}{4u^2}
\right)Y=0.
\tag{5}
\]
Thus any Liouville-Green argument must use either (3)-(4), or the correctly transformed equation (5).

## Existing Literature

The KKT paper itself is the source of the refined conjecture; its main motivation is the connection between Bernstein-type inequalities for Jacobi polynomials and dispersive estimates for generalized discrete Laguerre operators. See [arXiv 1602.08626][1].

Haagerup and Schlichtkrull prove a broad Bernstein-type inequality for Jacobi polynomials, uniform in the degree and in real parameters \(\alpha,\beta\ge0\). Their estimate is structurally relevant, but it is weaker than the sharp KKT conjectural constant. See [arXiv 1201.0495][2].

Krasikov proves strong upper bounds for Jacobi polynomials and obtains, in particular parameter ranges, estimates confirming and strengthening Erdelyi-Magnus-Nevai type behavior. Again, this is relevant, but it does not by itself provide the exact KKT constant in the problematic real-parameter endpoint range. See [arXiv math/0610111][3].

For the Laguerre limit, the most relevant existing work is Krasikov-Zarkh on equioscillatory behavior and sharp uniform bounds for Laguerre polynomials, together with the Frenzen-Wong-Temme line of uniform Laguerre asymptotics with explicit error representations. These are the correct sources for a rigorous endpoint certificate, but they do not appear to state exactly the uniform inequality needed here in the form
\[
\sup_{u\ge0}
\left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|
\le
\left(\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4}
\]
for all \(n\ge1\) and all \(\alpha\ge0\). See [ScienceDirect][4].

## Rigorous Reduction Obtained from the Four Reports

Put
\[
s=\alpha+\beta,
\qquad
d=\beta-\alpha,
\qquad
A(x)=1-x^2,
\]
and
\[
\kappa=n(n+s+1)+\frac{s}{2},
\qquad
F(x)=\kappa(1-x^2)-\frac14(d-sx)^2.
\tag{6}
\]
The normalized Jacobi function \(G=G_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}\) satisfies the self-adjoint equation
\[
(AG')'+\frac{F}{A}G=0.
\tag{7}
\]
On any interval where \(F>0\), the Sonin quantity
\[
S(x)=G(x)^2+\frac{A(x)^2G'(x)^2}{F(x)}
\tag{8}
\]
satisfies
\[
S'(x)
=
-\frac{A(x)^2F'(x)}{F(x)^2}G'(x)^2.
\tag{9}
\]
Thus \(S\) is monotone on the monotonicity intervals of \(F\). This is the correct structural starting point.

Define
\[
\sigma=\frac{s}{s+2n}.
\tag{10}
\]
The branch-safe central regime is
\[
[x_-,x_+]\subseteq[-\sigma,\sigma],
\tag{11}
\]
where \(x_\pm\) are the two roots of \(F(x)=0\). In this regime the Sonin comparison, together with the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull type estimate, proves the KKT bound.

The overflow analysis gives the following necessary confinement. If the right endpoint overflows,
\[
x_+>\sigma,
\]
then
\[
\alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
\tag{12}
\]
Similarly, if the left endpoint overflows,
\[
x_-<-\sigma,
\]
then
\[
\beta<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
\tag{13}
\]
Therefore the only noncompact obstruction is not simultaneous largeness of \(\alpha,\beta\), but a one-sided endpoint limit, for instance
\[
\beta\to\infty,
\qquad
n\ \text{fixed or large},
\qquad
0\le \alpha<n+\sqrt{4n^2+2n}.
\tag{14}
\]

The weighted energy identities are
\[
\int_{-1}^1 G(x)^2\,dx
=
\frac{2}{2n+s+1},
\tag{15}
\]
\[
\int_{-1}^1\frac{G(x)^2}{1-x}\,dx
=
\frac1\alpha,
\qquad
\int_{-1}^1\frac{G(x)^2}{1+x}\,dx
=
\frac1\beta,
\tag{16}
\]
for \(\alpha,\beta>0\), and
\[
\int_{-1}^1(1-x^2)G'(x)^2\,dx
=
n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)}.
\tag{17}
\]
Consequently
\[
\int_{-1}^1\frac{G(x)^2}{1-x^2}\,dx
=
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta},
\tag{18}
\]
and the Sobolev-Cauchy estimate gives
\[
\left|G(x)\right|^4
\le
\left(
\int_{-1}^1\frac{G(t)^2}{1-t^2}\,dt
\right)
\left(
\int_{-1}^1(1-t^2)G'(t)^2\,dt
\right).
\tag{19}
\]
Therefore the KKT estimate follows in every region where
\[
\frac{s}{2\alpha\beta}
\left(
n+\frac12-\frac{1}{2(2n+s+1)}
\right)
\le
\frac{(n+1)(n+s+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}.
\tag{20}
\]

Passing to the right endpoint limit
\[
x=1-\frac{2u}{\beta},
\qquad
\beta\to\infty,
\tag{21}
\]
one obtains
\[
G_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
\longrightarrow
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u),
\tag{22}
\]
where \(\ell_n^{(\alpha)}\) is given by (2). The KKT constant converges to
\[
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}^4
\longrightarrow
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}.
\tag{23}
\]
Hence a necessary limiting inequality is
\[
\boxed{
\left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|
\le
\left(\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4},
\qquad
u\ge0.
}
\tag{LAG}
\]

The Laguerre energy identities are
\[
\int_0^\infty \ell(u)^2\,du=1,
\tag{24}
\]
\[
\int_0^\infty \frac{\ell(u)^2}{u}\,du
=
\frac1\alpha,
\qquad \alpha>0,
\tag{25}
\]
and
\[
\int_0^\infty u\ell'(u)^2\,du
=
\frac{2n+1}{4}.
\tag{26}
\]
For each \(u>0\),
\[
\ell(u)^2
=
2\int_0^u \ell(t)\ell'(t)\,dt
=
-2\int_u^\infty \ell(t)\ell'(t)\,dt.
\tag{27}
\]
Thus, with
\[
A=\int_0^u\frac{\ell(t)^2}{t}\,dt,
\qquad
B=\int_0^u t\ell'(t)^2\,dt,
\]
\[
C=\int_u^\infty\frac{\ell(t)^2}{t}\,dt,
\qquad
D=\int_u^\infty t\ell'(t)^2\,dt,
\]
one has
\[
\ell(u)^4\le4AB,
\qquad
\ell(u)^4\le4CD.
\tag{28}
\]
Since
\[
\min\{AB,CD\}
\le
\frac14(A+C)(B+D),
\tag{29}
\]
we get the sharp two-sided energy consequence
\[
\boxed{
\left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|^4
\le
\frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}.
}
\tag{30}
\]
This proves \((\mathrm{LAG})\) whenever
\[
\frac{2n+1}{4\alpha}
\le
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}.
\tag{31}
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\alpha\ge
\alpha_E(n)
:=
\frac{(2n+1)(n+1)}{2n+3}
=
n+\frac{1}{2n+3}.
\tag{32}
\]
The small-endpoint argument controls \(0\le\alpha\le1/2\). Therefore the exact limiting Laguerre gap is
\[
\boxed{
\frac12<\alpha<
\alpha_E(n)
=
n+\frac1{2n+3}.
}
\tag{33}
\]

## The Decisive Laguerre Certificate

The cleanest remaining statement is the following stronger inequality:
\[
\boxed{
\sup_{u\ge0}
\left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|^4
\le
\frac12,
\qquad
n\ge1,\quad \alpha\ge\frac12.
}
\tag{U}
\]
Indeed, if \(1/2<\alpha<\alpha_E(n)\), then
\[
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1}
>
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha_E(n)+1}
=
\frac{2n+3}{4(n+1)}
>
\frac12.
\tag{34}
\]
Thus \((\mathrm{U})\) implies \((\mathrm{LAG})\) throughout the entire missing strip (33).

This is the real remaining analytic core. It is also the point at which A2-12 fails: one cannot replace \((\mathrm{U})\) by a non-effective Hilb expansion, because the conjecture requires a global supremum in \(u\), uniformly for
\[
n\to\infty,
\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le n+\frac1{2n+3},
\qquad
u\in[0,\infty).
\tag{35}
\]
This range contains several different asymptotic regimes:
\[
\alpha=O(1),
\qquad
\alpha=o(n),
\qquad
\alpha\asymp n,
\tag{36}
\]
and the maximum may lie in a hard-edge Bessel zone, a bulk oscillatory zone, or near a transition point.

## Feasible Proof Strategy for the Laguerre Certificate

Let
\[
\lambda=n+\frac{\alpha+1}{2}.
\tag{37}
\]
The equation (5) has potential
\[
Q_{\lambda,\alpha}(u)
=
\frac{\lambda}{u}
-\frac14
+
\frac{1-\alpha^2}{4u^2}.
\tag{38}
\]
The turning points are the positive roots, when they exist, of
\[
Q_{\lambda,\alpha}(u)=0.
\tag{39}
\]
Equivalently,
\[
u^2-4\lambda u+\alpha^2-1=0.
\tag{40}
\]
Thus the soft turning point is approximately
\[
u_+
=
2\lambda+\sqrt{4\lambda^2-\alpha^2+1}
=
4\lambda+O\left(\frac{\alpha^2}{\lambda}\right),
\tag{41}
\]
and, when \(\alpha>1\), the inner turning point is approximately
\[
u_-
=
2\lambda-\sqrt{4\lambda^2-\alpha^2+1}
=
\frac{\alpha^2-1}{4\lambda}
+O\left(\frac{\alpha^4}{\lambda^3}\right).
\tag{42}
\]
For \(1/2\le\alpha\le1\), there is no genuine positive inner turning point, but the hard edge \(u=0\) remains a Bessel singular endpoint.

A rigorous proof of \((\mathrm{U})\) should be organized into three zones.

First, in the hard-edge zone, set
\[
t=2\sqrt{\lambda u}.
\tag{43}
\]
One proves an explicit Bessel approximation
\[
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}
\left(\frac{t^2}{4\lambda}\right)
=
K_{n,\alpha}
\left\{
J_\alpha(t)+R_H(n,\alpha,t)
\right\},
\tag{44}
\]
valid uniformly for
\[
\frac12\le\alpha\le \alpha_E(n),
\qquad
0\le t\le t_H(n,\alpha),
\tag{45}
\]
where \(K_{n,\alpha}\) is the exact gamma-normalizing factor and the error is bounded by a computable Olver variation integral:
\[
|R_H(n,\alpha,t)|
\le
\exp\{V_H(n,\alpha,t)\}-1.
\tag{46}
\]
The point is not merely formal asymptotics; one needs an explicit inequality of the form
\[
K_{n,\alpha}
\left(
\sup_{t\ge0}|J_\alpha(t)|
+
E_H(n,\alpha)
\right)
<
2^{-1/4}.
\tag{47}
\]
For fixed \(\alpha\ge1/2\), the Bessel maximum is far below \(2^{-1/4}\). For large \(\alpha\), the classical Bessel maximum decays like \(O(\alpha^{-1/3})\). Hence (47) has large numerical slack once the remainder is genuinely effective.

Second, in the bulk oscillatory zone
\[
u_-+\delta_H\le u\le u_+-\delta_A,
\tag{48}
\]
one should use a Sonin or Krasikov-type envelope for the Laguerre equation. The relevant quantity is not simply \(\ell^2+Q^{-1}\ell'^2\), because the equation has a singular endpoint; after the transformation \(Y=u^{1/2}\ell\), the standard Sonin quantity is
\[
{\mathcal S}(u)
=
Y(u)^2+\frac{Y'(u)^2}{Q_{\lambda,\alpha}(u)}.
\tag{49}
\]
Its derivative is
\[
{\mathcal S}'(u)
=
-\frac{Q'_{\lambda,\alpha}(u)}{Q_{\lambda,\alpha}(u)^2}Y'(u)^2.
\tag{50}
\]
This gives monotonicity between turning points. Combined with a hard-edge boundary value at \(u_-+\delta_H\) and an Airy boundary value at \(u_+-\delta_A\), it gives a certified bulk bound. Krasikov-Zarkh type Laguerre estimates should also be usable here, especially for the large-\(n\), large-\(\alpha\) portion of the range.

Third, in the soft-edge zone, introduce the Liouville variable
\[
\frac23\zeta(u)^{3/2}
=
\int_u^{u_+}
\sqrt{Q_{\lambda,\alpha}(t)}\,dt
\tag{51}
\]
inside the oscillatory region, with the usual analytic continuation outside it. Olver theory gives
\[
Y(u)
=
A_{\lambda,\alpha}(u)
\left[
\operatorname{Ai}\bigl(\lambda^{2/3}\zeta(u)\bigr)
+
R_A(n,\alpha,u)
\right],
\tag{52}
\]
where the remainder satisfies a computable bound
\[
|R_A(n,\alpha,u)|
\le
\exp\{V_A(n,\alpha,u)\}-1.
\tag{53}
\]
After dividing by \(u^{1/2}\), this gives a uniform soft-edge estimate for \(\ell\). Since in the missing strip
\[
\alpha\le n+\frac1{2n+3},
\tag{54}
\]
the soft edge lies at scale \(u_+\asymp n+\alpha\), and the Airy amplitude is too small to reach \(2^{-1/4}\) for large \(n\). Thus only finitely many low degrees should remain after the effective Airy estimate is installed.

The output of this analysis should be an explicit theorem of the following form:

There exists a computable integer \(N_0\) such that, for every \(n\ge N_0\), every
\[
\frac12\le\alpha\le n+\frac1{2n+3},
\]
and every \(u\ge0\),
\[
\left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|^4\le\frac12.
\tag{55}
\]
Moreover \(N_0\) is obtained from explicit variation-integral bounds in the hard-edge Bessel, bulk Sonin, and soft-edge Airy regions.

The remaining finite set
\[
1\le n<N_0,
\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le\alpha_E(n),
\qquad
u\ge0
\tag{56}
\]
can then be certified by interval arithmetic.

## Finite Verification for the Laguerre Certificate

For fixed \(n\), critical points of \(\ell_n^{(\alpha)}\) are the zeros of
\[
C_{n,\alpha}(u)
=
(\alpha-u)L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
-2uL_{n-1}^{(\alpha+1)}(u).
\tag{57}
\]
Indeed,
\[
\frac{d}{du}
\left(
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
\right)
=
u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}
\left[
\left(\frac{\alpha}{2u}-\frac12\right)L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
-
L_{n-1}^{(\alpha+1)}(u)
\right].
\tag{58}
\]
Multiplication by \(2u\) gives (57).

Thus a rigorous finite verification proceeds as follows.

1. For each \(1\le n<N_0\), partition
   \[
   \left[\frac12,\alpha_E(n)\right]
   \]
   into rational intervals \(I_j\).

2. For each interval \(I_j\), construct an interval polynomial enclosure for
   \[
   C_{n,\alpha}(u),
   \qquad
   \alpha\in I_j.
   \]

3. Use a Sturm or subresultant method with interval coefficients to isolate all real critical points in a finite interval
   \[
   0\le u\le U_{n,j}.
   \]

4. Choose \(U_{n,j}\) so that the tail estimate
   \[
   \left|
   u^{\alpha/2}e^{-u/2}L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
   \right|
   <
   2^{-1/4}
   \left\{
   \frac{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}
   \right\}^{1/2}
   \]
   holds for all \(u\ge U_{n,j}\) and all \(\alpha\in I_j\). This is elementary because \(L_n^{(\alpha)}\) is a degree-\(n\) polynomial while \(e^{-u/2}\) dominates in the tail.

5. On each critical-point interval and at \(u=0\), evaluate the interval enclosure of
   \[
   \left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|^4-\frac12.
   \]
   Refine until every interval is strictly negative.

This gives a fully rigorous finite certificate for \((\mathrm{U})\). No floating-point sampling is sufficient by itself; the verification must be interval-based.

## Finite-\(\beta\) Bridge

Even after \((\mathrm{U})\) is proved, one more endpoint issue remains for the original Jacobi conjecture. The limiting implication
\[
\mathrm{KKT}\Longrightarrow\mathrm{LAG}
\]
is automatic, but the converse is not automatic. One must either prove a finite-\(\beta\) endpoint lift, or prove the Jacobi endpoint estimate directly.

The correct endpoint scaling is
\[
x=1-\frac{2u}{\beta},
\qquad
0\le u<\infty,
\qquad
\beta\to\infty.
\tag{59}
\]
Using the hypergeometric representation,
\[
P_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
=
\frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}
\,{}_2F_1
\left(
-n,n+\alpha+\beta+1;\alpha+1;\frac{u}{\beta}
\right),
\tag{60}
\]
whereas
\[
L_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
=
\frac{(\alpha+1)_n}{n!}
\,{}_1F_1(-n;\alpha+1;u).
\tag{61}
\]
Termwise comparison gives an explicit finite-degree expansion in powers of \(1/\beta\). However, to be useful here, the error must be uniform in
\[
n\to\infty,
\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le n+\frac1{2n+3},
\qquad
u\ \text{in the endpoint oscillatory range}.
\tag{62}
\]
A naive termwise bound is too crude when \(u=O(n)\). Therefore the preferred route is a Jacobi Liouville-Green theorem in the compactified endpoint parameter
\[
\eta=\frac1{1+\beta}\in[0,1],
\tag{63}
\]
where \(\eta=0\) is exactly the Laguerre limit.

The needed theorem is the following. There exist computable constants \(B_0,N_0\) such that, whenever
\[
n\ge N_0,
\qquad
\beta\ge B_0(n,\alpha),
\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le n+\frac1{2n+3},
\]
the Jacobi endpoint function satisfies
\[
\left|
G_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
-
\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)
\right|
\le
E_{J\to L}(n,\alpha,\beta,u),
\]
with
\[
\sup_u E_{J\to L}(n,\alpha,\beta,u)
<
T_{n,\alpha,\beta}
-
\sup_u|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)|.
\tag{64}
\]
In practice, one should not use the last inequality in this sharp form. One should instead prove directly that
\[
\left|
G_n^{(\alpha,\beta)}
\left(1-\frac{2u}{\beta}\right)
\right|^4
\le
\frac12+\varepsilon(n,\alpha,\beta)
\tag{65}
\]
with
\[
\frac12+\varepsilon(n,\alpha,\beta)
<
\frac{(n+1)(n+\alpha+\beta+1)}
{(n+\alpha+1)(n+\beta+1)}.
\tag{66}
\]
For large \(\beta\), the right-hand side tends to
\[
\frac{n+1}{n+\alpha+1},
\tag{67}
\]
which is strictly larger than \(1/2\) throughout the open strip (33). Near the upper boundary \(\alpha=\alpha_E(n)\), the energy estimate (30) already takes over, so one does not need uniform positive margin all the way to the boundary.

## Conditional Completion of the KKT Proof

The preceding analysis gives the following conditional theorem.

Assume the following two explicit certificates.

First, the Laguerre certificate:
\[
\sup_{u\ge0}
\left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|^4
\le
\frac12,
\qquad
n\ge1,\quad \alpha\ge\frac12.
\]

Second, a finite-\(\beta\) endpoint lift, uniform in
\[
n\ge1,
\qquad
\frac12\le\alpha\le\alpha_E(n),
\qquad
\beta\ge0,
\]
with computable remainder and with the same estimate after interchanging \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\).

Then the KKT conjecture holds for all
\[
n\in{\mathbb N}_0,
\qquad
\alpha,\beta\ge0,
\qquad
-1\le x\le1.
\tag{68}
\]

The proof is as follows. The central branch-safe regime is handled by the Sonin monotonicity argument (8)-(11) and the Haagerup-Schlichtkrull comparison. The weighted energy estimate handles all parameters satisfying (20). The small endpoint exponents \(0\le\alpha\le1/2\) and \(0\le\beta\le1/2\) are handled by the endpoint Bessel comparison. By the overflow confinement (12)-(13), the only unbounded remaining regions are the one-sided endpoint limits. By symmetry it is enough to treat the right endpoint with \(\beta\to\infty\). In that region the finite-\(\beta\) endpoint lift reduces the estimate to the Laguerre certificate. The Laguerre certificate proves the limiting strip (33), while the energy estimate proves \(\alpha\ge\alpha_E(n)\). This exhausts all regimes.

## Conclusion

The four reports do not yet contain a complete proof of the KKT conjecture. The correct present state is:
\[
\boxed{
\text{A1-12 gives the correct reduction; A2-12 is invalid; A3-12 and A4-12 provide useful but incomplete strategies.}
}
\]

The remaining analytic gap is sharply identifiable. It is the endpoint Laguerre certificate
\[
\sup_{u\ge0}
\left|\ell_n^{(\alpha)}(u)\right|^4
\le
\frac12,
\qquad
n\ge1,\quad \alpha\ge\frac12,
\]
together with a uniform finite-\(\beta\) Jacobi-to-Laguerre lift. The most feasible route is not a formal Hilb expansion, but a certified hybrid proof:

1. Hard edge: Bessel-type Olver expansion with computable remainder.
2. Bulk: Sonin/Krasikov envelope for the transformed Laguerre equation.
3. Soft edge: Airy-type Olver expansion with computable remainder.
4. Low degrees and transition leftovers: interval arithmetic using the critical polynomial (57).
5. Finite Jacobi endpoint: compactified Liouville-Green expansion in \(\eta=(1+\beta)^{-1}\), or a direct interval-certified Jacobi endpoint computation.

This strategy is finite, explicit, and certificate-level: once the variation-integral constants are written down, the remaining checks reduce to finitely many interval inequalities. At present, the literature supplies the right asymptotic machinery and related sharp Laguerre/Jacobi bounds, but I do not find an existing published theorem that directly closes the KKT real-parameter conjecture with the exact constant and the required global uniformity.
